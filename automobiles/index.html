Idle schmidle

One year there was a parent at our preschool who used to pull her SUV right up to the front door and keep it running while she dropped off her child, sometimes spending ten minutes inside getting him settled and talking to the teacher. It drove me absolutely insane. When our preschool decided to pursue the Eco-Healthy Childcare qualification this year, the 'no idling' rule was on the list of qualifiers, and we've so far been successful educating our current families about the hazards of idling. The Portland Department of Transportation has put together a great list of reasons to turn off your car:

All of us have idled our vehicles while waiting to pick up kids from school, waiting at the drive-up window, or waiting for a bridge. Part of the reason is that when we were just beginning to drive, the mindset was that it’s better to leave the car running rather than turn it on and off.

Well, maybe that was true then, but it’s certainly not true now. As a matter of fact, it’s actually better for your vehicle to turn it off if it’s going to be idling for more than 10 seconds.

This fact leads to the top ten reasons to turn off your vehicle when you’re not moving:

  1. Studies have shown a direct link between contaminants in vehicle emissions and significant respiratory health effects.
  2. Vehicle exhaust is the leading source of toxic air pollution in Oregon.
  3. Excessive idling can be hard on your engine. Because it isn’t working at peak operating temperature, fuel doesn’t undergo complete combustion, leaving spark plugs dirty, which can increase fuel consumption by 4 to 5%. Also, the engine oil becomes contaminated by idling.
  4. According to Ford and the Auto Alliance, vehicles produced after the mid-‘80s don’t require additional time to heat a “cold engine.” The best way to warm up your vehicle is to drive it, even in cold weather.
  5. Besides, idling warms only the engine, not the wheel bearings, steering, suspension, transmission, and tires. These parts warm up when the vehicle is driven.
  6. Frequent restarting has little impact on engine parts such as the battery and starter motor. The wear on components that restarting the engine causes adds about $10 a year to your costs. This is money that you’ll likely recover several times over in fuel savings from reduced idling.
  7. More than 10 seconds of idling uses more fuel than restarting the engine.
  8. Toxic air pollutants account for an additional 700 cases of cancer for every million residents.
  9. Children breathe 50% more air per pound than adults, and their asthma symptoms increase as a result of car exhaust.
  10. When you idle you get 0 miles per gallon.

World Car Free Day on September 22

Thank you, Betty, for letting us know about World Car Free Day on September 22 (today! sorry for the late notice). Here's some info from their site:

Every September 22, people from around the world get together in the streets, intersections, and neighbourhood blocks to remind the world that we don't have to accept our car-dominated society.

2007 should be no different.

But we do not want just one day of celebration and then a return to "normal" life. When people get out of their cars, they should stay out of their cars. It is up to us, it is up to our cities, and our governments to help create permanent change to benefit pedestrians, cyclists, and other people who do not drive cars.

Let World Carfree Day be a showcase for just how our cities might look like, feel like, and sound like without cars…365 days a year.

I like the tie-in to not returning to "normal" car-addicted life. Just last week, the Oregonian ran an article about families who are living car-free. Proves it can be done here in Stumptown...

The perfect eco-friendly car?

I should be thinking bike thoughts, but instead I'm dwelling on the perfect eco-friendly car for our family. Not that we need a new car, but sometimes it's nice to escape to that place in my brain where anything is possible. I visit there frequently. My neighbors, who have three kids, just bought a brand new Lexus SUV hybrid (26 hwy/24 city) as a family car and a Mini Cooper S convertible (32 hwy/22 city) as a commuter car in an effort to get better gas mileage and cut down on emissions. Those stats are okay (and about what my family's cars are getting), but not great. So I've been trying to figure out what would be the ideal scenario (beyond living in a village where everything you need is within easy walking distance).

For us, we need a family car and a commuter car. I like the Smart Car, which gets 40-plus mpgs, and think it would be an ideal commuter car. Even better than that though, is the Zap Xebra sedan (perhaps without the stripes), a totally electric car. It only goes 40 mph, so it's strictly for around town, but it seats four and only costs $10,500. I think these cars are adorable, but not sure my husband would drive one.

A larger family car is a little trickier. Have they come out with a hybrid station wagon or mini van yet? I have to confess -- and I never dreamed I would ever admit this -- but I recently drove a mini van, and I loved all the room. Plus, you could carpool (environmentally-friendly, yes?). The Prius might work, but it's still a little small. The Lexus is waaay out of our price range and I don't really want an SUV. I currently drive a VW Passat wagon, and it tells me that I usually get about 25 mpgs in the city, which I guess is okay, but when you compare it to a 46 mpg Prius, it's still kind of sucky.

Have I come to any conclusions? Not really (except maybe I should spend more time on a bike). What is your dream eco-friendly car set-up for your family? Or, are you living it already?

Weekend getaway EnviroMom style

My mom has been visiting, and very generously offered to watch the kids for a weekend so my husband and I (the self-appointed Cheap-o-Dad) could celebrate our 11th wedding anniversary. So last weekend was our big trip away. Despite seeking advice from ReadySetMom readers on good weekend getaway locales, we ended up not going very far at all. About 12 miles down the road. We drove from Portland to Newberg, OR, which is the starting place of the annual Vineride. Pre-kids, we did a lot of biking. In fabulous far-away places like Tuscany, the south of France, the Netherlands. In the six years since kids, we've probably logged about 12 miles total on our bikes (not counting Cheap-o-Dad's recent bike-to-work commutes). The Vineride organizers very generously leave their maps up on their site. We choose the 35-mile option. Of course, we weren't riding on "ride day," so it was unsupported and we printed our own Google maps. But it was just gorgeous. We saw lush orchards, vineyards, tree farms, farmland, chickens, and one overly zealous guard dog (who warned us that we really shouldn't be biking on his gravel road in city-slick tires). The sun was shining. The humidity was low. We biked past an old-fashioned swimming hole -- something you never would have seen from a car. We lunched at the fabulous French-inspired Cuvee in Carlton (on Bastille Day, no less), and met a very nice couple from Boise who seemed to enjoy bike travel as much as we do.

Ultimately, it ended up being 42+ miles (factoring in one wrong turn and one detour). Killer noodle legs at the end of the day, and I nearly told off a cow (yes, as in bovine) to mind her own beeswax as I huffed up the last painful hill into Newberg on granny gear. My point (if there is one) is that like Heather wrote since reading Last Child in the Woods, there really is a world of wonder just in our own backyard. And if you venture a little further, like, say 12 miles, it's kind of like being in Tuscany (but with lots more pickup trucks and far too many big gulp cups discarded by the side of the road). We never actually made it to any wineries. They all seemed to be atop large hills. But it was lovely. To get a taste of non-city life in a way that I don't on a daily basis. Can't recommend it highly enough. And since we didn't drive very far, Cheap-o-Dad is still on track to not fill up his gas tank all summer!

Cheap-o-dad: one fill-up summer

There's no question that summer is the nicest time of year in the Pacific Northwest: blue skies, warm temps, low humidity, little rain. That's why Cheap-o-dad is trying an experiment right now. His goal is to avoid driving his car this summer so that he doesn't need to fill-up his gas tank until fall. The last fill-up was in May on the day of our oldest daughter's preschool graduation.

So far so good. Most days he rides his bike to and from work. He's 4.5 miles to his office in downtown Portland. We're in the hilly part of town, so the real workout comes on the way home. On days when he doesn't bike, he jogs to work in the morning, and carpools home with a co-worker (or sometimes the opposite). On rare days he does drive.

We are fortunate to live in a town where business casual reigns, there's lots of bike lanes, and physical fitness is valued. We're saving a lot of money on gas and parking, and he doesn't spend any time at the gym now that his workout is integrated into his life.

While I can't always avoid using my car with two kids in tow, I am enjoying the summer weather and finding days that we can walk/stroll to places instead of driving. At least in the summer we are not on a tight time schedule like the school year.

Do not believe everything you read

I hate to even give this any more exposure, but I’ve just learned a valuable lesson in the importance of research before reaction. The Sunday Oregonian ran an editorial (sorry, couldn't find it on OregonLive but it's the same) titled “Hybrids might look green, but look behind that battery,” written by James L. Martin. His editorial basically repeats the findings of a report by Oregon-based CNW Marketing Research, which claim that a Toyota Prius actually consumes more energy than a Hummer when you look at the amount of resources needed to manufacture a Prius. Converted to dollars per gallon, the report asserts that a Prius has an energy cost of $3.25 with a life expectancy of 100,000 miles while the Hummer costs $1.95 with a life span of 300,000 miles.

For about 24 hours after I read the piece I was really bummed. I’ve longed for a hybrid for awhile for its low emissions, so reading this was deflating. Then I did some online research to see what other people were saying about the study, and discovered that it is flawed in so many ways. For one, the study assumes that Hummers will be driven for 300,000 miles and a Prius for only 100,000. No way on both counts. That skews the results right there. Secondly, the study factored in research and development costs – a hybrid is a unique automobile and its R&D costs are going to be much higher than a vehicle that is just a souped-up version of an existing SUV. Maybe RIGHT NOW it is taking more energy to manufacture a hybrid, but there’s no denying that once it’s on the road it is consuming far, far less. And in 10 years, when we’re paying $8/gallon for gas and the earth’s temperature has risen even more and Toyota has mainstreamed its production and perhaps is no longer using nickel-based batteries, the hybrid will rule.

And just who is James L. Martin, the author of the op-ed piece? Why he is the President of an organization called 60 Plus Association, which states it is a “non-partisan seniors advocacy group with a free enterprise, less government, less taxes approach to seniors issues.” On the home page of its Web site, you can count FOUR photos of Mr. Martin shaking hands with his close, personal, long-time friend George W. Bush. Non-partisan, you say? Um, okay.

Cutting back on car trips

We've talked about a lot of ideas here for living a more eco-healthy lifestyle, but there's one we haven't touched on much -- driving less. This is probably one of the hardest changes to make, particularly for parents of young children, but it's one that can have a big impact on the environment. Recently Portland unveiled a campaign called Drive Less/Save More, which encourages each household to eliminate two trips per week. One of the ways they suggest doing this is by "trip chaining" -- making a list of places you need to go and hitting them back-to-back. Chances are, if you're like me, you do this anyway because you hate buckling and unbuckling kids into their car seats. But can you reduce your trips even more?

I like to think that by ordering my groceries online, that I've eliminated one or two trips to the grocery store each week (really, I'm so addicted to this convenience that I will do ANYTHING to avoid a trip to the grocery store). Plus, the bio-diesel fueled delivery van is hitting several households on the way to mine. Riding a bike with two kids hitched behind me, traveling the southwest hills, is simply not an option. But I could run local errands on the weekends by bike when my husband is home to watch the kids. I could get in the habit of calling Renee when I'm headed out to see if she needs anything on my route -- that would save another car trip for her. It's just another small lifestyle adjustment to make, like bringing your own bag to the store, that takes a little effort in the beginning, but becomes habit very quickly.

Do you have any tips for cutting out the car? Do you bike with your kids to the store? Take mass transit? Please share -- the more we hear the stories of others, the more we'll be inspired to change.
