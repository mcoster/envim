Updates on past posts, and other assorted tidbits

I'm long overdue to update on a few past posts, so will do so today:

  • I finally got around to conducting the "de-stink Pinky" test. I spritzed Pinky with a half/half mix of vinegar/water and PH12.6. PH12.6 worked instantly and left no residual smell. The vinegar water worked but left a residual smell for a while, but then dissipated. This is also one instance where procrastination worked in my favor as untreated areas seem to have lost the campfire smell just from airing out over time.
  • I made the soft butter using a hand-mixer instead of a stand-mixer with no problems and thumbs up from the family. I am not a kitchen whiz, but this was very easy, and no one seems to note any difference in taste from the store-bought in-a-throwaway-plastic-container version. Yay! There's one less plastic item I need to recycle!
  • This whole thing about reuse of "disposable" baby wipes is beyond cool. Sometimes when I write a post, I'm mostly just trying to be funny and then end up learning something amazing like this. I don't use a lot of baby wipes, but still have enough around, and am thrilled to be tossing them into the hamper for a wash now instead of in the trash. I even made sure during a family discussion last night that everyone understood I was throwing these in the hamper with a purpose, and for them not to think ol' mom has really lost it and is throwing garbage in the hamper.
  • Cheap-o-Dad continues to ride his bike to work. His new goal is to not gas-up again until Thanksgiving. I am trying not to freak about the horrific recent spate of bike v. truck fatalities in Portland. If there's any good in it, it's that some real discussion has been going on regarding making the roads safer for bike riders.
  • OPB will feature a special on The Silence of the Bees this Sunday on Nature from 8-9pm. Looks very interesting and worth viewing or taping. Did you know honeybees are responsible for every third bite we eat?

October 3 - Walk or Bike to School Day

Heather posted about International Walk/Bike to School Day, which is coming up next week on Wednesday, October 3. Here are three great reasons to walk or bike:

  • Build healthy activity into the daily routine of kids
  • Spare the environment and improve air quality by putting less cars on the road
  • Create safer routes to school for kids

According to TerraBlog and the EPA, commute traffic jumps 30% right after Labor Day. So, we the soccer moms (yes, I now count myself as belonging to this group) account for a large amount of daily emissions!

There are lots of valid reasons why your kids may not be able to walk or bike to school. What's key is we all start thinking creatively about it, and find ways we can carpool or take the bus, or walk/bike as groups. Small changes make a major difference.

Weekend getaway EnviroMom style

My mom has been visiting, and very generously offered to watch the kids for a weekend so my husband and I (the self-appointed Cheap-o-Dad) could celebrate our 11th wedding anniversary. So last weekend was our big trip away. Despite seeking advice from ReadySetMom readers on good weekend getaway locales, we ended up not going very far at all. About 12 miles down the road. We drove from Portland to Newberg, OR, which is the starting place of the annual Vineride. Pre-kids, we did a lot of biking. In fabulous far-away places like Tuscany, the south of France, the Netherlands. In the six years since kids, we've probably logged about 12 miles total on our bikes (not counting Cheap-o-Dad's recent bike-to-work commutes). The Vineride organizers very generously leave their maps up on their site. We choose the 35-mile option. Of course, we weren't riding on "ride day," so it was unsupported and we printed our own Google maps. But it was just gorgeous. We saw lush orchards, vineyards, tree farms, farmland, chickens, and one overly zealous guard dog (who warned us that we really shouldn't be biking on his gravel road in city-slick tires). The sun was shining. The humidity was low. We biked past an old-fashioned swimming hole -- something you never would have seen from a car. We lunched at the fabulous French-inspired Cuvee in Carlton (on Bastille Day, no less), and met a very nice couple from Boise who seemed to enjoy bike travel as much as we do.

Ultimately, it ended up being 42+ miles (factoring in one wrong turn and one detour). Killer noodle legs at the end of the day, and I nearly told off a cow (yes, as in bovine) to mind her own beeswax as I huffed up the last painful hill into Newberg on granny gear. My point (if there is one) is that like Heather wrote since reading Last Child in the Woods, there really is a world of wonder just in our own backyard. And if you venture a little further, like, say 12 miles, it's kind of like being in Tuscany (but with lots more pickup trucks and far too many big gulp cups discarded by the side of the road). We never actually made it to any wineries. They all seemed to be atop large hills. But it was lovely. To get a taste of non-city life in a way that I don't on a daily basis. Can't recommend it highly enough. And since we didn't drive very far, Cheap-o-Dad is still on track to not fill up his gas tank all summer!

Cheap-o-dad: one fill-up summer

There's no question that summer is the nicest time of year in the Pacific Northwest: blue skies, warm temps, low humidity, little rain. That's why Cheap-o-dad is trying an experiment right now. His goal is to avoid driving his car this summer so that he doesn't need to fill-up his gas tank until fall. The last fill-up was in May on the day of our oldest daughter's preschool graduation.

So far so good. Most days he rides his bike to and from work. He's 4.5 miles to his office in downtown Portland. We're in the hilly part of town, so the real workout comes on the way home. On days when he doesn't bike, he jogs to work in the morning, and carpools home with a co-worker (or sometimes the opposite). On rare days he does drive.

We are fortunate to live in a town where business casual reigns, there's lots of bike lanes, and physical fitness is valued. We're saving a lot of money on gas and parking, and he doesn't spend any time at the gym now that his workout is integrated into his life.

While I can't always avoid using my car with two kids in tow, I am enjoying the summer weather and finding days that we can walk/stroll to places instead of driving. At least in the summer we are not on a tight time schedule like the school year.

How do you avoid using the car?

Um, Clever Cycles may change my life

Yesterday JJ posted about Clever Cycles, a new bike shop in SE Portland. It's not just any ol' bike shop, however. It's the bike shop of the revolution. Actually, I haven't been there yet, but I have spent obscene amounts of time on their blog site today and can already feel my thighs burning. I had posted a few weeks back about the dilemma of one adult biking with two kids who are too big for a trailer, though not cycling on their own yet. I do believe I will find my solution at Clever Cycles. They sell bikes that are alternatives to the mini van. Bikes that make it easy to transport multiple children and groceries without having a heart attack. Electric bikes. Plus accessories for regular bikes, like that front carrier. Just look at these photos! I'm actually having serious thoughts about becoming a one-car family and getting one of these bikes (honey, we'll talk tonight). Maybe it's the weather. Or maybe it's the realization that the biggest environmental impact we can personally make is to drive less, or not drive at all. I know. Gasp. I may wake up in the morning and wonder if someone slipped something in my coffee. But right now it's pretty clear. It's time to get biking.

How does one adult bike with two kids?

I used to have a bike. In my twenties when I was too poor to afford a car, the bike was my main means of transportation. I also lived in SE Portland, where the terrain is a lot flatter. Now I'm in the SW hills, and up until recently the thought of riding a bike around here sounded horrible. Too many hills! (Though don't tell that to the devoted bike commuters who huff up Terwilliger every day.) So I gave my bike away. Now I'm thinking about biking again as a way to cut back on car usage, but there's another issue: the kids. During the day when my husband is at work, is it possible for one adult to bike with two kids? My 5.5 year old is still on training wheels and can't possibly handle going down (or up) hills on her own. The almost 3-year old is too small for a tag-along. Is it possible to do? Do I need to wait one more year until my daughter is a confident cyclist? She does great on the tag-along, but then what do I do with my son?

The Oregonian had a great story about a family with three little kids who'd traded in their van for a Bakfiets bike -- a bike with a big cart in front. All three of the kids ride in the cart. It works for them, though I wonder if they ever go up big hills. It also costs $2,500, which is not in my price range (but, it's a great price for a car-substitute!) So I'm looking for stories! Do you bike with your kids? How do you do it?
