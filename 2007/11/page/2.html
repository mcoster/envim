Free Geek founder starts up new e-waste recycling center

When I was taking the Master Recycler class we got to take a tour of Free Geek led by Founder Oso 'Bear' Martin. He had so much enthusiasm and passion for electronic waste recycling, it was infectious. Now, Bear has started another recycling center, Bear eCycling, located at 5432 SE 63rd Ave. (near Foster & Holgate). Bear eCycling takes most of the items that Free Geek takes, with two major additions: they will accept and disassemble television sets and microwave ovens. What's so important about this? Typically electronics are shipped overseas for disassembly and recycling in impoverished areas where workers are unprotected from the hazardous materials lurking inside electronics. Both Free Geek and Bear eCycling disassemble the electronics right here in Portland and try to find as many local recyclers for the parts before shipping them overseas responsibly.

Bear eCycling charges fees for recycling electronics that contain hazardous waste, like TVs, microwaves, computer monitors and CFLs. Other items like computers, telephones and printers don't carry a fee. They will also accept household alkaline batteries! Hooray!

Fun with pinecones, sidewalk chalk and letters

Having the kids at home this week has really put a cramp into my blogging schedule. I usually brainstorm my topics, write 'em and post 'em in the fleeting hour between when my 3-year-old takes her nap and my 5-year-old comes home from kindergarten on the school bus. But we did do something kind of fun while the little one was napping today. We wrote out some letters on the driveway, then outlined them using some pine cones we'd gathered on a nature walk earlier this month.

We thought about writing "gobble, gobble" or "happy turkey day" but it was a little too chilly and windy to spend much time outside today!! At the very least, it'll be a nice greeting for Cheap-o as he rides home on his newly tuned up used bike, recently purchased off Craigslist (he was able to justify the cost of the bike from money saved on gasoline). Sadly, he did have to drive his car to work during the severe rainy days of earlier this week, and filled his gas tank a few days shy of Thanskgiving. But he's been a real trooper, having only filled his gas tank twice in the past two seasons. He's ridden his bike on a lot of days I would have easily made excuses and opted for the car. So as we celebrate Thanksgiving this year, I'm thankful for:

  • a husband who bike rides for health and gas-savings
  • two lovely little girls who ask me so many questions, I think my brain is going to explode, but always make me laugh and smile
  • beloved family both near and far
  • good friends who come here, share ideas and challenges, and teach me a whole lot about being green...

Happy Thanksgiving!! What are you thankful for?

Outdoor Kids: Camping with kids off-season

Earlier in the month, EnviroMom Hope went camping with her husband and two young sons (ages 2 and 4) at Cape Disappointment. I thought, "Camping in November? With little kids? Are you crazy, woman?" But she's not. (Actually, I just met Hope at last week's Eastside GreenGroup meeting, and she's quite sane...and delightful.) Here's her report (and my comments):

  • First of all, it is great to go off-season because there are no crowds and the rates are cheaper. We have a small camper/trailer that we bought last year and it has been a wonderful investment. Our campsite backed up basically to a tree line that separated the beach and campground. You could actually see and hear the ocean from our campsite. (OK, so far so good. Really good.)
  • My husband took the kids to fly kites and build sandcastles while I got a much needed break. (I'd like my husband and kids to go fly a kite some days.)
  • We also visited Leadbetter Point and did some hiking.  There are are a variety of mushrooms there,  though not for picking. So that was fun to point out to the kids. (Mushrooms are cool. Wish I knew more about them.)
  • And we followed deer tracks through the sand. (Nice.)
  • Our next stop was the Lewis & Clark Interpretive Center. Very cool place. Lots of hands-on things for the kids to do. On the trail that leads up to the Interpretive Center, they have signs that tell what some of the trees are and it talks about the temperate forest. Our 4 yr old knew what a temperate forest was from the JumpStart Animal Adventures CD-ROM we just got from the library. He even sang us a song. While we were there, I kept seeing this animal surface the water and dive back down. I never saw the head, but I definitely saw a dorsal fin. Of course while we were there, my husband never saw it and teased me that maybe I was seeing the Loch Ness Monster. And of course you can see Cape Disappointment Lighthouse from there. (Hmm, her 4-year old is smarter than me. I feel inadequate.)

Baby step: explain yourself to a young'un

One of the really cool things about having kids is sometimes they are really interested in what you say, and more than anything, they're watching what you do. If you're like me, and you have kids who ask a lot of questions, I tend to go into "tour guide" mode -- explaining just about every blessed thing I do in anticipation of the millions of questions they will pepper me with. Kinda like a preschool teacher: "So first we rinse the milk jug because the good people who recycle the plastic need it to be clean. As far as the caps go, we can put those aside for the Master Recycler plastics roundup...."

The beauty of this is that they pay attention and parrot my actions and words with other kids, and will sometimes give me friendly reminders when I've forgotten my reuseable grocery bags, or didn't turn out the bathroom light, or left the water running just a hair too long while doing the dishes. I hear them do it with me, my husband, and my father. These little pups help us old dogs learn new tricks.

And then sometimes they come up with really cute things on their own, using the enviro-friendly lingo we banter about. Case in point, my 5-year-old came home from school the other day with a tissue and construction paper crown that she says makes her "Queen of Recycling." Words I never would have put into the same sentence when I was 5. And I am honored to wear that crown.

The Give!Guide! makes it so easy

Last year I donated to a few non-profits via Willamette Week's Give!Guide! and loved how easy it was. It's like online shopping: they have 49 non-profits from which to choose. Just select the ones you want, the amount you want to donate to each one and put it on your card. The hard part is deciding which ones to choose. Habitat for Humanity? Urban Gleaners? Schoolhouse Supplies? Growing Gardens? Community Cycling Center? All of these organizations work for our community, and the kicker check is coming...

(Oh, and you get a whole bunch of cool stuff for donating money: Tazo Tea, free Flexcar membership, a cuppa joe from Stumptown Coffee, and discounts from local retailers. Sweet.)

Opting out of phone book deliveries

You pretty much have to pay me to pick up the phone and call someone. So the idea of opting out of the phone book sounds pretty good to me. Hope sent an email about just that:

Has anyone done this?  I think I get about 5 new phone books a year. I found this post. Just wondering what others may have experienced doing this.

I too seem to get not only the baby-bell endorsed yellow pages and white pages each year, but several imitation yellow pages. I keep the Dex phone books, but all the others go straight to the recycle bin. The stat of 540 million phone books a year is mind-boggling. Here are the phone numbers of the major players:

  • AT&T/YellowPages (formerly SBC and Bell South): 1.800.792.2665
  • Verizon (Idearc): 1.888.266.5965
  • Dex: 1.877.243.8339
  • Yellow Book: 1.800.373.3280 or 1.800.373.2324

And I've just signed the Paperless Petition. Here's hoping we can make an impact!

Baby step: Make a SCRAP bin

My daughter and I went to SCRAP this morning to scout out materials for her upcoming 'green' birthday party. SCRAP (for those who don't know) stands for School and Community Reuse Action Project. Basically it is a storefront in N. Portland full of stuff donated by businesses and individuals that you can use for arts and craft projects. Stuff that would probably have ended up in a landfill. There are bins of buttons, tiles, ribbon, paper, stickers, yarn, fabric, bottle caps, wine corks, fake flowers, carpet squares -- you name it, it's there. There are a whole bunch of "what in the heck are these?" types of things -- like if a business made a widget, but a piece of it wasn't quite right -- they might donate a bushel of weird plastic parts that some crafty person might see and think, "Whoa! I can use these for ___. Score!" It can be a little overwhelming unless you go with a particular project in mind. But, the prices are really, really cheap.

As soon as we got home, I found a cardboard box and wrote SCRAP on it so that now we'd have a place to put all that weird miscellaneous stuff like: one lone earring without a mate; a broken necklace; a small, broken toy (like a matchbox car missing all its doors, or a Fisher-Price Little People person's head); excess wrapping paper or wall paper; extra porcelain tiles from a bath remodel; old chipped plates broken into small pieces for mosaics; trophies and medals; mint tins...the list goes on. Check out SCRAP's list of acceptable items. Sure, some of this stuff could be recycled (like metal objects and some papers) but, remember, there's a reason that reuse comes before recycle in reduce/reuse/recycle. It's better to reuse an item than use more energy to turn it into something else. SCRAP will be getting my scraps from now on!

(BTW, we spent $4 on all of the stuff for birthday take-home craft projects and games -- 2 bags full of goodies. I cannot wait to show you! Stay tuned...)

Rethinking Holiday cards...

Here's a recent email from Behan, which has got me thinking:

Hey guys!  Love your blog.  I had an idea for you... what about a post on holiday cards?  Families with kids send bazillions a year...so maybe some clever methods or resources for reducing the impact?  I'm thinking of DIY, or printers who use responsible products, etc., or even some ways to easily do an electronic card and forgo the mail completely.

So here's the deal in our family. Every Thanksgiving weekend, we set ourselves up for disaster. We get the kids all gussied up, try to get myself and Cheap-o-Dad at least looking presentable, then we put the digital camera on a tripod, and take thousands of pictures over what seems like an eternity just hoping to get a picture -- not a perfect picture, mind you, that would make us all look like air-brushed celebrities and their darling kids -- but just a picture where we are all facing the camera, not picking our noses, or with eyes closed, or where it's really obvious that I have a double chin and have not lost all the baby weight... So by the end of it, we are just bribing the kids with something awful like M&Ms or pulling out all the "recycled" Halloween candy and begging them to "PUH-LEASE, just look at the camera and SMILE PRETTY for us, JUST ONE MORE TIME!"

Then we all have a meltdown of some sort and we hope there might be some kind of gem hidden in the digital camera that we so far haven't noticed that will jump out and say: "Pick me! THIS is the best, most flattering and glorifying holiday picture ever taken..." But really, there's usually just one where we mostly look semi-OK, but not great, and we shrug our shoulders in defeat and say "Fine. OK. I guess this one isn't toooooo horrible. And it doesn't really look like she's picking her nose that much. And my double chin is only noticeable if you're really looking for it..." So, we pick that one. Then print up about 50 holiday cards at Costco, mail out about 40 (on a good year) of those.

And then after being mailed and read, they probably end up in someone's garbage can under the dirty kitty litter and used Q-tips.

So what are your ideas to green up the whole holiday card thing this year? It's Friday. I (hopefully) have provided the humor. Do you have any great, green holiday card ideas to share?
