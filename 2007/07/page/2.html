Do under the sink garbage disposals get the green thumbs up or down?

Hope is wondering how green under-sink garbage disposals are:

"Ok, so I'm going to the experts here, what's the word on under-sink garbage disposals?  Other than diapers, a lot of our garbage seems to be table scraps that can't be composted.  We're thinking of remodelling our kitchen next year and I was wondering if it would be good to include a garbage disposal into the plan.  Any thoughts?  Thanks!"

You are too kind to call us experts, Hope. But here's my understanding of the situation with a little help from the Master Recyclers and Portland Environmental Services' "After the Flush" publication. According to Lauren, the master recycler coordinator, "The majority of the roughage, or more fiberous material (if you will) is filtered immediately at the treatment plant and sent to the landfill.  The majority of this is paper, but some of the food from the disposal will also be filtered here. So it is the same problem: it ends up at the landfill. However, this first screen is fairly wide and I would say some of the food (especially if you have an efficient disposal) will get through this filter. It will then be put through several levels of physical or biological filters that end up then being used as compost in Eastern Oregon.  So, it is better than just throwing it away as it has a chance of being composted via the sewer system." Additionally, Lauren adds "I get the impression that Portland is unique in composting our waste from sewers. I would bet that most jurisdictions are landfilling."

I've posted about how informative the "After the Flush" brochure is, especially to kids who enjoy anything with a high grossness factor. For all intents and purposes, any wastewater or solids in the sewers, whether from flushing toilets or kitchen drains/disposals all goes through the same process. So does the garbage disposal get the thumbs up or down? Kinda depends on where you live. I'd suggest you compost as much as you can at home as a first step because then you know with certainty what is happening to it.

Another thing to keep in mind is that Portland Metro will very likely expand the recycling options of home-owners in the next two years. Hopefully we'll soon be putting kitchen scraps out for composting in curbside bins as they currently do in Seattle and San Francisco. It does remain to be seen what scraps will be included.

Searching for an enviro-friendly remodeler

Isn't it cool when you're talking about something one day, and the same topic comes up again the next day? Our GreenGroup met yesterday and we discussed enviro-friendly remodeling. Now here's an email from Marie, who is looking for a green remodeler too:

"Hi enviromoms.  We have lived in our house in NW for 10 months (moved here from Virginia) and want to do some remodeling work.  We are expecting our second child and so it just feels like its time to make the space really our own.  I'd like to make the remodeling process as environmentally green as possible, but don't really know where to start.  Do you have any suggestions?"

We spent a fair amount of time talking about remodeling in the Master Recycler class, and I was surprised to learn that building-related waste is the third largest waste stream in Oregon. The good news is that it doesn't have to be.

Check out the Office of Sustainable Development's Green Building info. You'll also want to acquaint yourself with the ReBuilding Center and their Deconstruction Services. The ReBuilding Center in an incredible non-profit that accepts donated building materials for reuse, and sells them at nominal prices to the public. The quality you can get in re-using materials is often much higher than buying new. Seems weird, but it's something to noodle over with a remodel. Deconstruction Services is the art of taking apart a building by hammer and hand, and reusing and recycling everything that can be reused and recycled and almost eliminating the need to landfill demolition waste.

As for the green remodeler/contractor Erin mentioned yesterday, I am sorry to say I forget the name. (Erin, please remind me or leave a comment if you have a minute). And anyone else who has suggestions for Marie, please chime in. Good for you in your quest to remodel green!!

Outdoor Kids: Letterboxing

In response to our new Outdoor Kids series, Hope sent us a couple of intriguing links:

Thought I would share these websites with you for getting kids excited about going outside for hikes:
www.letterboxing.org
www.geocaching.com
We do letterboxes with our 2 boys (there are so many in the Northwest!).  We haven't done the geocaching yet, but plan to soon.  So if your kids like treasure hunts, they'll love letterboxing.  Happy hunting!
I've heard of geocaching, but never letterboxing. The Letterboxing site lays it all out, and it sounds pretty fun! It's essentially a treasure hunt, searching for little boxes that have been left in public, outdoor spaces. You find the boxes by following clues, which are posted on the site. It's intended to be an environmentally-friendly game that gets you and your family out of the house and into the woods or a park. You shouldn't have to disrupt nature to find a box or hide a box. My kids, particularly my daughter, loves mysteries, hunting for clues and finding treasures (really, what kid doesn't?), so this sounds like something she would LOVE doing. Has anyone else tried letterboxing? Thanks, Hope!

Be green: Support local crafters (and get some really cool stuff)

Lucky for us, Portland is home to oodles of creative, crafty DIYers. Buying from local artisans is probably one of the greenest things you can do:

  • Hand-made creations have low-impact/no-impact manufacturing processes (it seems a sin to even suggest they are manufactured, but I'm trying to make a point here)
  • These locally-made goods didn't travel thousands of miles to get into your hands (in fact, I'll bet some arrived via bicycle)
  • Many artisans reuse existing materials, minimizing the impact on our waste stream
  • Buying from the locals means that you keep your dollars circulating through our local economy
  • The less you buy from a big box store, the less high-impact, overseas manufacturing will result

And one of the best things? You get really cool, really unique stuff. Think about upcoming birthdays and holidays and then head to one, or preferably both, of these events:

Crafty Mamas Street Fair at Milagros Boutique: July 28, 11am-3pm: Clothing for babies, children, and mothers, jewelry, toys, baby carriers and more.

Portland ETSY Art & Craft Summer Sale @ PSU Park Blocks, next to Farmers Market: Sat. August 11, 8am-4pm: Ever checked out ETSY.com? Fantastic stuff -- and here's your chance to touch it and buy it.

EWG: survey to improve infant formula safety

Thank you, Lisa, for sending us info about this important survey conducted by the Environmental Working Group to improve the safety of infant formula:

"Environmental Working Group (EWG) is conducting a survey on how much formula babies consume. The data will be used to understand infants' exposure to Bisphenol-A (BPA), a powerful hormone disruptor found in the plastic liner in consumer product packaging. Initial studies have shown that BPA can leach into infant formula.

The analysis will be presented to the National Institutes of Health (NIH). We need your help to gather food and weight information about *real babies* to understand the magnitude of this potential problem. *Timing is critical!* NIH is meeting in early August to decide about BPA use. Deadline for taking the survey is July 31. The more data collected, the more accurate the analysis."
