Curbside food scrap composting has come to Portland (and I'm on TV)!

Whew! I just spent the morning in front of many TV news cameras discussing Portland's new curbside food scrap composting pilot program with Mayor Adams! It was an amazing experience, and I'm stoked to have been a part of launching the program. Portland peeps, you should see coverage on all of the local news stations at noon and again at 5 and 6 today.

But what a coup for Portland! San Francisco and Seattle have had great success with their food composting programs, and it's so exciting that our turn has come. This pilot program will run for about a year and will only involve 2,000 households. The city wants to get feedback from the program participants and make sure it's working before the city-wide rollout next year. You can't sign up to participate -- everyone in the program will be notified this week. Participants will come from portions of the Richmond, Roseway, Centennial, Hazelwood and Arnold Creek neighborhoods. BUT HERE'S THE COOL PART:

We will be able to compost ALL types of food: meat, poultry, seafood, bones, dairy, grains, veggies and fruit, eggshells, baked goods, coffee grounds -- basically if it's food, you can compost it. They will also accept paper napkins and pizza boxes. All of this will be placed in the yard debris rollcart, along with typical yard debris, and it will be picked up weekly instead of bi-weekly. Each household will receive a 2-gallon kitchen pail to be kept under the sink or on the counter top, making it easy to dump in food scraps.

Think about it: food waste accounts for almost 30% of Portland's residential waste! That's a significant amount. Food in the landfill releases methane, a potent greenhouse gas, so we're dramatically reducing toxic emissions. Plus the food scraps will be taken to a commercial composting facility near Corvallis and will be turned into usable compost in only 8 weeks time. The compost in turn will be used by farmers and gardeners to enrich the soil and grow more food (and the lovely cycle continues...).

So a big hooray for Portland and all of the folks in the pilot program! Are any of our readers in the program? Let us know, and please post your experiences and questions here. We'll continue to post about it in anticipation of the city-wide rollout next year. Woo hoo!

Learning a thing or two from Jamie Oliver's Food Revolution

I am really, really enjoying Jamie Oliver's Food Revolution, which is kind of a surprise. As a rule I cannot stand reality TV -- all of that manufactured drama and those out-of-context 'reaction' shots. And there's always a villain. Whatever. All of that stuff is in this show, too, but if you can get past it there's some real substance here. There are lessons to be learned even if you've already hopped on the anti-processed food bandwagon and are cooking most meals from scratch. How else can I explain this image? 


This is my 8-year old, and this is only the third time she'd ever held a knife in her hand. (She's helping me prep Easter dinner.) The first time was the night before when her dad and I showed her how to use a steak knife to cut her food. The second time was when she cut her Easter pancakes by herself. Our children had never cut their own food before. On one of the first two episodes, Jamie is horrified to learn that the elementary school kids aren't given knives to cut their food. The cafeteria cooks tell him that there's no reason for them to have knives! They don't know how to use them anyways! Jame was appalled. What kind of country is this that doesn't teach children to use knives to cut their own food?! Gulp. Er, the kind where the parents think it's faster and easier if they do it themselves? Like me? It takes me 10 seconds to cut their pancakes. If they did it themselves it could take several minutes! Not that we're in a hurry or anything. So it was a big wake-up call for me. These children must learn to cut their own food. So now they are, and they're taking pride in it.

From that little success and empowerment, I asked my daughter to help with dinner. After seeing those kids on Jamie's show prepare that feast for 80, I figured I could teach her a few tricks, too. I showed her how to use a vegetable peeler on carrots and potatoes and how to chop vegetables with a six-inch knife. She ended up prepping all the veggies herself. She peeled asparagus and tossed it in olive oil. She seasoned the roast. She measured out chicken stock and white wine. We gave ourselves plenty of time and had a lot of laughs, and I believe it was the most joyous holiday meal I've ever prepared (and quite tasty I might add). She is inspired and enthused and wanting to learn more and more and more.

So Jamie, keep it coming. I hope there are millions of people watching and learning and getting inspired to stop buying microwave meals and chicken nuggets and start cooking with whole foods. I hope there are school districts paying attention and realizing that french fries ARE NOT VEGETABLES. I hope there are members of Congress who are appalled by the scary state of school lunches and childhood obesity and will fight for increased funding when the Child Nutrition Acts come up for renewal this month.

If you've missed any episodes, watch them on ABC.com! (It's even better that way because you don't have so many commercials.) And watch it with your kids! Mine were pretty grossed out watching Jamie make chicken nuggets, which is kind of the reaction I'd hoped for.

Are you watching and enjoying it?

Foodies on network TV: Michael Pollan & Jamie Oliver

Alert! Set your Tivo, your DVR, tie a string around your finger, whatever you need to remember that Michael Pollan will be on Oprah tomorrow (Wednesday)! Kudos to the media queen for bringing him and his food message to mainstream TV. It looks like they'll be quizzing the audience about eating habits, talking about what we should be eating and, most importantly, how it is produced. Plus Alicia Silverstone will be spreading her vegetarian gospel! Hooray! I've said it before, and I know that many of you have read his books, but Michael Pollan is my food hero. His books The Omnivore's Dilemma and In Defense of Food rocked my world and set my family down the path of whole foods and healthier eating. He is an engaging, down-to-earth guy and I can't wait to see him tomorrow!


I have to admit that I am intrigued by the Naked Chef Jamie Oliver's new reality show, "Jamie Oliver's Food Revolution," which I just heard about yesterday. As a general rule I avoid reality TV. But this 6-episode show has Jamie trying to revamp the eating habits of an entire town -- Huntington, West Virginia -- labeled 'The Unhealthiest City in America' by the Associated Press in 2008. Unhealthy due to high rates (50%) of obesity and its associated ailments, thought to be triggered by a high rate of poverty and lack of access to fresh produce in the area. I hope it's done well and not so over-dramatic and uncomfortable to make it unwatchable. I watched a preview on YouTube that shows Jamie taking on school lunches (yea), but then there was the bit with him crying (boo). The series is set to debut Friday, March 26 at 9pm on ABC.

Hello and welcome!

EnviroMom has had a little bit of media exposure these past few days, which always brings lots of new visitors to the site. Thanks so much for coming, and we hope you'll stick around and become part of this supportive community. We have over 25,000 visitors to the site each month coming from around the country, and those numbers continue to climb. Renee and I work really, really hard to keep the tone of this blog friendly, funny, respectful and supportive because making lifestyle changes can be intimidating and overwhelming. Updates on past posts, and other assorted tidbits

I'm long overdue to update on a few past posts, so will do so today:

  • I finally got around to conducting the "de-stink Pinky" test. I spritzed Pinky with a half/half mix of vinegar/water and PH12.6. PH12.6 worked instantly and left no residual smell. The vinegar water worked but left a residual smell for a while, but then dissipated. This is also one instance where procrastination worked in my favor as untreated areas seem to have lost the campfire smell just from airing out over time.
  • I made the soft butter using a hand-mixer instead of a stand-mixer with no problems and thumbs up from the family. I am not a kitchen whiz, but this was very easy, and no one seems to note any difference in taste from the store-bought in-a-throwaway-plastic-container version. Yay! There's one less plastic item I need to recycle!
  • This whole thing about reuse of "disposable" baby wipes is beyond cool. Sometimes when I write a post, I'm mostly just trying to be funny and then end up learning something amazing like this. I don't use a lot of baby wipes, but still have enough around, and am thrilled to be tossing them into the hamper for a wash now instead of in the trash. I even made sure during a family discussion last night that everyone understood I was throwing these in the hamper with a purpose, and for them not to think ol' mom has really lost it and is throwing garbage in the hamper.
  • Cheap-o-Dad continues to ride his bike to work. His new goal is to not gas-up again until Thanksgiving. I am trying not to freak about the horrific recent spate of bike v. truck fatalities in Portland. If there's any good in it, it's that some real discussion has been going on regarding making the roads safer for bike riders.
  • OPB will feature a special on The Silence of the Bees this Sunday on Nature from 8-9pm. Looks very interesting and worth viewing or taping. Did you know honeybees are responsible for every third bite we eat?
