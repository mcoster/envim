Walking to school: 'Just try to keep up, mom!'

I am all for walking to school. Especially on sunny, warm, dry days that come once or twice a year in the form of International Walk + Bike to School Day. Otherwise, I've been pretty happy to have my kids ride that big yellow school bus. Because:

1) The kids have almost always enjoyed the fun atmosphere of the bus, such that it has been like a rolling playdate

2) Riding the school bus is good preparation for using mass transit

3) It amounts to a full hour a free day care every day (selfish! awful! I know!!)

So when my kids spontaneously decided last weekend that they wanted to walk to and from school every day of the year from here on out forever and ever amen. I was like: whoa. Because do you know what that means? I will not be able to wave from the front window to my kids on the bus (whilst still in my slippers and PJs holding a cup of java). It means I will really need to start waking up earlier, even thought it's winter and daybreak still comes late and most mornings I feel like a hibernating bear. It means I can't linger over the morning paper as I have for the past several years.

But if there's one thing I've learned as a parent, nothing is as golden as when your kids set a healthy goal for themselves and just pounce. Thus I am trying to kick my sorry behind into gear and keep up. We've so far enjoyed the fresh air, and sociability of walking with friends every morning and afternoon. It's yet to rain on our parade. Change never comes easy, but I'm EnviroMom, right? I'd be an idiot to pass on this walking opportunity even if it means my day just got a lot shorter. Just try not to mention it if you happen to notice I'm wearing my PJs under my coat, ok?

More school salad bars means fewer fries and tater tots

Grist reports that the White House is hoping to install 5,000 salad bars in schools around the country. Amen to that.

Our elementary school has had a salad bar for ages. It's wonderful. It's self-serve. The kids are fairly responsible about taking only what they can eat. They can also come back for seconds, or thirds, as time permits (not all that much since lunch is only about 22 minutes long).

But if we want our nation's kids eating better, we gotta give them access to fruits and veggies. A salad bar does that handily. Pish, I say on fears about spreading germs, and proper height levels for the sneeze guards. Just let them eat salad and fruit, for crying out loud.

That's all I have to say. Thank you. Does your school have a salad bar? How's it working over there? What do you think about the White House salad bar expansion plan?

Recycle baby teeth

On this momentous day, my baby, my six-year-old baby, lost her first tooth. After weeks of wiggly-ness. And about a year of grousing about how all the other kids her age lost teeth in kindergarten, for crying out loud! So when the heck was she EVER going to lose a bleeping tooth? The answer came: today! She yanked it out herself this morning, with a bit more bloodiness that I'd come to expect, and probably due to the fact that Heather's six-year-old son also has a loose tooth. My competitive little one couldn't possibly let him beat her to that milestone.


As for the tooth? Don't let my headline fool you. We don't have any special networks for recycling teeth, or crazy reuse, nor compost schemes. We make an appointment with the tooth fairy like most people. But here's the 'recycling' part, and let me just add it's something my kids cooked up on their own. They say that when a big kid loses a tooth, it means a baby somewhere in the world gets a baby tooth. Personally, I'd rank that as one of the most stinking cute things I've ever heard. And it seems to adhere to good karma rules, and certain laws of (meta?) physics.

So somewhere, some time today, some little baby who's been fussing and keeping his/her parents up at night will finally cut a tooth. Congratulations. I hope you get some sleep, whoever you are! Oh, and Ms. Tooth Fairy, if you're reading this, can you please get us on your schedule? (I happen to know the Tooth Fairy follows us on both Facebook and Twitter).

Are you worried about cell phone radiation?



As I write this, movers are here in my house packing up all of our Earthly possessions. By next week we'll be calling Columbus, Ohio 'home.' We're moving into a rental house for a few months and weren't planning to get a landline. We figured we'd just use cell phones, which would be a HUGE jump for me as I am a total cell phone luddite. I own one but hardly ever use it. (And my dear friend, Renee? Bless her, doesn't own one at all.) But the more I read about cell phone radiation, I'm not sure I'm ready to be a total convert.

I've hesitated to write about this topic because on many levels it feels alarmist. I already feel like we are so alarmed by many unknowns -- like the true consequences of using plastics and eating produce that's not organic and, frankly, just breathing the air around us. I've read many contradictory articles over the past year about cell phone radiation -- whether it's truly a concern or not. In a nutshell: it is believed that the small amounts of radiation emitted from cell phones can contribute to brain cancer. This article in the NY Times is just the latest one I've read. What I found rather interesting about this article that I'd not remembered before, is that some cell phone makers actually have guidelines about how close to hold your phone to your ear. 

...the legal departments of cellphone manufacturers slip a warning about holding the phone against your head or body into the fine print of the little slip that you toss aside when unpacking your phone. Apple, for example, doesn’t want iPhones to come closer than 5/8 of an inch; Research in Motion, BlackBerry’s manufacturer, is still more cautious: keep a distance of about an inch.

This, to me, implies that cell phone radiation should be a concern. It's most important for children to keep cell phones away from their thin skulls and limit their interactions to texting or using a headset. One study cited in the article states that people who use a cell phone for ten years or more double their chances of developing brain gliomas, a type of tumor. Different phones have different levels of SAR, or specific absorption rate, which is how the radiation levels are measured. The city of San Francisco is now requiring manufacturers to prominently display the SAR rates on phones. But the FCC believes that the best way to avoid exposure:

...is not by choosing a phone with a marginally lower SAR...but rather by holding the cellphone “away from the head or body.”

We're so used to seeing EVERYONE (except Renee) walking around with cell phones plastered to their ears that it probably doesn't seem like there is any risk. I think there is one. It's frustrating that their doesn't seem to be any DEFINITIVE answer to the question, but really, cell phones haven't been around that long. Not long enough for any long-term studies, anyways. Manufacturers and the FCC certainly aren't bringing any serious attention to the issue, so it's really up to each individual user to figure out whether all of these unknowns are worth the risk. Is it worth it to you?

To find out where you cell phone ranks, check out the Environmental Working Group's database.

My low-carbon, high-super-fun gym

I've been in a bit of an eco-funk these past several weeks. I don't know if it was our extended dreary weather or the gulf oil spill, but I haven't been terribly motivated to try new 'green-living' projects. (You might have noticed, yes?) Not that I've reverted to old habits -- I mean, once you've given up Secret anti-perspirant there is no going back.! (Yesterday's high-90's weather was the ultimate test of my homemade deodorant, and I'm still loving it.) If there's been one bright spot in my day-to-day routine, it's been the accidental discovery of the most enjoyable type of exercise I've ever experienced. It's a story of a neighborhood business that gave me a whole new appreciation of my community, and frankly, my body.


It started with a postcard handed to me at our farmer's market. CrossFit Hillsdale, it said. Come try it free for a week, no obligation. I knew this place -- it had opened up right in my neighborhood just a few months earlier. Literally a 10-minute walk from my house. Hmm, a gym in my neighborhood -- that's a little too convenient. The exercises on the main Web site looked really intimidating: pull-ups and barbells and those crazy iron balls with handles (kettlebells) that make you think of Russian musclemen. It looked hard, and I doubted I could physically do it. But after a winter spent sick with non-stop sinus and respiratory infections, my body needed a kick in the ass. I kept going back to the line on the gym's Web site, that you don't have to be in good shape to do it, just show up.

So I showed up. And I kept showing up. Now, exercise is a pretty personal experience. You just need to do what works for you. And I think there's a reason that my old system of driving to the gym filled with machines and people with earbuds staring at televisions didn't work for me. I wasn't getting any support there. At my new gym, I was getting support. I was being taught correct form and being encouraged by my peers and being put in the position of really challenging myself in a very motivating environment. The workouts are different every day, and there's almost this game-like feeling to them, so it's fun. It's by far the hardest working-out I've ever done, and oftentimes I have to dumb-down an exercise or go slow, but it's fine. Because you know what? I've got abs now. And I'm getting stronger and more confident in my ability to push myself. It's awesome.



 

That's the Oprah-moment part of the story. What's also cool about this particular gym is its small footprint. It's a small space, compared to most gyms, but it makes use of every square inch. There aren't any televisions or fancy, plugged-in machines, just dumbells, barbells, kettlebells, medicine balls, a couple of rowing machines, pull-up bars and lots of floor space for push-ups, sit-ups, box jumps, jumping rope and the like. You don't need a lot of interior space to go for a run. Wally, the owner, told me that 80% of his equipment was bought used on Craigslist. Most days he rolls up the garage door so we get fresh air. It's in the basement of the building, so it stays cool on hot days. And because it's located at the base of a neighborhood, there are a few members (like me) who walk or bike there. Wally bikes there every. single. day. And, bonus: my kids both now take Kung Fu there.

So that's what I've been up to. Getting fit at a local, low-carbon gym that offers high-energy opportunities my family didn't even know existed before! We love it. Here's to taking care of ourselves and taking full advantage of our neighborhood!

Feeding a family of four on a half pound of meat

Yesterday morning, in what I believe was a moment of fate, I switched on the TV for a moment and caught Mark Bittman's cooking segment on the Today Show. Bittman is a personal hero of mine, and I've sung his praises in the past. He's a believer, much like another hero, Michael Pollan, in a diet that emphasizes plants, not meat. So here he was on the Today Show talking about how you can feed a family of four using just 1/2 pound of meat, treating meat like a condiment. The segment's spin was purely focused on saving money, but I know Bittman was also secretly relaying his own less-meat-more-veg values.

Raising meat the industrial way is terrible for the environment, the animals and our health. (See: Food, Inc.) Raising pastured, organic, free-range meat is much better for the environment, the animals and our health, but it's not sustainable at the current levels of America's meat consumption, which is 200 pounds per person per year or 1/2 pound per day. So a good alternative is to eat the drug-free meat, but eat less of it.

The Today Show hosts had serious doubts (surprise, surprise) about Bittman's recipes -- whether they'd be filling enough -- so I tested one out last night. I made his Stir-Fried Vegetables with Ground Chicken in Lettuce Wraps except I used ground pork and did not use lettuce wraps. (More on what I subbed down below.) I pretty much followed the recipe exactly as listed, though maybe not so much on veggie measurements. I just sliced and peeled until it looked like I had enough for my family.


This recipe got major thumbs up from everyone. Yes, even the children. Yes, even the picky one.

We're talking cabbage, carrots, garlic, mushrooms (shitake not necessary, button or crimini are fine), red onion (instead of scallion, for me), ginger, 1/2 pound of ground meat, soy sauce/tamari, stock or white wine (the latter, for me), a little sugar, salt & pepper. Crushed peanuts for garnish, if you like. Prepared just like he described in the link above. Delish. Flavorful. Filling. The perfect amount for a family of four, though if I had two teenage boys I might have doubled the veggies. This is like an $8 dollar dinner, $2 per person.

This dish would be great over a little brown rice. We're experimenting with a low-carb diet over here, so I cut long ribbons of a zucchini with a vegetable peeler and sauted them in olive oil, salt & pepper for two minutes. For us, it's kind of like noodles. So good. I'm sure the lettuce wraps would have been tasty, too.

Yes, America, it's possible to feed a family of four on 1/2 pound of meat. Listen to Bittman. Give it a try.

PS: I realize that most of America is not reading this blog and that I'm speaking to a bunch of people who pretty much get it. But still, it's good to revisit this once in awhile.

National conversation on chemicals coming to a city near you; plus CNN's Toxic America

This seems pretty huge: two government agencies that deal with disease and toxic substances are launching a two-year plan to gather info from the public about chemical exposure concerns. Chemicals in household products, our water systems, the air we breathe, pharmaceuticals, hazardous waste, industry -- how are these affecting our quality of life? The Centers for Disease Control and the Agency for Toxic Substances & Disease Registry are conducting 'conversations' on chemical exposure in communities throughout the country. They want citizen input. They want to hear about concerns and issues that are impacting YOUR life. This is a chance to voice your concerns and push for an overhaul of chemical regulation.

Here in Oregon, the Oregon Environmental Council is hosting five conversations in June:

Salem: Wednesday, June 2, 7:00 pm- 8:30 pm
Straub Environmental Learning Center, 1320 A Street NE
Contact: reneep@oeconline.org for more information

Eugene: Monday, June 7, 6:15 pm-7:45 pm
Eugene Public Library Tykeson Room
Contact larkin@oregontoxics.org for more information
 
Hood River: Thursday, June 10, 5:00 pm - 7:00 pm
Hood River County Museum,300 E Port Marina Drive
Contact maye@oregonpsr.org for more information

Gresham: Sunday, June 13, 12:30 pm-2:30 pm
Gresham Library, 385 Northwest Miller Avenue
Contact reneep@oeconline.org for more information

Astoria: Sunday, June 20th, 5:30 pm - 7:00 pm
Blue Scorcher Bakery Café, 1493 Duane Street
Contact maye@oregonpsr.org for more information

There doesn't seem to be a 'master calendar' that lists community conversations in other states, which isn't helpful to our many out-of-state readers. But you could email the CDC at nationalconversation@cdc.gov and see if they can help you find one. Or if you're really motivated, you can download their conversation toolkit and organize a forum in your own community.

In what seems to be a timely coincidence, CNN correspondent Dr. Sanjay Gupta is hosting a program called Toxic America on June 2 and 3 at 8pm. He'll be focusing on the health and environmental effect of toxins found in plastics. The June 2nd show focuses on a town in Louisiana that has been experiencing problems related to toxic chemical plants for decades. The June 3rd show will be focused on childhood, particularly expectant mothers and their babies, and will feature a woman who's lived plastic-free for over two years. I don't have cable, so I'm hoping this will end up online!

Have a fun, safe, long weekend, and we'll see you back here on Tuesday!

The President's Cancer Panel says it's time to regulate chemicals

The President's Cancer Panel is made up of three medical doctors who are experts in the field of oncology. They are about as mainstream medical as you can get. Their job (in a nutshell) is to assess the current cancer climate in the US and make recommendations to the President about how to handle it. Last week the panel released its annual report (for 2008-09), which was focused on environmental risks, namely chemicals. It's a stunner. The panel blames lax regulation for the fact that we're all walking around with hundreds of chemicals in our bloodstream manifesting in increased cancer risks. Here's a quote from NY Times columnist Nicholas Kristof on the subject of the panel's report:

Noting that 300 contaminants have been detected in umbilical cord blood of newborn babies, the study warns that: “to a disturbing extent, babies are born ‘pre-polluted.’

(I highly recommend reading his op-ed on the study.)

(Did you know that there are over 80,000 chemicals used in the US and only a few hundred have been tested for safety?)

I think what is so stunning about the study is that for so long environmentalists have been pooh-poohed for raising alarms over toxins and chemicals. Now here's a panel of doctors (two of which were appointed by George W. Bush) telling us we should be:

  • eating organic foods and free-range meats whenever possible
  • avoiding BPA and phthalates and drinking water from glass, stainless steel or safe plastic containers
  • not microwaving foods in plastic containers
  • filtering tap water
  • avoiding toys and foods containing endocrine disrupters
  • using databases like The Household Products Database (No way. It sucks. Use the Cosmetics Database and the Good Guide.) to research common products

This is all stuff that the green movement has been advocating for years, so for this to be directly linked to cancer by this mainstream panel is huge. But what will ultimately become of it? Right now it's up to the individual to make informed choices (which typically involves a lot of research). When will the responsibility be placed on manufacturers? Will we ever see stricter regulation, or will the deep pockets of industry and lobbyists continue to beat it down?

The panel stresses that children, in particular, should be shielded as much as possible from toxins:

An analysis by the National Academy of Science found that children are particularly vulnerable to environmental contaminants for several reasons. Due to their smaller size, children’s exposures to toxics are disproportionately large compared with adults. Because their metabolic pathways are immature (particularly during fetal development and in the first months after birth), they are slower to metabolize, detoxify, and excrete many environmental chemicals. As a result, toxins remain active in their bodies for a longer period of time than would be the case in adults.

So, what do we do? Make the best decisions we can for our families based on the information available. Refer to books on the subject of toxins. (I find myself reaching for Healthy Child Healthy World when in doubt.) Use the Environmental Working Group's form to send a message to your Senators asking them to co-sponsor the Safe Chemicals Act. Pay attention. Demand reform. What else?
