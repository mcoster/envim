Baby step: cutting down on washing towels

Family reunion. An annual summertime event. Three elementary school aged girls. Two teenagers. One EnviroMom and one EnviroDad, all sharing one bathroom. Last year? The washing of towels was a non-stop endeavor. Hand towels. Washcloths. Bath towels. Beach towels. This year, with a little pre-planning the washing of towels has been whittled down to a minimum. Hey -- if the better hotels can ask you to hang your towels and use them repeatedly, then so can we. But it wasn't so much the issue of reusing, as keeping track of whose towel was whose. When the (ahem) children typically just toss them on the floor when finished.

Enter an easy fix. Each person was issued one large white bath towel, marked with a colored ribbon and safety pin of their own choosing. The bath towel does double duty as a hand towel. No sharing of germs and an avoidance of the dreaded disposable bathroom hand towels. Given the briskness of Seattle summers, the beach towels haven't been an issue thus far.

Less time washing towels = more time for family fun. Easy peasy.

Oregon: buy an Energy Star appliance in 2010 and save big

If you've been cursing your 20-year old fridge, dishwasher and washing machine, then it's probably time for an upgrade. Both the Oregon Department of Energy and the Energy Trust of Oregon are offering incentives that can knock a big chunk off the price of qualifying new Energy Star appliances. But you have to act before December 31, 2010. Check it out:

Refrigerator/Freezers: Get a residential energy tax credit for 25% of the purchase price (max credit is $90) plus an additional $50 from Energy Trust. As a bonus, Energy Trust will come haul away your old fridge and give you $30 to boot. Total potential savings: $170.

Dishwashers: Get the 25% energy tax credit (up to $80) plus $25 through Energy Trust, for a total savings of $105.

Clothes Washer: The maximum tax credit is $180 combined with $75 from Energy Trust for a whopping $255 in incentives.

In addition, Energy Trust provides lots of info on incentives for other home improvements like new windows (we recently saved a bundle on new windows through the federal energy tax program), high efficiency gas fireplaces and solar electric systems. Save energy, save money, save the planet! (No pressure or anything.)

Sunday Parkways: Let's ride! (or walk! or hoop!)

Yesterday, our family participated in the first Portland Sunday Parkways event we could fit in this summer, and it was awesome. Glorious sunny day. So many families out biking, running, roller skating, strolling babies, skate-boarding, or just plain old walking.



We've always used the occasion to bike ride, as it gives our young riders a chance to safely practice their still developing riding skills. There weren't that many sudden stops that nearly caused a pile-up among the riders behind us. But I sure did witness and experience a few of those and even caught myself blurting: 'Hey kid! You can't just stop in the middle of the road!' Learning experiences, with no blood lost. Yet, I need to remember that wheels are not required. It'd be just as fun to show up in some running or walking shoes, and take Sunday Parkways at a slower pace.

For the kids, the music and playgrounds and park activities are probably the best part. Not to mention the VooDoo donuts.



Me? I just like riding along, feeling the wind on my arms. Checking out electric bikes up close. Seeing different neighborhoods of our great city with little gardens everywhere. Being a part of the community of family friendly Portland bike culture. Observing all the bike boulevard and bike lane improvements that happen in a year's time. Seeing the friendly smiling faces of the Portland police as they manage the dicier intersections, and getting the chance to thank them and the countless volunteers who make these events possible.

There's three more Sunday Parkways coming this summer. If you can, put on your really flattering bike shorts (ha, ha!) and ride! Or walk, or hoop or what-have-you. Just get outside. Good times!

Driveway freebies: the best way to shop


Driving down our street last week, I spied a lonely white bookcase wearing a FREE sign sitting at the end of my neighbor's driveway. Screeech went my brakes. Mine. Mine, mine, mine. It was in great shape and the perfect size for my son's closet. I've been wanting to organize his closet and had contemplated investing in a 'system.' Ha! I say to that. I can't tell you how happy that little bookcase made me. I'm a fan of reuse, but I am NOT a fan of traveling from reuse store to reuse store hoping to find what I'm looking for. I think more people should put their unwanted goods at the ends of their driveways for people like me who hate to shop.

I'm sure this is frowned upon, or possibly even illegal in some places. So sad. It essentially saves two trips: one for the homeowner, and one for the person who stumbles onto the find. I remember putting out a pile of bricks in front of our home one summer's day after replacing our old patio. They were snapped up in 10 minutes, much to the delight of both parties. 

There was a time in my life when I would never have stopped to take someone's free junk, but I like to think I'm a far more evolved person now. :-) Do you delight in driveway freebies? Or does it give you the heebie jeebies?

Dryer balls: how do you machine dry clothes, avoid static and those stinky dryer sheets?

I was doing a load of laundry yesterday, and it dawned on me that we have yet to put up the clothesline and have not solar dried one single piece of clothing so far this year. It has been such a funny spring. So there we were. On the deck. The sun was shining nicely: not too hot, but pleasant. The kids were alternating between jumping rope, hula hooping and building fairy houses. I was reading a book, more or less undisturbed, while they played. That's when it dawned on me about the clothesline. And do you think I jumped right up and put my crazy clothesline system of rope and bungee cords into place? Nah. It was just too lovely to break the spell. But next time, I will. I promise.

So I went to the basement, and loaded our trusty dryer with the wet clothes. Then did something crazy! I threw some tennis balls in with it. I've been researching alternative static busting methods for clothes dryers. Sure, you can buy those spiky plastic balls. Or you can get some lovely felted wool ones that cost twice as much. Or I could continue to use the giant box of nasty chemically scented dryer sheets that I have had since my 8-year-old was an infant. Yet, I find that the more I eliminate unnatural scents from our lives, the more I am troubled by those that still are hanging on.


Thus I dipped into the world's largest collection of flat tennis balls, threw three in the dryer and crossed my fingers (If you'd like to try this at home, and are lacking the necessary tennis ballsâ€¦ Call me. I'll set you up). Survey says? It worked. Laundry was as static free as when I used a dryer sheet, but sans the fake chemical-induced 'springtime freshness' stench. There was one pair of girl tights that are synthetic that had a wee bit of static electricity, but everything else in the load was cottony and static free. Nice.

What do you use to eliminate static while machine drying your clothing? I've noticed the above mentioned spiky balls and wool balls at various shops (not to be confused with Alec Baldwin's Schweddy Balls of SNL fame). And Mrs. Meyers and other natural brands have their version of dryer sheets. Would love for y'all to weigh in on what you use and how it all comes out in the wash.

Tips for picking berries at a U-pick farm

Despite the horribly soggy spring we've had here in the Pacific Northwest, we managed to get out last week to pick some strawberries. I met up with three friends and their offspring over at Lee Farms in Tualatin (not organic, but they don't spray) and went home with 34 pounds of Hood strawberries. It felt so good to be out in the field on a rare, sunny day, bent over, brushing aside leaves in search of ripe, red fruit. And finally, after a couple of years of U-pick experience, I feel like I'm finally getting the system down.

Call First: Since this spring has been so miserable, some farms might not be open every day for U-picking because the fruit might not be ripe enough. Always call the morning you want to pick to double-check.

Containers: Many U-pick farms will provide flat, cardboard boxes, but I try to bring my own containers from home. I have a motley assortment of lidded plastic containers that I use, all of which stack nicely into a reusable shopping bag, leaving a free arm to carry any additional containers I might need. (See also: Kids) This trip I had to use a couple of the farm boxes because I didn't bring enough of my own, but I can reuse them on the next trip.

 
It's nice to use the flattest container possible so that you aren't piling lots of tender berries on top of one another. Mine are a little too deep, but they get the job done. Renee has a bunch of shirt-box-sized flat lidded containers that I think are perfect. I might have to steal them.


Kids: Picking berries with kids can be tricky. I like to devote at least 90 minutes (if not more) to picking, but my kids get restless after about 20 minutes. Luckily I have a group of friends who love u-picking, and all of our kids are friends, so we try to arrange to be at the farm at the same time. The kids end up hanging out together; they're happy and we're happy.


Last week was particularly adorable -- this group formed the Strawberry Army and marched up and down the rows chanting 'Hut 2-3-4, Hut 2-3-4!' We all felt much better knowing these precious strawberries were being protected by such devoted troops. Also, bring water and snacks. (You'd think a field of strawberries would provide enough snacking, but my kids get tired of eating them, which seems so wrong.) Also, make them help you carry berries to the car. Many hands make light work, etc.


Sun Protection: Long-sleeved shirts, hats and sunscreen -- you really need them because there is no shade out there. Renee here strongly believes that one sun hat is simply not enough.


Look at the size of those weeds! A good sign of a no-spray farm.

Processing: So what do you do with 34 pounds of strawberries once you get home, dirty, exhausted and sore? You get back to work. Last year I made a ton of strawberry freezer jam, and we still have a ton left over. So this year I'm totally focused on freezing whole berries for smoothies and baking. I rinse them, cut the tops off and lay them flat side down on whatever baking sheets and pans I can find and pop them into our massive upright freezer. After they're frozen I bag them up in gallon zip-locs.

Oh, precious berries, how I love thee in the dark depths of winter. Oh, precious sunshine, will you ever come out again?

I want to pick at least 100 pounds this summer, but we need some freaking sun! Have you been out picking yet? Any good tips to share? To find a U-pick near you, check out PickYourOwn.

What do you cook on cast iron cookware?

Do you ever buy yourself something in the hope that you will use it or wear it, and then it sits in your closet collecting dust? Oh, you may peek at it from time to time. Like a scarf that might look fantastic on a chic woman in Paris, but not so much on you (or me!) in Portland, Oregon. So there it sits. Lonely. Dejected. A museum piece.

I fear that may happen with my cast iron skillet I just triumphantly bought at a rummage sale a few weeks ago. Problem being? I don't know what to cook in it. I remember my mom making fried eggs and bacon in hers. Not something we eat a lot of.


Couple this conundrum with the brand spanking new double-sided cast iron griddle we presented my husband with for Father's Day. If my husband hadn't already claimed the super-hero name 'Cheap-o-Dad' he might easily jump into the persona of 'Pancake Man!' He loves pancakes more than life itself (while I like a pancake just fine, I usually find after eating pancakes that I am hungry again by about 9:30, a mere hour or so after breakfast). Regardless, Saturday is holy pancake day (and I just have to deal with my hungries). The need to replace the old pancake griddle we had received as a wedding gift was noticed some time ago. I shudder to tell you, but it was in the family of non-stick, quite possibly Teflon. And after 14 years of marriage, and untold batches of pancakes, it did not look good.

So, dear readers. Do tell. What do you cook on your cast iron cookware? In your skillets and on your griddles? I really want to make a love connection with cast iron and get any and all remainders of Teflon-ish cookware out of my house once and for all. Any tips about cleaning and keeping cast iron seasoned are highly welcome.

It's the summer of Outdoor Kids! (really...)


It is our first day of summer break. Last week it all looked so promising. The sun came out for the first time in what seemed like decades. My husband took Friday off, and right after school, we picked up the kids, changed into grubby clothes and went out strawberry picking. It was glorious! My youngest said something along the lines of: 'This feels like June is finally summer actually for real.' Couldn't have said it better myself. Over the weekend, the shiny happy trend continued. We put on sunscreen for the first time and went to a parade. Oh, those greasy, white-smeared clown faces of summer using healthier sunscreens. We've missed the clown faces!

Fast forward to now. It's cold and gray. We are on the verge of having the wettest June ever. And coldest! My freaking furnace kicked on this morning. Instead of strawberry smoothies, we just might be whipping up some hot cocoa. Argh.

Regardless, I will not be deterred in my plans to get the kids outdoors for much of the summer. It will be the summer of Outdoor Kids! Sure, we may need our parkas, but we are Oregonians. It is what we do (when we are not feeling like wusses).

What are your favorite outdoor kid friendly activities? How do you plan to spend the summer?
