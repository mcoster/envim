Oregon couple rocks their personal garbage reduction challenge!

OK, so when is living a waste-free lifestyle going to become an Olympic sport? I think we start petitioning the Olympic Committee right after you read this...

Now, Heather and I strive to keep garbage to a minimum within our two EnviroMom households, and we boast putting out only one garbage can of waste per month. Respectable for families of four, with kids! Because you've probably heard that the average American family produces 4.5 pounds of garbage per person per day. So imagine, if you will, an Oregon couple who produced a mere 3 pounds IN A YEAR! Yes, you read that right. Three pounds.

Thanks to Willamette Week, I just read about Amy and Adam Korst, of Dallas, Oregon, who have just completed their Green Garbage Project. A year of striving to live trash free, with a fan-flippin-fantastic result of only having 3 pounds of garbage after their extreme waste-free adventure.

So what, you may ask, did they end up tossing? Some rubber gloves, a few light bulbs, the lining from a box of Cracker Jack, the blister packaging from a year's worth of birth control pills, the heads from some razors, among other things. Not a whole heck of a lot. Just goes to show what you can do when you put your mind to it.

Read all about the Korst couple's garbage free adventure at GreenGarbageProject.com. Nice job you two!!

Could you give up air-conditioning in your home?

Our friend, fellow Master Recycler and frequent commenter, JulieG, alerted us to this fascinating interview on NPR's On Point program -- an interview with Stan Cox who wrote the book Losing Our Cool about the environmental ramifications of air-conditioning. First consider some of the points Stan made:

  • In the US, we use 1/2 trillion kilowatts of energy on air-conditioning each year. This is the equivalent of every household in America buying another car and driving it 7,000 miles a year.
  • This is also the same amount of total electricity used yearly in Africa.
  • 3/4 of electric energy generated in the US is via fossil fuels.
  • 92% of new homes built in the US have air-conditioning; now more than 80% of all homes in the US have it.
  • A/C is growing in India; now 40% of energy use in Mumbai comes from A/C; 10 years ago it was none.
  • The more energy we spend cooling our homes, the more greenhouse emissions are released, the hotter the climate.

What is to be done? People have become accustomed to A/C. Most folks in the South can't imagine life without it. Here in the Northwest it's not as prevalent as other parts of the country; there are maybe five days each summer when the temp rises over the mid-90s. My house doesn't have it, and except for those few days, I don't miss it. Growing up in Indiana you couldn't imagine not having it -- the goal was to go from air-conditioned house to air-conditioned car to an air-conditioned store. But we are paying for it.

The show had on folks who talked about design solutions -- designing homes and buildings in such a way as to not need A/C. Insulation, reflective roofs, not cutting down large shade trees -- all of these factors help. But what do you do about the millions of households that already have and love their central air? And office buildings? We've built ourselves a real hot mess.

Can you imagine not turning on your air-conditioning any more in an effort to decrease global warming?

Electric bikes? Anyone, anyone...

I was out for my evening walk last night, and just on the last leg heading into my street when coming up fast upon me were two bikes. Now, living in America's second most bikey city, fast bikes on our hilly residential street are no surprise. You wouldn't believe the Lance Armstrong look-alikes who make our street a training route on a regular basis. Mostly, they're 80 percent male and dreamy in that tight bike shorts thighs of steel way. (The females are just as impressive, but I'm not going to go there, ok?)

But hold on! These folks were not spandex clad. And, get this: they were wearing normal clothing and IMPRACTICAL shoes. Because they were riding electric bikes. I have been seeing more and more of these newfangled (my goal is to use the word 'newfangled' in as many posts as I can this July) contraptions. I've read about eBikes. I've never test ridden one. But, damn. They seem pretty kick-ass. I cannot emphasize enough how hilly our neighborhood is. Not exactly San Francisco, but darned close. HILLY! And these middle-aged people in regular clothes and impractical shoes were just shredding up the hill, and not even winded and going 'Oh, hello. Nice evening, isn't it?' Whereas, a serious bike rider on these hills would still be shredding but huffing and puffing like all get-out. A not so serious biker (ahem, like myself) would probably be walking the bike and seeing stars and trying really hard not to have a heart attack or pass out.

Now, I realize that many serious Portland bikers think eBikes suck because they are like cheating. What I think is cool is that they bring bike riding to a practical level for almost any rider. So many people say they can't ride a bike -- they're too old or whatever. Balderdash! Poppycock! Price tags for eBikes can be hefty (in the range of $500 - $3,500). But when you compare to the price of a car, that doesn't look so bad.

Riding an electic bike yet? If you're planning to ride the Sunday Parkways in SE Portland this weekend, there might be some test drive opportunities for electric bikes as at the last one. Would love to get your feedback!

Outdoor kids: finding snake skins and slug races

Welcome to the middle of summer! How did that happen? In some ways, it's going so fast I can't believe it. Other days, I start to wonder why it is that my kids think they need to follow me to the bathroom and be with me every single minute of every day and how I've managed to keep my sanity thus far.


The short answer? The great outdoors. My kids are better people when they play outside. The hula hoops and jump ropes have seen a lot of action Then there's the nature appreciation stuff. We've found two snake skins, which have become hallowed treasures. And the kids have also debuted a new game: snake slithering out of its skin. That's a good one for a really hot day when you've been out all morning, and it's time to retreat to your blissfully chilly basement and sort of play, but not really exert yourself.

I was also hanging with Heather's son a few days ago, and he was telling me about something he recently did at a nature camp: slug races! All you need is a few slugs (we have plenty), a hula hoop and some time. Pick a slug, put it in the middle of the hoop on the ground (substitute a chalk circle on pavement, or make a circle with string if you're not a hula-hooping fiend as we are in this family). And then just watch and cheer and wait for your slug to crawl to victory!

Next up? We're setting up a stake-out to spy those ravenous birds that have been eating all of our blueberries. We won't really take them out. We'll just give them friendly encouragement to move on. Maybe force them to listen to really bad loud music. You know. CIA tactics.

What are your favorite outdoor kid activities so far this summer?

Noodling: popsicles, cars and eggs

There are three things on my mind. (Yep, there's only room for three.)

Homemade popsicles. I never buy popsicles. Ever. 1. You can't recycle freezer boxes in Portland. 2. You can't recycle the plastic wrapper. 3. You can't recycle the stick. That's a whole lot of energy and natural resources wasted. And our one can of garbage doth protest. My kids aren't happy about the popsicle situation, but they solve this by going to the neighbor's house where there are always popsicles in the freezer. This is embarrassing. So we're experimenting with homemade. Except I don't want to buy a mold, so there's this:

 
  
Tall, skinny shot glasses and chopsticks. Amazingly, this works pretty well, but we're still trying to figure out the best way to get the frozen pop out of the glass without breaking it. So far we just let them defrost a few minutes, then I grab the glass with a towel and pull on the stick with all of my might. So much for spontaneous pops. Also, recipes. We tried half vanilla yogurt, half raspberry puree, which was a bit tart. I'm thinking plain yogurt, strawberries and a little sugar. I'd love your ideas.

One car. Could my family live with one car? Lots of families do, but could my family? Could my husband take the bus or bike to work sometimes? Could I bike to the grocery store in the rain? Of course the answer is yes, we're physically capable of these things, but can we surmount the mental hurdle of inconvenience?

Eggs. I've been eating a lot of eggs lately. My favorite eggs cost $6 a dozen.


They're from a local farm and are sold at my neighborhood co-op. Pastured hens out there in the grass, eating grubs and insects. Gorgeous eggs, they are. Also better for you. But $6 -- holy. I don't buy them all the time, but wish I could. I spend a lot of time staring at eggs in the grocery store.

Thanks for the brain dump. Have a splendid weekend!

My low-carbon, high-super-fun gym

I've been in a bit of an eco-funk these past several weeks. I don't know if it was our extended dreary weather or the gulf oil spill, but I haven't been terribly motivated to try new 'green-living' projects. (You might have noticed, yes?) Not that I've reverted to old habits -- I mean, once you've given up Secret anti-perspirant there is no going back.! (Yesterday's high-90's weather was the ultimate test of my homemade deodorant, and I'm still loving it.) If there's been one bright spot in my day-to-day routine, it's been the accidental discovery of the most enjoyable type of exercise I've ever experienced. It's a story of a neighborhood business that gave me a whole new appreciation of my community, and frankly, my body.


It started with a postcard handed to me at our farmer's market. CrossFit Hillsdale, it said. Come try it free for a week, no obligation. I knew this place -- it had opened up right in my neighborhood just a few months earlier. Literally a 10-minute walk from my house. Hmm, a gym in my neighborhood -- that's a little too convenient. The exercises on the main Web site looked really intimidating: pull-ups and barbells and those crazy iron balls with handles (kettlebells) that make you think of Russian musclemen. It looked hard, and I doubted I could physically do it. But after a winter spent sick with non-stop sinus and respiratory infections, my body needed a kick in the ass. I kept going back to the line on the gym's Web site, that you don't have to be in good shape to do it, just show up.

So I showed up. And I kept showing up. Now, exercise is a pretty personal experience. You just need to do what works for you. And I think there's a reason that my old system of driving to the gym filled with machines and people with earbuds staring at televisions didn't work for me. I wasn't getting any support there. At my new gym, I was getting support. I was being taught correct form and being encouraged by my peers and being put in the position of really challenging myself in a very motivating environment. The workouts are different every day, and there's almost this game-like feeling to them, so it's fun. It's by far the hardest working-out I've ever done, and oftentimes I have to dumb-down an exercise or go slow, but it's fine. Because you know what? I've got abs now. And I'm getting stronger and more confident in my ability to push myself. It's awesome.



 

That's the Oprah-moment part of the story. What's also cool about this particular gym is its small footprint. It's a small space, compared to most gyms, but it makes use of every square inch. There aren't any televisions or fancy, plugged-in machines, just dumbells, barbells, kettlebells, medicine balls, a couple of rowing machines, pull-up bars and lots of floor space for push-ups, sit-ups, box jumps, jumping rope and the like. You don't need a lot of interior space to go for a run. Wally, the owner, told me that 80% of his equipment was bought used on Craigslist. Most days he rolls up the garage door so we get fresh air. It's in the basement of the building, so it stays cool on hot days. And because it's located at the base of a neighborhood, there are a few members (like me) who walk or bike there. Wally bikes there every. single. day. And, bonus: my kids both now take Kung Fu there.

So that's what I've been up to. Getting fit at a local, low-carbon gym that offers high-energy opportunities my family didn't even know existed before! We love it. Here's to taking care of ourselves and taking full advantage of our neighborhood!

How to naturally deodorize and clean a car's interior

Peee-yew! My car stinks. And unlike me, it's not the armpits (car pits?). But the whole inside. I'd guess we've u-picked at least a hundred pounds of strawberries and raspberries at this point, and you'd think summer berries might naturally perfume the interior space of a car? Well, not really. Given a little time in the hot sun, mixed with the various body odors we humans emit, that then coat the interior windows and hard surfaces with an oily, stinky slime. Not so much!

So, it's time for an interior car spa treatment! I did find that baking soda did a remarkable job removing the smell of kid vomit that filled our car once. Thus a dusting of baking soda, perhaps overnight, followed by a brisk vacuuming might de-stink and help the upholstered surfaces. As for the windows… vinegar? Lemon juice? Again, I fear the lingering scents might not be too sweet smelling. Perhaps if I wash the interior surfaces of the windows with vinegar or lemon juice, and let them air out with windows open overnight?

I'd love to hear your tried-and-true suggestions for naturally cleaning and deodorizing a car's interior…

Oregon: it just got easier to recycle paint

A new program just launched that makes it much easier to get rid of all of those partially-used paint cans you have lurking in your garage. PaintCare, a non-profit that manages Oregon's Paint Stewardship Program, has partnered with paint stores around the state to offer collection of unused paint from consumers and contractors. Currently about thirty retailers around Oregon will accept your paint for recycling, and that number will expand to ninety by the end of the year. The paint will be sent to Portland's Swan Island recycling facility, where it will either be mixed and turned into new paint available through the MetroPaint store or used as additives in other building products.

Now, don't look at this as an opportunity to be careless with your paint purchases (not that you would). Part of PaintCare's efforts involve educating consumers about buying only what you need. And, if you want to keep a little extra on hand for touch-ups, store it properly so it doesn't dry out. To keep the seal tight around the paint can lid, cut out a piece of plastic bag or film and place it over the opening before hammering on the lid.

There is no cost to drop off your old paint -- a small fee has been added to the sale of new paint to cover the costs. To find a drop-off location near you, enter your zip code at the PaintCare Website.
