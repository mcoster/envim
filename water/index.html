Washing laundry in cold water

Betty from our SW Portland GreenGroup was the first person to suggest washing laundry in cold water. I admit I was skeptical. So often we get stuck in habits and are just unwilling to try something new. Well, at least that's what I was feeling about washing all my laundry in cold water. Then I heard a little more about it via New American Dream's Beat the Heat, Wash in Cold campaign. I was shocked to learn that:

  • 90% of the energy used in washing clothing is from heating the water
  • If you wash 80% or 4 out of 5 loads on cold/cold, you'll cut 72 pounds of CO2 emission in one month
  • If you wash 80% of your laundry on cold/cold for a year, you should save from $60-100 on your energy bills

So I tried it. And I can't complain at all. The clothing smells and looks just as clean as when I was washing on warm/cold. I can't believe I didn't try this sooner. I also worry a lot less about the laundry, because usually I forget to read the tags and don't know when to wash delicate things on cold. Now everything is on cold. And it's good. I'm curious now to compare some of our electricity bills with past years to see if we are really saving money too.

Do you wash on cold?

DIY: fix a drippy faucet?

I find myself with two pre-drippy faucets. They don't drip all the time. But sometimes, it seems like the valve doesn't get shut off all the way, or it seems to drip, drip, drip, drip, drip... then finally stop. I have a little brochure from the Portland Water Bureau that talks about how wasteful it is to ignore a drippy faucet. Buckets of clean water just dripping down into the sewer. And it says it's an easy do-it-yourself project. But I'm not so sure. Looks like a lot of little washers, screws, and specialized things (teflon tape?) are needed, and have to be put back in place, in order, and not screwed down too tight. The brochure does not account for my 5-year-old and 3-year-old plumbing apprentices sure to be "helping" at every step.

In our family, I'm the "handy-man", if you will. Cheap-o is our go-to guy for any kind of technical computer stuff.

I'll be the first to admit, I felt like a superhero when I fixed our running toilets last year. Just wondering if I should try and tackle these semi drippy faucets. Have you ever done it yourself? Let me know if it's do-able, or if I should just call a professional plumber.

Do under the sink garbage disposals get the green thumbs up or down?

Hope is wondering how green under-sink garbage disposals are:

"Ok, so I'm going to the experts here, what's the word on under-sink garbage disposals?  Other than diapers, a lot of our garbage seems to be table scraps that can't be composted.  We're thinking of remodelling our kitchen next year and I was wondering if it would be good to include a garbage disposal into the plan.  Any thoughts?  Thanks!"

You are too kind to call us experts, Hope. But here's my understanding of the situation with a little help from the Master Recyclers and Portland Environmental Services' "After the Flush" publication. According to Lauren, the master recycler coordinator, "The majority of the roughage, or more fiberous material (if you will) is filtered immediately at the treatment plant and sent to the landfill.  The majority of this is paper, but some of the food from the disposal will also be filtered here. So it is the same problem: it ends up at the landfill. However, this first screen is fairly wide and I would say some of the food (especially if you have an efficient disposal) will get through this filter. It will then be put through several levels of physical or biological filters that end up then being used as compost in Eastern Oregon.  So, it is better than just throwing it away as it has a chance of being composted via the sewer system." Additionally, Lauren adds "I get the impression that Portland is unique in composting our waste from sewers. I would bet that most jurisdictions are landfilling."

I've posted about how informative the "After the Flush" brochure is, especially to kids who enjoy anything with a high grossness factor. For all intents and purposes, any wastewater or solids in the sewers, whether from flushing toilets or kitchen drains/disposals all goes through the same process. So does the garbage disposal get the thumbs up or down? Kinda depends on where you live. I'd suggest you compost as much as you can at home as a first step because then you know with certainty what is happening to it.

Another thing to keep in mind is that Portland Metro will very likely expand the recycling options of home-owners in the next two years. Hopefully we'll soon be putting kitchen scraps out for composting in curbside bins as they currently do in Seattle and San Francisco. It does remain to be seen what scraps will be included.

Cheap-o-Dad: fun with greywater

Cheap-o-Dad has quickly gotten behind this green thing, once he realized you could be cheap and green at the same time.

One of his pet projects is greywater reuse. The first thing we did was put a big bucket outside the main exterior hose faucet. We have changed hoses, changed the gaskets, tried magic chants, and nothing will stop that hose connection from dripping when it is on. So now we make sure all the drip water is caught in a bucket, and we use it primarily to water the exterior plants.

The second one was to get a bucket for the shower. We use it to capture all the cold water that runs out (before it turns hot enough to take the shower). If we had any houseplants we'd water those, but that not being the case (I composted them when my kids hit the toddler stage -- too many were poisonous and I would flip out when they'd start playing with the dirt in the pots, making mud pies inside the house). So Cheap-o-Dad mostly uses this water for cold water torture in the bathtub. While the tub is filling, the kids usually hop in and then Cheap-o-Dad squirts cold greywater from the shower bucket on them via squirt-guns or dumps it on them from little re-used yogurt buckets. You'd think they'd hate this. But they squeal and laugh and seem to love this weird form of bathtub torture my husband has concocted for them.

Recently, Cheap-o-Dad has taken to cutting the top off a plastic milk jug to enlarge the opening, and keeps it under the tap in the kitchen sink. This captures any greywater from hand-washing of dishes. We then use this water to pre-rinse before putting dishes in the dishwasher or also for watering exterior plants.

While we haven't gotten around to disconnecting our downspouts and capturing rainwater, we seem to have really made a dent in conserving indoor water use (our water bill shows a major decrease in monthly water usage). Do you reuse your greywater, and if so, what are your favorite tricks?

Are you reaping Clean River Rewards?

My friend Amy recently went to a presentation about Clean River Rewards. I'd been under the false impression that the rewards only went to certain areas on the eastside of town. I'm sharing what we discussed, in the hopes it'll motivate more of us to sign up for Clean River Rewards:

"The rebates are citywide. Commissioner Sam Adams had a special session at the Multnomah Arts Center in which he said our part of town has special challenges in containing runoff because of our hilly terrain, and for that reason the city is going to be a little more generous in allowing rebates in SW.

It doesn't take much to meet the criteria for a rebate, especially for newer construction like ours. For example, we have a French drain, which we didn't even put in -- the builders did because our lot slopes from north to south -- and that counts as an anti-runoff action. Another one is disconnecting downspouts, which I didn't understand at first, but it turns out to be just having your gutter downspout disperse rainwater into your lawn rather than into the sewer system. I have to go outside and check, but I'm pretty sure our downspouts came that way.

If I recall correctly, even planting large trees can count. Adams had a PowerPoint that showed some pretty ingenious DIY methods of containing runoff, including digging a subtle depression into your lawn."

Are you signed up for Clean River Rewards? What changes did you make to qualify?

Blanket your hot water heater, and other ways to be hot water energy efficient

A comment by Becky the other day about conserving electricity got me thinking that my hot water heater was in need of a blanky. I took a trip to the hardware store, and bought a blanket. They're cheap -- less than 20 bucks. And it's one-size-fits-all. I had checked to see how big my tank was before I went, but it turns out I should have done a little internet research, and read the water heater manual first.

The rules of thumb for blanketing your water heaters:

  • If your water heater is more than 10 years old, you will likely save energy by blanketing it
  • If your water heater feels warm to the touch, you will likely save energy by blanketing it

Ours is less than 10 years old. We'd even bought a very energy-efficient model when the old one died a few years back. But what I did find is that our hot water heater has a few energy savings settings that I was not utilizing:

  • I dropped the temp a bit from 120 to 115 degrees. With the current summery weather, I didn't think we'll notice that difference.
  • I switched from Smart, to Energy Smart in the setting. Hopefully that will help a bit. There are also a few other modes for if we go on vacation, that I've never used but I vow to in the future.

The other point Becky made was to insulate exposed hot water pipes coming from the heater. So I am going to return the blanket, and exchange it for some pipe insulation. TTFN! Off to the hardware store!!

Project: Ridding the deck and patio of winter grime

I feel fairly confident that warmer weather and al fresco dining are just around the corner, so over the weekend I decided to get the deck and patio cleaned up. The deck is cedar, stained with a durable paint/stain blend so it doesn't need to be treated every year, and the patio is stamped concrete. Typically my husband would rent a pressure washer to take care of the slick layer of grime and goopiness that winter brings, but this year I paused and asked, "What would Ed Begley, Jr. do?" Not really. But I did want to avoid using the huge amount of water wasted by pressure washers.

First I tried just sweeping it off with the push broom to no avail. Then I wet down the whole deck with a hose and used my quivering upper body muscles to vigorously sweep/scrub the wood with the push broom. The grime came off so easily, and when I'd swept the whole deck I rinsed it all off again and it looked good as new. I even got down on my hands and knees with a sponge to get the corners and under the railing. It worked just as well on the concrete. It also took a lot less time to do it this way because with the pressure washer you just hit little patches at a time, but with the broom I was able to clean large swaths with one fell swoop. A little more elbow grease, yes, but ultimately worth it.

Have you discovered any green spring cleaning tricks lately?

Decreasing water usage in the home: how's it going?

Our first KidTip sheet featured decreasing water usage in the home. I'm glad to report that our family has come a long way in making positive water conservation changes:

  • I have stopped pre-rinsing the dishes before putting them in the dishwaster. We got a new energy-star dishwasher a few months back, and after having crappy dishwashers for so many years, I had to work to break the pre-rinse habit. The dishes are just as clean without pre-rinsing. If you haven't tried it, you should experiment, just to see if it works.
  • I also have pretty much stopped hand-washing dishes. The dishwasher does it better and more efficiently than I do. I had a bad habit of letting the tap run while scrubbing dishes, so that's a big savings right there. We also make sure the dishwasher is super full before we run it to maximize each wash cycle.
  • We've implemented the "if it's yellow let it mellow..." protocol. The kids thought I was whacked when I first suggested they "conserve flushes" (after nagging them for years to flush after each use). But it's coming together. Today I mentioned to my daughter that each flush is like two gallons of milk. She's a big milk drinker and that size comparision really got her attention.
  • This one has been hard to break consistently, but we now turn the water off when we wash our hands and brush our teeth. We're doing our best. The kids especially love to play in the water at the sink.

So how's it going for you? What things have you done to reduce in home water use? Did you order your free water conservation kit? Any helpful kid tips you can share?

After the Flush: our new favorite story

I recently picked up an educational brochure called "after the flush" put out by the Portland Environmental Services group. I have an enquiring mind. I like to know these things.

Much to my suprise, my kids wanted to read it too. It's since become one of our favorite bedtime stories. I thought about dumbing down the lingo a bit, but decided not to. So now we read about "primary clarifiers," "biosolids" and "chlorination" on a daily basis. The little one will ask, "what's sludge, mama?"

What I learned (besides the fact that a lot happens after the flush...): kids are never too young to learn about big topics involving big words. And that -- when they can understand why -- they can be your best advocates in changing habits and reducing home water usage.
