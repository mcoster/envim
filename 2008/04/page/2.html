Getting to the heart of why a community is environmentally focused

I've been pondering recently over the question of why Portand as a city, and Oregon as a state are so green. We have readers from all over the place who "visit" EnviroMom.com, and a few have jokingly accused us of working for the gov, trying to lure new people to move here. Really, we don't. It's a natural, organic kind of love we have for where we live. End of story. But it does make me wonder how Oregon and Portland got to be so environmentally focused, and how our communities have continued to grown with an emphasis on sustainability. Along comes yesterday's lovely Sunday Oregonian (which is printed on recycled newsprint), with a nice feature on how we've compiled such a green resume. It brings up some good points about how Oregon was the first state to enact a bottle bill, things I wasn't here for, or really thinking about.

I imagine our proximity to the Pacific Ocean helped Oregon become such a leader in recycling. All those container ships coming to the West Coast from the Far East, then going back home empty, unless of course your vibrant plastics recyclers fill them up with plastics to be sorted and recycled in distant lands. That explains how some recyclers such as Far West Fibers can make ends meet. Then there's the story of Dari & Allen Jongsma, founders of Agri-Plas. Amazingly, they created recycling markets that didn't exist before, and process and sell domestically. It's a great green story out of Brooks, Oregon.

Yet, recycling is just one aspect of sustainability. There are myriad things that make a community green-minded. What do you think is at the heart of your community's desire and determination to go green? Tell us where you're from and what makes your community enviro-friendly. It's like a love letter to your town on Earth Day. Well... day before Earth Day. Close enough. Smooooooch!

The ethical dilemma of donating 'toxic' items

Plastic sippy cups. Teflon-coated pans. Chemical-laden mattresses. You may have decided that you no longer want to expose your family to these products, but how do you justify donating them for other people to use? My friend Christy and I had this discussion the other day, and it's something that I've struggled with anytime I transition our household to an eco-friendly alternative. Common household items such as these are desperately needed by so many people. If the choice is between no cooking pot or a Teflon-coated pot, the answer is obvious. (I doubt that any social service non-profit receives donations of stainless steel or cast iron cookware very often, if ever.) I assume the vast majority of Americans don't believe there is anything toxic about these products anyway. Plus, it's not like tossing them in a landfill is the answer.

Do you ever struggle with this issue? Donating items for reuse is the environmentally and socially responsible thing to do (I think), but it's hard to feel good about donating items that you believe are unhealthy. Any insight to share (or are we just spending too much time thinking about a no-brainer)?

Recommendations on BPA free baby bottles that work

Liz is struggling to find baby bottles that don't have BPA, and that work! With some of the ones she's tried the nipples collapse and baby gets too much gas. Here's her dilemma:

I was wondering if you knew of green BPA, phthalates and adipates free bottles that have good nipples that don’t collapse. I’m trying different ones out and they are too narrow, or the nipples collapse and my daughter gets a lot of air. All the BPA free ones claim to not have collapsing nipples, but I’m not finding that to be especially true… I’ve tried Evenflo BPA-free glass and plastic bottles. I’ve researched Dr. Brown’s and Gerber and Bornfree, but the comments are contradictory. Some people are saying that they collapse and are terrible and some say they’re perfect. If it’s more about the nipple than the bottle, I’m fine with purchasing nipples separate. Your thoughts and personal or professional recommendations would be most welcome.

Our family has not used baby bottles for years. Sadly, we used Avent. I cringe to think of it now. I knew nothing about BPA then, and (major cringe) we heated milk in the Avent bottles regularly.

Anyone out there using BPA-free baby bottles that they would recommend to Liz? If so, do you use different nipples? What's your magic combo?

Does your family think biking and walking is unhealthy, too?

As a proud and happy director of Safe Routes to School for the Bicycle Transportation Alliance I am thrilled to add to my plate the fun hobby of writing for Enviromom. I'm the mama of two precious daughters, Ellie and Libby (in the photo at left), and a rabid bike commuter and recreational rider. Ellie walks to school every day, Libby follows me to preschool in a trailer, and we take over our streets on the weekends to 'bike bus' our way through errands, soccer games and fun trips to the park. That's my home life and I'll have plenty to say - and ask! - about bicycling as a family.

But at work this spring I've been collecting surveys for our Safer Routes to School program that we manage for the City of Portland and the data are pointing toward very fascinating and exciting results. Of course, I can't yet comment on those results since they're not finalized (and therefore not public) but as soon as I can, I'll let you know!

Cliffhanger aside, with the surveys we also gathered parent comments that are just as fascinating. Comments such as, "He loves to bike and its faster" or "I love that you have a program for teaching kids bike safety rules - my son said 'I can't wait for 3rd grade so I can ride my bike'"  are so encouraging!

But one comment in particular strikes me and I'd like to hear more from you. We often find that educating parents on the reality of safety and health concerns is our biggest barrier. How do you politely yet effectively educate adults who have made up their minds about what is best for their children? A parent wrote:

"I will not permit my child to walk or bike to school. It is unsafe and considering environmental toxins such as vehicle exhaust it is not particularly healthy."

What do you have to say? And remember, what we do know is that you catch more flies with honey...

City Repair Project's Earth Day at Overlook Park, 10am-10pm: This is the big Earth Day celebration in Portland (oh let's hope it doesn't snow). Dance of the Dinosaur is the theme, with loads of music and entertainment; the Kid's Village featuring jugglers, marimbas, parades, games and activities; and eco-friendly business booths. FREE. 3704 N. Interstate.

KidTip: Teaching reuse via recycled art

Yesterday my 6-year-old marched off to school toting a very large paper grocery bag filled with recyclables. No, I was not making her run errands for me. It was for a school project. With Earth Day right around the corner, her kindergarten class was soliciting recyclables to use for their big Earth Day art project. Is your school doing this too? You've probably seen this kind of art. You've probably made some of it. If you're looking for ideas, Savvy Source has a huge list of craft projects made from reuse items.

While it's unrealistic to expect our young'uns will create true, museum-worthy masterpieces working with toilet paper rolls, bottle caps, polystyrene trays and egg cartons, I like the underlying message that these art projects teach: things can be reused. Even if they are created for one-time use: the egg carton, the bottle cap, the toilet paper roll, and yes, the foam tray. These "recycled art" projects plant the idea that using a big empty yogurt container on the beach as a sandcastle form is all you really need -- instead of buying a special plastic sand castle building bucket. Aside from a little text on the yogurt container, they're really the same thing: cheap plastic buckets. Reuse where you can. Make "precycle" the first step in any new purchase consideration. If your kids can learn this from a young age, maybe "retail therapy" will die a premature death.

If you're not in the mood for arts and crafts, or you're looking for other kid-sized projects to celebrate Earth Day, Savvy Source also lists a few eco-friendly activities for kids, along the lines of our KidTips.

Now go off and make your egg-carton caterpillar, or hug a tree, for crying out loud! It's almost Earth Day.
