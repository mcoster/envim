How to pack a waste-free lunch box

Lisa S. emailed the following question:

I know you probably have already discussed this and its not the time of year to be thinking about it... but wondering if you could help me with packing a waste-free lunch box. I am sending my kids (3 and 5) to a summer camp with a your typical size fabric lunch pack, but having trouble finding reusable containers to fit in side. I would like to fit a sandwich, crackers, cut-up fruit and a drink. and would like to have everything separated. I have quickly looked a couple places, but found the containers too big or too small or wouldn't fit all the containers at once. I would like to keep their lunch packs, but if I need to re-evaluate their size I could. any suggestions would be great. thanks!

So, while packing my kids' camp lunches today, I took some pictures, trying to approximate the things Lisa would like to pack. My kids each have a small Sigg water bottle and a lunch bag. A sandwich is the norm, but I also will pack pasta from time to time. Usually that goes into a 9.5 oz container (rectangle with blue top). I do use zip bags for small things like crackers or cookies, and the kids bring them home, and we wash and reuse until they are worn out. We have some tiny tupperware containers that are great for nuts or hummus/salad dressing (approximately 2 oz.). I grabbed an old sour cream container as a packing option, say for some sliced fruit. The other white top plastic container is a little bigger, but also would work for fruit, or yogurt and berries (which is what my older daughter wanted today). Then a cloth napkin, and silverware (as needed) to top it off.

It fits, but with not a lot of wiggle room. Here's how it looks all squished into the bag:

I hope this helps Lisa, and anyone else trying to figure out how to pack a waste-free lunch box. If anyone wants to send us pictures with different approaches to the waste-free lunch, please do, and we'll be happy to post them.

Strawberry freezer jam (and what about pectin?)

Last week I went to Sauvie Island with a group of parents and kids to pick the delectable Oregon strawberry. It was still a little early, but I managed to harvest ten pounds. I'll be heading back out for one more really big pick because my hope is to make so much strawberry freezer jam that we won't need to buy any jam for the rest of the year. (Note to self: buy a bigger freezer ASAP.)

I'd never made any kind of jam before, and I really wanted to use as little sugar as possible. I'd seen a few recipes that called for four cups of sugar for every four cups of berries. Crikey! Happily, the no-cook Ball brand pectin I bought called for only one-and-a-half cups of sugar. So, I washed and hulled the berries, ran them through the food processor and measured out four cups of puree. I mixed the package of powdered pectin and sugar together and then mixed them in with the berries. I let the whole thing rest for about 30 minutes, et voila, strawberry jam. Delicious strawberry jam. Rub-it-all-over-your-body-and-lick-it-off strawberry jam. It was one of the easiest, most gratifying things I've ever made. I poured some of the jam into small glass jars with plastic lids and some into plastic containers and popped them into the freezer. Freezer jam will keep in the freezer for a year and in the fridge for three weeks. Have you tried making freezer jam yet? You must!

So what about pectin? I never really gave it any thought until I innocently looked at the ingredients: dextrose, sodium citrate, potassium sorbate...aren't these are additives and preservatives that should be avoided in general? Do they make a natural pectin (after all, pectin is a natural substance derived from plants)?

Time management and the pioneer woman

Holy cow. All I can say is it's a good thing pioneer women didn't have blogs. Or email. If they did, they'd never manage to can all the strawberries while they're in season. Or else they'd forget about doing the laundry. Or else they'd just never blog. Or reply to emails.

Because let me tell you, I'm feeling downright stressed out trying to dehydrate, make jam and freeze the mere 23 pounds of strawberries my husband and I picked in Hood River on Saturday morning. Then there's the laundry that needs to be hung out. The granola that needs to be made. The other three square meals that need to be made for this hungry family. Don't even get me started about the housework that needs to be done. Ridiculous.

How did those pioneer women do it? They couldn't exactly get a pizza delivery if they got in a time crunch. Of course, it's quite possible that the kids were more helpful. I don't want to over-romanticize an era I know nothing about (unless you count my devotion to the Little House on the Prairie TV series). But I doubt the kids were all: "Mom, she has all the Littlest Petshops and I don't!"

As I take on more eco-responsibility at home: hanging out the laundry to dry in the sun, making more food from whole foods (vs. buying processed), walking more/driving less... the time crunch is undeniable. I also wonder if I'm really being more efficient with this multi-tasking I do, or if I should just compartmentalize: laundry day; market day; canning day, etc. I also think that at some point, I may need to enlist the help of my children with certain tasks. My 6-year-old can be amazingly helpful, and she enjoys helping. Progress is greatly slowed down with the million and one questions that come up. But truly, she can be a joy to work with. And I like that she's willing to help and learn. My three-year-old has the attention span of a gnat and really is just there to start arguments: "It's my turn to pour in the oats! I want the clothespin with the smiley face!"

How do you do it? Any time management tips for the pioneer woman in the digital age?

101 re-uses for: old clothes

We frequently get asked what to do with old clothes: socks, jeans, sweaters, mittens, intimates, shoes, t-shirts, you-name-it.

We've patched the knees of our kids' worn out pants. We've darned the holes in our socks. Hope, our Eastside GreenGroup leader, made a darling tote bag from an old pair of jeans. We have one friend of a GreenGroup friend who makes rugs out of old wool socks. We've made as many sock puppets as our sock-puppet theater can handle. We've talked about local clothing re-use organizations that we particularly like. We've made t-shirt napkins. Yet, our rag bags runneth over.

What do you do with the rest of your old clothes (the clothes that are too beat up to hand down)? Surely, some creative EnviroMoms out there have some fabulous re-use ideas for old clothes.... Anyone, anyone?

Potatoes in the compost: yea or nay?

The other day I noticed a vibrant, leafy stem growing out of a slat in my compost bin. Upon further investigation I discovered long, tuber-like tentacles snaking through my compost, which were ultimately attached to a months-old potato at the very bottom of the bin. This can't be good. If my food scraps are living and breathing in the compost bin and not decomposing, wouldn't that affect the magic balance of carbon and nitrogen that I'm trying to achieve in order to turn the scraps into compost? As I was digging around I noticed that I'm not getting as much dark, crumbly compost at the bottom as I thought I was. Are potatoes the evil villain?

KidTip: Don't be so quick to put those dirty duds in the hamper

I really let my kids get away with murder. They don't make their beds. Ever. I do give them the evil-eye every time they kick their shoes and socks off in random places all around the house -- which of course leads to mismatched shoes and much frustration trying to find a matching pair when getting dressed. It isn't a big problem with socks, however. There seems to be this trend in my 6-year-old daughter's school of wearing mis-matched socks. Maybe like Michael Jackson's one sequined glove, but hopefully the mismatched socks thing won't lead to other weird Michael Jackson-esque habits. And although it has the potential to drive me crazy on a bad day, the kids always take off their clothes at the end of the day leaving dirty t-shirts, skirts, jeans, underwear, etc. in little heaps mostly in their room, sometimes trailing into the bathroom, or often the hall. You know how you are supposed to pick your battles as a parent? I guess I've never chosen to die on the hill of dirty laundry piles, mismatched shoes and unmade beds.

Today being the first day of summer break, we didn't have to rush to get out the door. So I took a little time to sort through the piles of clothing in their room. And I discovered a bright side to this little dirty clothing problem. It's that I get a second chance to remember to spot treat their clothing before throwing it into the hamper (and if the spots are taken care of, the clothing stands a chance of getting handed down to another person). I also get a second chance to decide if it's even clean enough for a second wearing, or third, or fourth. Thankfully, my Thing 1 and Thing 2 are young enough to not get very stinky. Sometimes they even manage to make it through the day without their art project or meal ending up on their clothing. Lastly, we are not weirded out or driven to wash everything after just one wearing. There are people who can't deal with that.

Evidently, it's one of those cases of seeing the glass (or hamper) half empty or half full. If you can stand the piles of clothing that are somewhere on the continuum of being clean and dirty, you'll save a lot of water, laundry detergent and perhaps some parental grief in just letting the kids throw it on the floor. It mostly works for me.

Battling global warming with viral social change

Record heat on the East Coast. Flooding in the Midwest. Snow on the West Coast. It's June, folks. Methinks that something is in the air. Something noxious that is altering the climate. Are you with me?

My aunt and uncle's home in Franklin, Indiana had five feet of water in it on Saturday. Everything is ruined. The photos of flooding in central Indiana, where I grew up, are devastating. I am pissed. Scientists have long said that global warming will result in extreme, destructive weather. So instead of waiting around for the earthquake to tumble my house down the hill, I pledge to do the following:

  • Follow Cheap-o-Dad's example and fill my car's tank up with gas once in the next eight weeks and rely on my bike, feet and public transportation when I need to get somewhere.
  • Finally install that clothesline!
  • Rely on the farmer's market for 75% of our food this summer.

It doesn't sound like much. It's really not going to affect the climate. But what if we all did it? Michael Pollan wrote a powerful essay titled Why Bother? that spoke to the reasons we all need to do our best even though others may be canceling out our efforts. It's called viral social change, and here's what he says about it:

If you do bother, you will set an example for other people. If enough other people bother, each one influencing yet another in a chain reaction of behavioral change, markets for all manner of green products and alternative technologies will prosper and expand. (Just look at the market for hybrid cars.) Consciousness will be raised, perhaps even changed: new moral imperatives and new taboos might take root in the culture. Driving an S.U.V. or eating a 24-ounce steak or illuminating your McMansion like an airport runway at night might come to be regarded as outrages to human conscience. Not having things might become cooler than having them. And those who did change the way they live would acquire the moral standing to demand changes in behavior from others â€” from other people, other corporations, even other countries.

I want this, and I know you want it, too. Are you ready to make some changes and release the virus?

Car maintenance, fuel efficiency and other required activities of being a grown-up

You'll find me today blogging from a Les Schwab tire center in SW Portland. While typing that first sentence I was invited to join their wireless network. Man, the wireless internet is cool. How did we ever live without it? You can even use free wi-fi at Burgerville these days (you want some fries with that free wi-fi?)

But, lo, I am not here just for the free wi-fi. I am here for new tires. Apparently with these car things, you need to rotate the tires at regular intervals as well as change the oil and fill the gas tank. Is it any wonder that we have to get rid of my husband's car? This is far too much responsibility for us, given that we are also tasked with raising two fairly needy children, which involves feeding, clothing, basking them with unconditional love, etc. And, we also have a house! Which you might recall isn't high on my list of maintaining as far as cleanliness goes given the dust-bunnies in the bathtub... 

Back to the car thing. There's all these maintenance details we can keep on top of to improve fuel efficiency, and in these days of 4-plus freaking dollars a gallon -- fuel efficiency should be high on any driver's list:

  • Proper air pressure in tires (as much as I avoid doing anything car related, putting air in the tires is actually not too hard. If I can do it, anyone can...)
  • Rotation of tires on a regular basis (oops -- apparently driving 20,000 miles before rotating does not count as regular.)
  • Change your filters (these are the things they show me all disgusting and nasty every time I get my oil changed.)
  • Change your oil (OK. Well at least I get points for this. I do change my oil.)
  • Don't be speed racer (Points here too. I'm not a speed demon.)

Others have written with more authority on these car maintenance/fuel efficiency things. I won't pretend to have some grease monkey alter-ego. When the very nice Les Schwab man asked me what kind of tires I was looking for today, I'm like "round?"

I do have high hopes of becoming a one-car family though. At least we'll have only one car to try and remember to rotate the tires. And there will be two of us to blame if we forget for 20,000 miles next time... And with the responsibility of only one car, maybe we'll actually have time to work on our house a little bit.
