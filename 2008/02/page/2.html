Free car seat recycling this Saturday

There is no easy way to get rid of an old car seat. Most non-profits won't take them, because if a seat has ever been in an accident then it is no longer safe to use and is a liability. Plus, car seats are not supposed to be used longer than six years since over time the plastic actually breaks down and compromises the safety of the seat. If you've been holding on to an old car seat, then fear not, you can get rid of it this weekend!

Legacy Emanuel Children's Hospital is sponsoring a free "drive-through" child passenger safety seat recycling event:

When: Saturday, February 23, 2008, 10am-Noon

Where: Legacy Emanuel Hospital campus, in the parking lot at the corner of N. Knott and N. Vancouver Avenues

What: People who have outdated car seats are invited to drop the seats off for recycling, free of charge.

No registration is necessary. Call Legacy Referral Service, 503-335-3500, for more information.

KOIN-TV is a co-sponsor of this event and they have a special report on their Web site about the dangers of expired car seats. Click on the link for Special Report: Expired Car Seat Dangers.

Using it up: Joseph's little overcoat

Have you heard this old saying: "Use it up, wear it out, make it do, or do without"? It was new to me when I took the Master Recycler class last spring. I guess that goes to show how far our society has gone the wrong direction, in that most modern marketing stimulates impulse buying of unnecessary things.

During a playdate last week, our friends introduced me and my 3-year-old to a kid's book that really drives this message home. It's called Joseph Had a Little Overcoat. The story goes like this: Joseph has this great old coat, but he wears it out, so he cuts it down and makes a small jacket out of it. Then he wears that out, and turns it into a vest. After that it become increasingly smaller: next as a scarf, then a hanky and finally a button. Which sadly, in the end, he loses. My 3-year-old was obsessed with this point. After all that, he loses the button???? So he decides to write a book about it, thus all is not lost. It is based on a Yiddish song, and the library copy we saw had the song included on a cassette sung in both English and Yiddish. Really cute, and great message. My daughter has been talking about it for days.

We love stories that help our kids be green and learn to use things well and wisely. What are some of your favorites?

Birthday party durables?

Christy is hosting a birthday party but doesn't have enough durable goods to go around:

OK. We have my son's 5th birthday party coming up and we are in need of party durables (plates, utensils, napkins) for 30 people. I am willing to purchase them if I have to and then share them in turn with other friends for their parties. Any thoughts on a good place to purchase them? Is there anyone renting out party durables, especially for kid's parties? Good business idea, I think. Maybe not financially profitable but environmentally profitable. Have either of you looked into this? I'd be curious to know.

So here are my first thoughts...  You can rent dishes, utensils and linens from Barbur Rentals. Their stuff is probably more geared to adult parties and weddings (white china plates, wine glasses and actual linens), but it's worth looking into if they have anything for the younger party set.

If you buy, I'd suggest IKEA. They have those colorful, affordable, non-breakable kid plates and cups that come in sets of 5 or 6. Their utensils are also very reasonable, and all stainless steel.

If you don't want to buy that many place-settings, you could either borrow from friends and neighbors (I'm one who's willing to loan you my stash of kid plates). Or you can position the party like a picnic and suggest your guests bring their own place-settings. We did this when we went to the Muddy Boots festival last summer, and it was kind of fun!

Composting in the cold depths of winter

Thank you Erin for giving me the great article about composting in winter. Erin passed me her copy of Organic Gardening magazine (Feb-Mar 2008 issue) at our last GreenGroup meeting. I can't seem to link to the exact article (I think they must not put all of their article online). But I did find one about keeping your compost pile from freezing if you'd like to check that one out. I will also summarize a few of the points that I learned from reading this great piece:

  • Your microbes are still busily composting even if the temps are cold. It's just slowed down based on how cold the pile gets.
  • Be very vigilant about mixing/layering your browns and greens. This will help insulate and keep the heat hot enough to keep composting at a quicker pace. They noted that manure from chickens and rabbits are loaded with "heat-generating nitrogen" so add some of that, if you can. We recently had an offer of free organic chicken poop on our site, so you could probably find some no matter where you are.
  • Chop and shred your browns and greens before adding them to your pile. The smaller the pieces, the faster they will compost.
  • Winter wind and low humidity can dry out your compost. Keep it damp to keep those microbes alive.
  • In warm weather, turning gives the pile fresh air and oxygen. In winter, don't turn the pile. The layers of brown and green act like blankets to keep the heat in. Save turning for spring and summer.
  • Add some heat either by putting your pile in a sunny spot, covering with snow which will act like a blanket, cover with a tarp, pack with straw bales or dig it into a trench so the warmth from the ground will keep the heat high.

We've had a number of questions from readers who were wondering how to compost in freezing temps, so I hope this info helps. Here in Portland, it doesn't freeze very hard or often, so it's not our area of expertise. But I did find this article very interesting, and I hope it will help others who compost through very cold winters.

EnviroMom Kid's Clothing Swap, March 8 @ Milagros

It's back! We're hosting another EnviroMom Kid's Clothing Swap, so start cleaning out your closets! Clothing swaps are an excellent, eco-friendly way to keep clothing circulating through the community and extending their usefulness and lifetime. Here are the details:

WHEN?    Saturday, March 8, 11am - Noon

WHERE?  Milagros Boutique, 5433 NE 30th, Portland, 503-493-4141

WHAT?    Bring gently-used items with no stains, rips or tears, missing buttons or broken zippers in sizes newborn up to 5T. Acceptable items include:

  • clothing
  • socks and tights
  • shoes
  • coats, hats, sunglasses and other accessories
  • cloth diapers and diaper covers
  • maternity clothing and nursing bras

Please no toys, books or games -- just items to wear. It helps to sort your clothing beforehand (so you have more time to shop!) in these five categories:

  • newborn to 12 months
  • 12-24 months
  • 2T/3T
  • 4T/5T
  • maternity

There are no limits to how much to bring or take, we just ask that you take what you need. Please bring a bag to carry home all of your new-to-you finds! Any leftover items will be donated to Portland Impact, a non-profit organization devoted to helping Portland-area residents alleviate the effects of poverty.

Have any friends who are expecting their first baby and might not have anything to swap yet? We always have a ton of newborn to 24 month clothes left over, so please tell your pregnant friends to come check it out!

Milagros is a kid-friendly shop, and children are more than welcome to attend. Make sure to stick around and browse their collection!

Questions? Send us an email at enviromompdx@gmail.com. See you there!

No more wire hangers!

KK has an abundance of wire hangers. Here's the scoop:

Any ideas of what to do with a bunch of wire coat hangers?  I'm at the very last stage of pregnancy, where none of my clothes fit anymore and I've got about 5 things hanging in my closet, so I'm using this as an opportunity to donate or repurpose clothes I didn't wear anymore anyway.  But now I've got a ridiculous amount of hangers left over, and I'd rather just have the plastic ones (bought second hand of course!) I can't seem to find any info on recycling them, and I hate to donate them along with the clothes because I doubt they'll get bought and will probably just end up in the landfill that way.   I've found some crafty ideas for using them, but honestly, I can only make one pair of fairy wings and then I've got about 99 coat hangers left over.  Any thoughts? Thanks!!!

Here's what I was thinking. If they are the kind of wire hangers you get from a dry cleaner, why don't you see if the dry cleaners in your area will take them back and reuse them? It's always better to try to reuse first vs. recycle. If you have no luck with the cleaners, you could also try posting them on craigslist.org or freecycle.org as I'll bet there is someone out there who could use them for a rummage sale or to stock a thrift store. Worst case, they can probably be recycled -- scrap metal is a hot recycling market right now, at least where we live in Portland. I don't believe you mentioned where you live, so find out if you can recycle them as a last resort.

Do you have any other ideas for KK on what to do with her wire hangers?

Happy (reused) Valentine's Day!

As is our tradition, the kids and I spent yesterday making Valentine's cards, because it would be a cold day in hell if we actually made them any earlier than the day before. Over at ReadySetMom we've talked about what to do with all that preschool artwork, and some good suggestions were made. Last year I diligently went through stacks and stacks of artwork, keeping some, recycling some and saving some in a large portfolio for reuse. I wasn't really sure what we'd reuse it for, but we've actually dug into that portfolio several times for various projects (like wrapping small presents). The latest one? Making Valentine cards.

My three-year old picked out some 'abstract' paintings he'd done and we cut hearts out and simply wrote "Happy Valentine's Day" on the back. (You don't have to get too elaborate when you're three.) My daughter preferred to dig through our stash of colorful cardstock and make actual cards. Then we cut out hearts from the old artwork, glued them to the front, and she did all the writing. Again, very basic, but she really loved them, which is all that matters.

We didn't have to leave the house to make these Valentines, we used what we already had, and we spent a fun afternoon around the kitchen table. Take that, Hallmark!

SW GreenGroup February meeting notes

Our SW GreenGroup meetings are always abuzz with tons of good info to share. Invariably, I miss some of it, but let me at least try to capture most of what we discussed:

  • Phil, a neighbor of Betty's, came to our meeting for the first time. She told us that she likes to use baking soda and lemon juice to clean a bathtub. All of us said we'd had no luck finding a non-toxic cleaner for a toilet bowl ring. Any ideas out there?
  • Christy said she has really liked the air filter she has been using by EcoQuest, which has helped her and her son's allergies greatly. Jen also mentioned the Recuperator by Ultimate Air and said that worked well for her.
  • We discussed and brainstormed on Heather's mold problem after a roofer suggested she use chlorine bleach.
  • Two in our group are loving the Diva Cup.
  • We talked about stainless steel sip cups, and chickens (two subjects we seem to talk about every time we are together). From our most experienced Chicken Mom, Jen, Rhode Island reds make great backyard chickens, but stay away from the silkies and bantams.
  • It was said that mylar balloons can be refilled with helium for reuse, or also if you cut off the top as gift bags.
  • Christy mentioned she has a backyard experiment of biodegradeable items. I encouraged her to take some pictures and share that with us.
  • We talked about trying to find ways to avoid butcher paper waste from meat wrapping. After the meeting Erin mentioned the FoodSaver system, but none of us has experience with is. Has anyone used this?
  • The new styrofoam recycler will take styrofoam meat trays.
  • I was sad to hear that metal cans of food, such as for beans, all have a BPA lining. We were unsure if the aseptic boxes do or not.
  • Phil mentioned that a drop of rosemary oil behind a child's ears works as a lice repellent.

That's all I have folks. Did I miss anything? See you next month!
