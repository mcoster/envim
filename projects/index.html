Good 4 Girls: reuseable cloth menstrual pads for developing nations

I love this. The Good 4 Girls initiative collects donations of reusable cloth menstrual pads -- either hand-sewn or commercially sewn. Goods 4 Girls is working with aid organizations in Kenya and Uganda to distribute the pads to girls in need. Why, you ask? Because in developing nations, they don't have tampons and maxi pads as cheaply and readily available from vending machines in public restrooms as we do. So when they get their periods, they often have to miss school, or worse, they're trying to change-out the rags or wads of newspaper they are using behind a shrub. I cannot imagine. We have Seattle's own Crunchy Chicken to thank for this great idea, who's clearly put a lot of thought into it (please read her post for more information and to get involved).

Science project: sugar ant annihilation

We passed up on our kindergartner's first shot at a science fair project. Science fair is tomorrow night. I feared I didn't have the time to devote to it (I feel we're just treading water trying to get her homework done nightly). But we do plan to go visit the science fair, and hopefully be inspired for next year.

In the meantime, we took on a little science fair project here at home. With the return of sunny, springlike weather in Portland, my little ant problem has resurfaced. We mixed up Metro's recommended ant baits, with little success:

"Blend 1/4 cup confectioner's sugar and 1 tablespoon of borax. Sprinkle the mixture in ant traffic areas, but not where children or pets might ingest it. This is not enough borax to kill the worker ants immediately--they take the mix back to the nest, where it will eventually eradicate the whole colony."

What happened is I'd sprinkle some in their path, and they'd sniff or taste or whatever it is they do, then keep on walkin'. Even though they did not really acquire a taste for the stuff last spring, I was hesitant to just throw it out, thus we still had some of the powdered sugar/borax mix leftover in a small container. So this year we decided to conduct an ant bait taste test. In past years, I'd had some success with a syrupy-borax over the counter product, but had not been able to find it again. So this is what we made:

  • A mix of the sugar/borax powder with water, to turn it into syrup.
  • A mix of the sugar/borax powder mixed with some artificially-flavored bubble gum fluoride rinse I'd once bought for my kids when we were going through a tooth-brushing boycott (but never really used -- have you noticed I have a hard time getting rid of things?).
  • And finally, a mix of the sugar/borax with just a smidgen of the Torani Vanilla Syrup my husband takes in his coffee every morning (sorry honey, we only used a tiny, tiny bit).

It turns out the ants have a fairly refined palate. They thumbed their noses at the watered down stuff, and the bubble-gum pink. But they just lapped up the Torani syrup mix. For days the ants came out in full platoons. Bringing it dutifully back to the nest. And now they are gone. Nothing remains but the carcasses of a few fallen ant soldiers.

Science fair project grade: B+. Now, I really did have my kids help mix it. And they also spooned it onto little pieces of paper, which we left by the ant trails on the back of the kitchen counter. Lots of fun, and I do think there really may be a science project in this for us next year...

Have the ants come marching into your house? How are you fighting them?

Eco-healthy childcare: Wanna go green?

Heather and I are not the only parents at our preschool who want the school to be as green as possible. So this year's preschool board is asking an "old school" (it was founded in 1958) to learn some new tricks. Since we are a cooperative, the first thing we needed to do was establish a "committee" so that there would be an  "Eco-Awareness Parent" at the school.

Lucky for us, there's guidance available. The Oregon Environmental Council has established criteria to certify schools and childcare centers as "Eco-Healthy." It's something we're striving for, and hope to achieve in the next year. They have a checklist of 25 criteria to qualify as Eco-Healthy.  And a list of providers that are already meeting the criteria.

Does your child attend a qualified Eco-Healthy Childcare facility? Do you have any lessons to share about shepherding your school through the process? If so, we'd love to hear from you!

Are you reaping Clean River Rewards?

My friend Amy recently went to a presentation about Clean River Rewards. I'd been under the false impression that the rewards only went to certain areas on the eastside of town. I'm sharing what we discussed, in the hopes it'll motivate more of us to sign up for Clean River Rewards:

"The rebates are citywide. Commissioner Sam Adams had a special session at the Multnomah Arts Center in which he said our part of town has special challenges in containing runoff because of our hilly terrain, and for that reason the city is going to be a little more generous in allowing rebates in SW.

It doesn't take much to meet the criteria for a rebate, especially for newer construction like ours. For example, we have a French drain, which we didn't even put in -- the builders did because our lot slopes from north to south -- and that counts as an anti-runoff action. Another one is disconnecting downspouts, which I didn't understand at first, but it turns out to be just having your gutter downspout disperse rainwater into your lawn rather than into the sewer system. I have to go outside and check, but I'm pretty sure our downspouts came that way.

If I recall correctly, even planting large trees can count. Adams had a PowerPoint that showed some pretty ingenious DIY methods of containing runoff, including digging a subtle depression into your lawn."

Are you signed up for Clean River Rewards? What changes did you make to qualify?

Encourage Costco and other bulk warehouses to sell more post-consumer recycled paper products

Earlier this week, I wrote a letter along these lines to Costco:

Dear Costco,

I have been a Costco member for several years, and really enjoy buying in bulk. I shop with the environment in mind, and I appreciate that there is less packaging when you buy in bulk. On my most recent Costco trip this week, I was hoping to find post-consumer recycled content toilet paper, such as Seventh Generation or Green Forests. It has come to my attention that paper production is highly energy intensive, especially when made from virgin materials; and that making recycled content paper uses less energy than from wood pulp.

But sadly, I found no recycled content toilet paper for sale in the Costco warehouse. I like the convenience of buying a large stock of toilet paper and not having to worry about it for many months. I appreciate and purchase your recycled content office supplies (such as printing paper, file folders, etc.), and your growing selection of organic food. If you begin to sell post-consumer recycled content toilet paper, I promise that I will buy it. And I will encourage all of my friends to do so as well. But, for now, I will have to stick to buying post-consumer recycled content toilet paper at the grocery store in those tiny, inconvenient 4-roll packs. Because buying recycled is that important to me.

If this is important to you, I suggest you let Costco know what you think and send them an email too. Or where ever it is that you bulk shop. They really need to hear from us.

Greenbuilding, fairy-style

A couple years ago during a summertime park playdate, a then-5-year old taught my then-3-year old daughter about fairy houses. Fairy houses, as told by author Tracy Kane in the book by the same name, are tiny shelters built in natural settings out of natural materials that are found on the ground. The story goes that if you build it, the fairies will come. The book, which we finally read last week, offers tips for building these little structures, which can be as primitive or elaborate as your child wants. The idea is to encourage exploration and respect of nature: the fairies won't come to any houses if leaves or branches have been pulled off trees in order to make it. It's also a great exercise in creativity and problem-solving: how can we set up these little twigs to support a roof? Can we make a hammock out of a fallen leaf and two twigs?

So for the past two summers, we've built numerous fairy houses around our yard. For the most part they've been very simple and easily toppled in a light wind. But it's been great to watch my daughter, and now my son, scavenging for twigs, getting their fingers dirty and imagining how delighted the fairies will be when they find their new house. Check out the fairy house Web site and see what other kids are building (some of them are pretty amazing). Building fairy houses is a great substitute for watching Bob the Builder on a sweet, summer day.

Project: Ridding the deck and patio of winter grime

I feel fairly confident that warmer weather and al fresco dining are just around the corner, so over the weekend I decided to get the deck and patio cleaned up. The deck is cedar, stained with a durable paint/stain blend so it doesn't need to be treated every year, and the patio is stamped concrete. Typically my husband would rent a pressure washer to take care of the slick layer of grime and goopiness that winter brings, but this year I paused and asked, "What would Ed Begley, Jr. do?" Not really. But I did want to avoid using the huge amount of water wasted by pressure washers.

First I tried just sweeping it off with the push broom to no avail. Then I wet down the whole deck with a hose and used my quivering upper body muscles to vigorously sweep/scrub the wood with the push broom. The grime came off so easily, and when I'd swept the whole deck I rinsed it all off again and it looked good as new. I even got down on my hands and knees with a sponge to get the corners and under the railing. It worked just as well on the concrete. It also took a lot less time to do it this way because with the pressure washer you just hit little patches at a time, but with the broom I was able to clean large swaths with one fell swoop. A little more elbow grease, yes, but ultimately worth it.

Have you discovered any green spring cleaning tricks lately?

Baby step: change your garbage cans to recycle bins

Is your house like mine, with a garbage can in every room? Trash night requires that you schlepp around the house, empty the kitchen garbage, the  bathroom cans, the diaper pails, the cans in all the bedrooms, under desks, etc. We have a few mixed paper recycling boxes in our home offices, but other than that, we've actually made it hard to recycle in our house. The recycling station is in our basement. So if you've got a shampoo bottle or a magazine that rightly belongs in the recycle bin, it's much easier to find a garbage can and toss it, rather than trekking down two flights of stairs to the basement.

The discussion on the "Recycle at Work" program in our Master Recycler class last night gave me a good idea that would work in our home (perhaps yours too?). It's one of our famous "baby steps." Try this: Don't have a garbage can in every room. Change some of them to "recycle only" cans. Changes like this require a bit of training on the part of your co-workers and/or family members. It's not a good idea to just take away all the garbage cans, and not explain why. But it is a good idea to make it less convenient to throw trash away, and more convenient to recycle. I think with a short family meeting I can get my green family to buy into this new system. I'll ask the kids to draw pictures for the newly knighted recycle bins, so that they take ownership in this project. I'll leave a few garbage cans, but transform most of them recycle bins.

I will also note that downsizing our curbside trash pickup to monthly from weekly has amped up my resolve to get all the recyclables in the right place. Take a look at what you're currently using. See if you're really filling your trash can each time. Talk to your hauler and find out the next lower size, and see if that change would motivate you to reduce/reuse/recycle more.

A recycling center that works for the whole family

About a year ago I started realizing that a lot of plastics can be recycled - more than what you put out curbside or can take to the local market. So I became a little obsessed with keeping them out of the landfill. Recycling plastics is probably the only green thing I manage to do consistently (I'm still working on composting and about 107 other things). So after the giant pile of miscellaneous plastics finally threatened to take over our one-car garage, I decided to put my hours of watching HGTV home organization shows to work. I created a recycling center. Since I park my car in the garage and we store four bikes, two trikes and two ladders I was basically resigned to a small section of wall space. But because our garage leads right into our kitchen, it was the perfect spot.

I had about three feet of horizontal wall space to use, so I ordered a shelving system from Storables, cut to my exact dimensions. It was super-easy to install (I did it in about 10 minutes) and cost about $65. Plus I have the flexibility of adding more shelves. I've got bins on the shelves for rigid plastics, compact fluorescent lightbulbs (which contain mercury and should not be thrown in the trash), alkaline batteries and styrofoam. The long, white mesh bag (also bought at Storables for $10) on the right side has 5 compartments where we store plastic bags, sorted by number (two newspapers delivered in the morning, both double-bagged = a heck of a lot of plastic bags). I actually started running out of space, so in a plastic bag on the other side I store cereal box inserts. Perhaps if I actually took the stuff to the recycling center more than three times a year I might have a little more room.

Underneath, I built a stand for the curbside bins from scrap lumber so that I could store muddy shoes and a bin for more plastics. Our bucket for glass is on the floor next to the yellow bins. I recently started letting my kids help with some of the recycling, so I made labels using images of familiar food products that I pulled off of New Seasons online shopping site. Now they know where to put the flattened box of Annie's Mac & Cheese and the plastic milk cartons. My 5-year old is at the ripe old age where she is THRILLED to have big-girl responsibilities. I recently got a video from the library called Adventures with Wink & Blink: A Day in the Life of a Garbage Truck. It was painfully cheesy, which is why the kids loved it, but it also gave a nice overview of the garbage process all the way to the stinky landfill. So now my daughter is making the connection between garbage/recycling/landfill/litter/pollution. My Master Recyler class starts tonight, and I'm looking forward to learning more about recycling and sharing with all of you!

Time for a rebirth in bricolage

Bricolage, in case you don't know, is a fancy French word for tinkering, fixing or doing-it-yourself. And I think it's time for a rebirth in bricolage.

Tell me this hasn't happend to you: you buy a new VCR or DVD player. Right after the warranty runs out, like clockwork, said product stops working. You take it to a repair shop. They say, "It'll cost more to fix it than buy a new one." So what do you do? I know what I do. I buy a new one.

So that brings me back to bricolage. I've been tinkering with a lot of things lately. I've been resisting the urge to run out to a big box store and buy new things every time something breaks. Recently the folding handle on my hair dryer broke. The blower part still worked fine, but it was like blowing my hair with a wet noodle. Don't tell my husband he's right, but dang, if that wasn't fixed with a little bit of duct tape.

Now I know I don't have the expertise to go and fix my VCR. But maybe I should just pay the guy to fix it next time instead of buying a new one. For now I'll do what I can on little projects with Captain Duct Tape. I do enjoy the resourcefulness and creativity it takes.

What do you think about fixing, tinkering and bricolage? What are some of the things you've repaired on your own instead of replacing? Any professional bricoleurs you'd recommend?

Worms + kids + kitchen scraps = a great big squirmy fun mud pie

My husband is fond of saying that "the geek shall inherit the earth," but I believe the worms are a more likely winner. After a long hiatus, we've begun worm composting our kitchen scraps again. It's primarily a project for my husband and kids. They make a ritual of taking the compost bin outside, churning in the new scraps and chanting "come butter come." I have no idea how my husband gets them to say this - as the scraps don't smell nearly as sweet as fresh churned butter. In any case, it's their favorite outdoor game now. The challenge for me is to remember to put the coffee grounds, eggshells, veggie peelings, apple cores, etc. into the kitchen compost bin (I think I need to put a giant neon sticky-note directly on my brain). We've got our compost bin from Williams-Sonoma with a carbon filter in the lid so the bin doesn't get stinky. It doesn't look like Williams-Sonoma carries them now, but lots of other places like the Improvements catalog do.

If your kids are squeamish about worms, there's a great Web site for kids about worm composting by the University of Illinois called The Adventures of Herman.

And Metro has a plan for worm composting at schools, if you'd rather your school did the dirty work.

Do you compost in your backyard? Are there any compost bins or other products you can't live without? What are some other things you do to teach your kids about lessening our impact on the environment?
