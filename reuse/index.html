Crafting up a homemade holiday Sedaris-style



I wasn't sure I'd have time to get a post up today what with all of the crafting I've been doing. As the snow falls gently out my window, I've been snug inside with a cup of tea and a wonderfully inspirational craft book that's had me bustling about like a little elf on Christmas Eve. You see, when I left Portland Renee gave me this book as my parting gift, and frankly I think it should be on the shelf of every crafter. It's called Simple Times: Crafts for Poor People by Amy Sedaris, and while I think the title is a little misleading -- because even rich people can make these super crafts! -- it is chock full of inspired homemade creations that contain a strong dose of reuse. (Hooray for reuse!) OKAY FAMILY MEMBERS AND FRIENDS, IT'S TIME TO STOP READING NOW!!!!!!!!

One special friend is getting this rustic, woven fireside seat warmer made from newspaper. This was so easy to make, and what's cool is that if it rips you can just tape it back together. And once it's reached the end of its lifespan, you could either recycle it, compost it or burn it. It's like the circle of life.

 

 

Look at the detail on these canisters. I just love her use of green here.

 

 

I know you've seen lots of charming lighted Christmas villages on craft blogs, but not everyone lives in a cozy gingerbread house. Amy's Skid Row village shows a real sensitivity toward all people while making great use of empty milk cartons.

 

There is so much more in this book -- I'm particularly fond of her sausage crafts and human hair decorations -- that you just need to get it for yourself. Make your loved ones feel really special this season. Make it a homemade holiday.

Growling for beer

Having lived in the microbrew capital of the US these past 18 years, I've developed a taste for those foamy, hoppy libations. My preferred beverage while cooking? A bottle of something local, please. When we moved to Columbus three weeks ago, the beer selection at the grocery stores was completely foreign. There were plenty of microbrews, but none that were familiar. So my husband and I have begun the arduous task of sampling six pack after six pack (sigh) in search of our new favorite beer. Life is tough.

Recently, while trolling the aisles at our local Whole Foods, I spied beer taps behind the deli counter. What's going on here? They sell growlers! Refillable growlers of microbrews! Swoon. A growler is a glass jug that holds 64 fluid ounces of beer, equivalent to four pints. 



Drink it up, rinse it out, then bring it back for refills. I haven't figured out whether this is a cost-effective solution, since I'm still unfamiliar with the beers and what they might sell in 6-pack form. What I DO know is that it is an effective reuse solution. Yes, glass can be recycled over and over again, but it does require a whole lot of energy to do that. Bringing along my empty growler on my next shopping trip requires zero energy (well, except for lifting it). Perhaps the biggest downside of the growler system is that you need to drink the beer within 48 hours of opening it, or it will go flat. But I think I'm up for that challenge.

I don't believe all Whole Foods stores offer this service, which is a cryin' shame. I know you can get refillable growlers at pubs (Portland peeps, check out this growler map) but for convenience sake, wouldn't it be great if more grocery stores offered this service?

Buy Nothing New Halloween 2010: the unveiling...

The penguin came together fairly easily. I found a black hooded goblin outfit at a thrift shop early in the season. I sewed a white panel (formerly a t-shirt that said 'American Stoner' so I didn't feel too bad down-cycling it) in the front. Added some cardboard wings covered with old black tights (I have a habit of putting tights with runs in my drawer thinking they are going to mend themselves! ha!). We had some scrap yellow felt and elastic to make the feet and beak. Voila! Penny the Penguin (my girls really like alliteration, so it had to be Penny). We painted a plastic egg today, and I'm guessing she'll carry it around in her trick-or-treat bag.

Morgan le Fay? She was trickier than I expected. The white dress ($2 at a rummage sale) set this costume in motion. All we needed was a rich looking cape. I talked with my daughter and she really liked the idea of red velvet. Which is, of course, the only color I could not find. But I persevered. I did not go to a fabric store and buy new, as I was sorely tempted to do. The cape isn't as full as I hoped, but eventually, I found a red velour dress that had seen better days. A slice up the middle, a wee bit of hemming, and a dash of costume jewelry: the Enchantress lives! Oh, and a headband with a bunch of old clip-on earrings. Our Morgan le Fay is entirely styled upon the illustrations in the Magic Treehouse books.

OK, and just one more photo of them together since they were so obliging to put their costumes on early and not exactly smile, but not exactly scowl either. We'll call it their album cover look.

How are your Buy Nothing New Halloween costumes coming? If you have any photos you'd like to share, we'd be happy to post them later this week. Just send us an email. And keep praying for good weather on Halloween, wherever you may be!

Does the fear of bedbugs have you avoiding thrift stores?

I stopped by a local consignment store the other day and bought a cotton jacket for myself. The whole bedbug scare didn't even occur to me until I got it home. I guess it's been awhile since I bought anything second-hand. So now what?

I found a site that quoted a study by an expert in the field. Apparently putting bone-dry garments in a dryer on the hottest heat setting for five minutes will kill any bugs or eggs. Dry-cleaning will work, too. So I looked carefully at every seam and pocket and didn't see any signs of bugs. Thinking it's better to be safe than sorry, I washed the jacket (as I would any thrifted clothing) on cold (to avoid fading), then put it in the dryer on low (to avoid shrinkage) until it was dry. Once it was completely dry I upped the heat to high for five minutes. Hopefully that should do it.

I'd read that freezing a garment in a plastic bag for three days would also work, but apparently this is untrue because typical residential freezers don't get cold enough. Mine is set at 4 degrees, but it's thought that the temp needs to be below 0 degrees F to be effective. Unless you have access to a commercial deep-freezer or live in Mongolia, I wouldn't count on the freezing method.

While the high heat treatment might work for clothes, thrifted upholstered furniture is a bit tougher. I would avoid buying it altogether. Steam is often used by pest control experts to combat the bugs, so if you have a steamer you might research that route if you have your eye on the perfect vintage couch. 

It's such a bummer that reuse has become so risky. Have you cut back on your thrifting because of bed bugs?

Old cashmere sweaters become a new scarf

Recently I bought a gift for someone special. Me. I was having a day of mourning...feeling sorry that soon we would be leaving Portland and our friends and family. I needed something, something to make me feel better. Seldom do I ever turn to shopping therapy for that kind of relief (which is always short-lived) but this time I headed to a little boutique in NW Portland called Oxalis that I'd heard great things about. And it was there that I found this scarf, handmade by a local woman, of pieces of felted cashmere sweaters.

No two scarves were the same since the designer probably had a limited stash of sweaters with which to work. I love this scarf. It's recycled, it's soft and warm, it's pretty and it's so Portland. Perfect for those bitter Ohio winters. I have a stash of moth-eaten wool sweaters that I've been saving to make felted winter hats for everyone in the family, and now I'm inspired to get going on that long-delayed project. We're going to need them!

2010 Buy-Nothing-New Halloween Costume Challenge is on!

The EnviroMom buy-nothing-new Halloween costume challenge is pleased to kick off its third year! Your options are many. Swap costumes with friends and neighbors! Create something new-to-you by shopping in your closets or by tapping into your local thrift stores. Talk your younger kid into reusing a costume one of your older kids used several years back. Just don't buy anything new when decking out your kids in their Halloween duds this year. It's easy and fun!

Our family has gotten over the first hurdle in record time, I might add. The kids named their costumes of choice, to which I had no objections, therefore wasted no energy in trying to convince them to be something easier to make. We even got basic ingredients for our costumes at end-of-summer rummage sales to the tune of two dollars. Nicely done! Thankyouverymuch!

Without further ado, I present the artists' costume renderings (and these were drawn by my kids, not me):


Behold the penguin, clutching an egg at its feet. Don't ask me how we're going to manage the egg part. But my 6-year-old has been working on her penguin waddle.


Next, we present the enchantress of Camelot, Morgan le Fay. My 8-year-old is a big fan of the Magic Treehouse book series, where Morgan le Fay plays a recurring role as librarian.

We've had a few recent rainy Sundays that have given me time to start working on the costumes, and oh, they are super fun and looking good. I can't wait to share the finished costumes with you! Reuse: summer book sale!

Yesterday our neighborhood's business association hosted its annual blueberry pancake breakfast and book sale. The pancake breakfast? The quintessential feel-good community event of the summer. Perfect sunny day. Chit-chatting with friends and neighbors. Delicious grub, locally grown and in season. What could be better? Add a book sale that benefits the community! As a special treat, this year's sale of kid books benefits our local elementary school's literacy programs. Win-win.


Earlier in the summer, I cleared out some of the book titles we're either not going to re-read, or in the case of kid books, we've outgrown. Teen-sy tiny little tax deduction for that. Then yesterday, we either bought 19 books or spent 19 bucks in kids' books. Either way, it's all good. I realize it's a ton of work to host a book sale like this, and it seems like a lot of school and church rummage sales are getting retired, with the complaint that it's too much work for the money. I just hope they never go away entirely. I love the reuse aspect, and even more, love a good book, or 19.
