Hip, hip, hooray for PPS durable lunch trays!

I just read a news tidbit that PPS, via a Metro grant, is using durable cafeteria trays -- instead of disposable styrofoam as is the standard in most schools -- in several elementary cafeterias. Hip, hip, hooray for durable trays!! Let's hope this is a successful experiment, and that more schools go to durable trays.

Local food businesses that make our lives easier & healthier

Do we Portlanders live in a bubble, or is this sustainable food movement happening nationwide? I keep hearing about all of these small Oregon food companies that prepare local, organic foods for children and families, and some that will deliver meals right to your door! Happy day! I vaguely remember those early days, fresh from the hospital, with a tiny bundle that seemed to keep me up all hours of the day. Who had the energy to cook? Check out some of these options that cater to babes and parents-of-babes:

Building Blocks Foods: Jonathan Grumbles is a professional chef who's worked in NYC, Minneapolis, and now Portland. He cooks up fresh, local, sustainable fare and delivers it to your doorstep! He's a dad to two little kiddos and knows how hard it can be to get nutritious food on the table when your life revolves around a newborn. Heck, it's a struggle for most of us with older kids to get this kind of food on the table. His menus are mouth-watering!

Gaia Baby Food: Another local parent (and she just gave birth to #2 -- congrats!), Alisa Gaylord, cooks up local, seasonal baby food the same day it is delivered to your door. She works with local farmers and only cooks up what is fresh today. Three categories of foods: Smooth (for babes up to 8 months), Chunky (9-plus months) and Toddler (11 months-plus) serve all phases of development. Check out her online store and delivery schedule (she's on a limited maternity-leave schedule until early Feb.)

Organic Fresh Fingers: Imagine your child at his full-time daycare center. It's lunchtime. What would you like him to be eating? (Certainly not the ho-hum, questionable fare our public schools are serving up, right?) Enter Organic Fresh Fingers, a Salem-based business that prepares fresh, nutritious, certified-organic meals that contain no GMOs, artificial flavors or coloring for schools and daycare centers around the region. It almost sounds too good to be true, but it's not. In fact, schools with a large percent of low-income students get extra funding for meals and have recognized that going organic is an affordable and smart option for them. Hooray!   

Nice Cubes: Mom-of-two-boys, Kate McNamara, used to cook up batches of baby food and freeze them in ice trays, which her husband referred to as 'nice cubes.' Nice for us, she expanded her talent into a business and now offers a variety of Nice Cubes organic frozen baby food in stores all over the Portland Metro area.

Farm to School Web portal launched

Check this out! Farm to School is a Web portal that details every school lunch program in the country working to bring more local food to students. Click on your state and read about what schools and districts are doing, who you should contact for more information, upcoming events and other information. It's heartening that so many states have active programs. This description of Portland Public School's program jumped out at me:

The district has engaged in a visioning process to work towards making healthy, nutritious foods readily available to all students, with significant goals such as 100% of menu items prepared regionally; 50% of menu items sourced locally; hormone, antibiotic and nitrate free meats to the extent possible; dairy products that are free of recombinant bovine growth hormone; fish products from sustainable sources; and fruits and vegetables that are sustainably or organically grown.

Just by chance I checked out Vermont's programs and discovered VT FEED (Vermont Food Education Every Day). They use a three pronged approach to local food education:

1. The Classroom, with standard-based food, farm, and nutrition-based curriculum that involves in-depth graduate level teacher training, including summer workshops and in-class mentoring;

2. The Cafeteria, through the integration of local food and nutrition education through food service institutes, in-cafeteria mentoring, and peer support;

3. The Community, through reconnecting people to their food sources- the farms and farmers.

All very exciting stuff.

Eco-friendly school fundraisers

Last week my daughter brought home the dreaded Sally Foster fund raising packet with the catalog of all the different styles of wrapping paper -- none made from recycled paper -- they want you to buy and sell. There were a few gift items, such as the requisite candies and candles, but wrapping paper was definitely the biggie. I made a promise to myself that I would no longer buy wrapping paper, and instead rely on reusable gift bags or alternative ways to present a gift that didn't add to the waste stream. So I can't in good conscious buy or sell any of the stuff in the catalog (that's my excuse and I'm stickin' to it).

It got me to wondering whether there are any fund raising programs out there that offer eco-friendly products. We have the Chinook Book, jammed with coupons for healthy living products and services, which my son's preschool sells. I love these books and usually buy two of them, but it seems like the Portland market is absolutely saturated with Chinook Book fund raisers. I found the site GreenRaising, where schools can have online (for a 25% return) or catalog (for a 40% return) sales of eco-friendly products like Sigg water bottles, lead-free lunch boxes, reusable bags and biodegradable cleaning products. Reusable Planet offers a program that recycles ink and toner cartridges and cell phones. Canada has a program called Green Students, where schools raise money by selling compact fluorescent light bulbs and Kleen Kanteen water bottles.

I understand that public schools have to have fund raisers to supplement their paltry budgets, but enough of the wrapping paper and candy already! Has your school held a green fund raiser? Have you heard about any others than those mentioned above?

Eco-healthy childcare: Wanna go green?

Heather and I are not the only parents at our preschool who want the school to be as green as possible. So this year's preschool board is asking an "old school" (it was founded in 1958) to learn some new tricks. Since we are a cooperative, the first thing we needed to do was establish a "committee" so that there would be an  "Eco-Awareness Parent" at the school.

Lucky for us, there's guidance available. The Oregon Environmental Council has established criteria to certify schools and childcare centers as "Eco-Healthy." It's something we're striving for, and hope to achieve in the next year. They have a checklist of 25 criteria to qualify as Eco-Healthy.  And a list of providers that are already meeting the criteria.

Does your child attend a qualified Eco-Healthy Childcare facility? Do you have any lessons to share about shepherding your school through the process? If so, we'd love to hear from you!

Crafting cloth napkins for child's lunchbox

I don't know where this crazy idea came from. I can't get it out of my head. I'm thinking about a batik/tie-dye project making cloth napkins for my kindergarten-bound daughters lunches. With my 5-year-old's help. Is this utterly insane? I'm all for durable goods. I bought some stainless steel utensils to pack in her lunch from Ikea. Hopefully we'll use them for years. Now this thing with the napkins is either brilliant or insane. You tell me.

Here's my idea: try to write her name on the napkins with the batik wax, then tie-die or batik the fabric so that they'll be vibrantly colored and fun to use. And with her name on them, they won't get lost. The wacky part of this equation is I'm not a crafty person. You should have seen the birthday cake I made for my 3-year-old's family party a few weeks ago. I had visions of a gorgeous, delectable cake a la Baker & Spice. It was more like a hockey puck with sprinkles. Heather and I nearly wet ourselves laughing when I first showed it to her.

What in the world these napkins are going to turn out like is beyond me. Probably quite ugly. To top it off, I was thinking I'd use all natural type dyes. Maybe something brewed from wildflowers. Am I stark raving mad? Please, someone who is a competent crafter, set me straight before I mention this hare-brained idea to my daughter and she buys into the project...

Eco-friendly back-to-school shopping

Pencils, crayons, scissors, markers, glue, folders, recycled paper, the list goes on and on. I got it all from Schoolhouse Supplies just sitting at my computer. Renee and I have written about this fantastic reuse organization many times, and they just keep on giving and giving back to the community. Six elementary schools (Rieke, Ainsworth, Stephenson, Alameda, Bridlemile and Laurelhurst) are participating in Schoolhouse Supplies' online shopping this year, so take advantage of it if your kids attend one. The schools give Schoolhouse Supplies the shopping list for each grade; you simply go to the site, find your school and grade, and click! the supplies will be delivered to your child's classroom. No gas wasted. No standing in line. Plus your purchase benefits this fantastic organization! Win, win, win.

I got everything on the Rieke kindergarten list (except the backpack) for $30. (Note: all of the supplies are new.) Prices will vary because each school requires different supplies, but the price is pretty competitive with what you'd pay at a big box store. Schoolhouse Supplies hopes to have more schools on board for next year -- talk to your principal if your school isn't on the list!

Does your child's school have a garden?

I was just reading about California's $15 million investment to develop gardens for the 6,000 schools in the state (via TreeHugger). Each school would receive an average of $2,500 to start or maintain a garden. That's a pretty impressive investment. It's a no-brainer that school gardens can help children understand the importance of eating well and caring for the environment -- as long as teachers have the proper understanding to teach them.

Our future elementary, Rieke, has a small plot of raised garden beds, but I don't know whether all the students get to participate in caring for the gardens, or just some. (I recently joined the school's Green Team, so I suspect I will soon find out.) I do know that it usually takes a dedicated parent or teacher to 'champion' a school garden, and not all schools have a champion. Every time I drive past Buckman Elementary in SE Portland, I look longingly at the beautiful garden next to the school and wonder how much time the students spend in it.

Does your school have a garden? Are all of the students involved in its care and maintenance?

Oudoor Kids: Mother Earth Bio-Immersion Kindergarten

We've been talking about ways to get your kids to spend more time outdoors, and this is probably the most fool-proof: enroll them in outdoor kindergarten! The Shining Star Waldorf School in NE Portland has started a satellite kindergarten program at the Tryon Life Community Farm in SW Portland. The Mother Earth Bio-Immersion Kindergarten will be held daily from 9:30am-1:30pm, and tuition is $320 per month. They still have spots available for Fall.

The kids will spend the majority of their day outdoors -- all year long -- which means rainboots and Gore-Tex. They will study things like zoology, botany, natural medicine, agriculture and animal husbandry, and follow the general curriculum that most Waldorf schools embrace. You can read more about the program in an article from today's Oregonian. It sounds like the program was inspired to counteract "nature-deficit disorder," a concept from Richard Louv's Last Child in the Woods.

I keep forgetting that the Tryon Life farm is in my neck of the woods, so I'll definitely be bringing the kids for a visit. What a great resource! The farm is open to the public for self-guided tours on Thursdays, Saturdays and Sundays from 11am-4:30pm, and you're invited to pull on a pair garden gloves and pitch in. They also offer a summer farm camp for kids ages 8-11 -- keep this in mind for next year.

Is your child's school an Oregon Green School?

There is an amazing organization called Oregon Green Schools, which is a non-profit dedicated to helping schools set up and manage waste reduction and other efficiency programs in order to create a healthy learning environment. Is your school on the list? If not, check out the Web site and find your area coordinator who will help you through the process. It just takes one person to get the wheels in motion! Your kids will get important lessons in the reduce/reuse/recycle credo and take pride in being part of something really special.

Looking for eco-friendly childcare? Want to become one?

The Oregon Environmental Council has a program that recognizes preschools and childcare providers that incorporate environmentally-friendly practices. They've created a checklist of items that the schools must fulfill in order to qualify. Check it out if you're looking for an eco-friendly provider or want to become one. Many of these items are fairly standard (I hope), like using non-toxic art supplies, keeping garbage covered and not using mercury thermometers. Other items require more effort, like not using pesticides, not allowing cars to idle near the facility and using biodegradable cleaning products. I'm hoping to get my daughter's preschool on the list, and it won't take much time or effort to get there. I think a lot of it involves educating parents and staff, who in turn pass it on to the kids.

October 4th is International Walk to School Day

Put down the keys to your Subaru! Walk or bike to school on Wednesday, October 4. It's International Walk to School Day. I'll bet you didn't even know there was such a thing. I sure didn't.

Oregon calls it Walk + Bike to School Day. Be on the lookout and drive carefully tomorrow if you are out. Around 14,000 kids in Oregon will be out there on walking feet or biking wheels.

This day brings our attention to the fact that kids need to move to be healthy, we need safe routes to school, and air quality is improved if some of us soccer moms stay out of our cars.

For information on this event in Washington and other states, check out www.walktoschool.org.

(Originally published on ReadySetMom.com)
My kids are not in Portland public school, and I think we'd risk our lives if we tried to walk from our house to preschool tomorrow - so I won't be walking or biking with my kids, and I'll be cheering all of the rest of you on. But do count on me next year. For sure. Even if it's raining.
