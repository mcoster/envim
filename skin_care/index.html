Eco-friendly cosmetics -- what do you recommend?

Up until recently I've been make-up free for most of my life. In high school I wore a little, but I always felt clown-ish. In my 20s and 30s I just didn't want to take the time to mess with it. Now with 40 only a couple years away, I'm starting to dabble in cosmetics again to, you know, even out and brighten up the ol' visage. I've transitioned my skin care regime over to a gentler, less-toxic cleanser and moisturizer, so now I think it's only appropriate (and responsible) to do the same with cosmetics. But where to start?

I've seen the make-up counter at Whole Foods and New Seasons, but the stuff is spendy. Before I plunk down some dough, I'd love to hear about brands you guys like. What about eco-friendly mascara? Is there such a thing, and does it clump, flake and run? What about these mineral-based cosmetics...did I read somewhere that they make your skin kind of glimmery? Is that a desirable look? Lipstick, anyone? Please help this cosmetically-challenged EnviroMom update her look responsibly!

The skin care dilemma

After reading about the purportedly dangerous chemicals in so many skin care products, I finally managed to wean myself off of Neutrogena and switched over to Avalon Organics facial cleanser and moisturizer, which I bought at Wild Oats. So I'm thinking, "Hmm, organic and sold at Wild Oats, so it's got to be pretty harmless, right?" I've used it for about a month and I LOVE IT. I'm using the Vitamin C Refreshing Cleansing Gel and the Vitamin C Rejuvenating Oil-Free Moisturizer. It feels really light on my skin and never oily. And I didn't get that nasty breakout that sometimes comes with a change in skin care. I'm thinking I hit the jackpot! Well, then I checked it out over at the Environmental Working Group database. Sigh. The cleanser scored a 5, which is not good (risk of cancer and all sorts of other nastiness) and the moisturizer was a 4, which is slightly better but not much.

So I'm looking through the list of cleansers that scored a 0, which means 'totally harmless,' and I see Dr. Bronner's Castile Soap, which Renee just used to make hand soap. Who is this magical Dr. Bronner, and should I trust him on my face? Would you? Or should I just stick with my beloved Avalon and live on the edge? But, if it does really contain unhealthy ingredients, shouldn't we be sending stern messages to the manufacturers by boycotting these products? So many questions...

Help EWG discover which personal care products are really safe for kids

The Environmental Working Group site has become my personal go-to site any time I have a question about the safety of skin care products and other environmental concerns. They perform exhaustive, comprehensive studies on so many important topics. Now they are working on a new children's health study and they need the input from parents like YOU. Amanda writes:

This summer, EWG is digging in to the safety of children's personal care products (shampoo, diaper cream, bubble bath and the like) to give you our researchers' recommendations on the best children's products on the market -- and to help fill gaps in a system that doesn't require companies to test products before they're sold.
We're asking parents to help us by filling out a brief survey on the products they use on their children.  We're looking for at least 1,000 responses over the next two weeks that will let us take a detailed look at children's day-to-day exposures, from the chemicals in their baths to the products on their hair and skin and even their bottoms.
A couple minutes of your time filling out the EWG survey will bring heightened public awareness of unsafe toxins and chemicals in certain products and hopefully encourage consumers to stop buying them. (And ultimately force the manufacturers to stop producing them.) Every voice counts, so please make yours heard!

Sunscreen, take II

A few days ago Renee posted about sunscreen, so I've been doing a little research to find a good one. Check out the Environmental Working Group's investigation of 783 brand-name sunscreen products and their effectiveness. It's a little overwhelming, but basically they've discovered that a huge percentage are totally ineffective and contain ingredients that are oh so bad for us and our wee ones. Their #1 pick is Badger SPF 30, which I've never heard of, but the site also offers links to where you can purchase all of the products. It is an incredibly in-depth study, and it makes my head hurt to look at all those words and numbers, but I can tell you I'll be chucking a couple from the cupboard that made the "worst" list. Eesh.

Cleaning out the cabinets, part II

I've been doing some major cabinet purging lately and amassed piles of stuff from the kitchen, bathrooms and laundry room that I really wanted to avoid tossing out, but knew my family would never use them again. Beth's recent email to us also got me motivated to find reuse organizations for as much as I could. Happily, I managed to find homes for almost everything:

  • Kitchen: An old mixer and Dustbuster, both working great; sturdy Avent sippy cups; plastic toddler forks and spoons; a wok and some miscellaneous kitchen utensils went to the Oregon Community Warehouse. I love this organization because they work directly with Portlanders in desperate need of household items.
  • Laundry Room: Lots and lots of mainstream cleaning products, most partially-used, like Drano, Comet Cleanser, Soft Scrub and industrial-strength floor cleaner (why did we buy this?) all went to the Good Neighbor Center in Tigard. I'd never heard of this organization before, but they are a shelter that serves many families with young children. In addition to the cleaning products, the Good Neighbor Center also gladly accepted items from my...
  • Bathroom: Half-used shampoos, skin lotions, bug spray, shaving cream -- products we'd tried or bought on impulse and didn't like. Also, baby lotions and diaper cream that we no longer need. The only products I was unable to get rid of were expired prescription medications. I took them to the Burlingame Fred Meyer pharmacy and was told that they are unable to dispose of them. The technician suggested I burn them in the fireplace! Obviously, not an option. Anyone have luck getting rid of medications? (Renee, I swear you took yours to Fred's.)

I was really appalled at the amount of stuff we had just sitting and gathering dust -- particularly the cleaning and personal hygiene products. Sure, we could have used them up, but it would have taken years with the cleaning products, and I really don't want that stuff in my house anymore. All of the lotions and potions made me realize that I really need to stop and think before buying anything like this -- this is where the REDUCE part of the Reduce-Reuse-Recycle equation comes in. I'm glad that someone can use it, but what a waste of money (and resources)!

What are your "sacred cows"?

Our GreenGroup met today, and in one of many tangential conversations, we talked briefly about our "sacred cows": luxuries we'd not willingly part with, even if we were down to our last collective gallon of gasoline. Well, maybe not that extreme, but hopefully you get the idea. (Let me note that this post is mostly for fun since we greenfolk are sometimes accused of being a little too serious much of the time).

For me, I don't think I could give up hair color. My hair started turning gray, well, white, when I was 16. It's almost pure white now. I end up coloring it every 4 weeks. And I know it's just a vanity thing, but I really feel so much better when I'm not sportin' a white roots skunk strip down the middle of my head. I also don't think I could give up the razor. I often feel I'm stuck between a rock and a hard place: which is worse? the disposable shower razor or the rechargeable electric razor? In any case, I'm still using both on a regular basis, and I don't think I'll give up either any time soon. I also have a soft spot for fizzy water. But, I will give that up, one of these days. At least I don't insist on Pellegrino or fancy imported fizzy water. Does that seem like a fair rationalization?

For my kids, they love those moist, flushable wipes. I wish I could break them of that habit. But I've almost given up trying.

So, what about you? Just for fun, what are your sacred cows? Are there any things you'd refuse to give up even for the good of mama earth, in the name of sustainable living, even as all the oil wells run dry?

Beauty may be skin deep, but the chemicals go deeper

This green-living business is starting to snowball. A month ago I wouldn't have believed that my way of thinking could have so radically shifted, but here I am, tackling the next big change: skin care. This is a pretty personal subject, and everyone has their favorite brands for their type of skin. I was a die-hard Lancome user for years, until I became a Neutrogena user to save money. Now I read about the harsh chemicals in these products, and it particularly bothers me when I think about how I've slathered my kids with questionable lotions, soaps and shampoos their whole lives.

So I'm in the market for a new skin care regime. I suspect the best thing I could do for my skin is eat healthier foods and drink more water, and that's on the to-do list. But I'd love some suggestions for a plant or vegetable-based facial cleanser and moisturizer. Is there such a thing as a natural wrinkle cream? And how about the kids? Any natural shampoos, soaps or lotions that you can recommend?
