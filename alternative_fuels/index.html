Putting cow poop to good use

Except for spreading cow manure compost in your garden, it's hard for us regular city-dwelling folk to put cow poop to good use. Unless you are a NW Natural gas customer... They just announced a new program that supports projects designed to reduce greenhouse gas emissions like biogas from cow manure. Manure produces methane, which can be captured and turned into fuel. Apparently, Oregon cows produce 14 million pounds of poop each day. That's a lot of cow pies. Steaming heaps of alternative energy just a waitin' to be utilized. Where do I sign up?

Soaring gas prices: are you feeling the pinch?

According to the Oregonian, Oregon has the dubious honor of the third highest gas prices in the nation. And many people -- especially working people -- are feeling it in the pocketbook. Are you feeling the pinch?

I know I am starting to make changes based on the high gas prices and changing weather. Earlier in the year, I had planned a camp-filled summer for the kiddos, and now I am rethinking that. I don't want to drive all over town, create smog and pay top dollar for gas. I'm finding activities closer to home. Places we can walk or bike to. We signed up for swimming lessons at a community pool that we can walk/stroll to, instead of driving to the SWCC as we have in past years.

What about you? Are you changing driving habits based on the high gas prices? Taking the bus more? Hitching up the Burly trailer to your bike? Switching to biodiesel or recycled veggie oil?

Do not believe everything you read

I hate to even give this any more exposure, but I’ve just learned a valuable lesson in the importance of research before reaction. The Sunday Oregonian ran an editorial (sorry, couldn't find it on OregonLive but it's the same) titled “Hybrids might look green, but look behind that battery,” written by James L. Martin. His editorial basically repeats the findings of a report by Oregon-based CNW Marketing Research, which claim that a Toyota Prius actually consumes more energy than a Hummer when you look at the amount of resources needed to manufacture a Prius. Converted to dollars per gallon, the report asserts that a Prius has an energy cost of $3.25 with a life expectancy of 100,000 miles while the Hummer costs $1.95 with a life span of 300,000 miles.

For about 24 hours after I read the piece I was really bummed. I’ve longed for a hybrid for awhile for its low emissions, so reading this was deflating. Then I did some online research to see what other people were saying about the study, and discovered that it is flawed in so many ways. For one, the study assumes that Hummers will be driven for 300,000 miles and a Prius for only 100,000. No way on both counts. That skews the results right there. Secondly, the study factored in research and development costs – a hybrid is a unique automobile and its R&D costs are going to be much higher than a vehicle that is just a souped-up version of an existing SUV. Maybe RIGHT NOW it is taking more energy to manufacture a hybrid, but there’s no denying that once it’s on the road it is consuming far, far less. And in 10 years, when we’re paying $8/gallon for gas and the earth’s temperature has risen even more and Toyota has mainstreamed its production and perhaps is no longer using nickel-based batteries, the hybrid will rule.

And just who is James L. Martin, the author of the op-ed piece? Why he is the President of an organization called 60 Plus Association, which states it is a “non-partisan seniors advocacy group with a free enterprise, less government, less taxes approach to seniors issues.” On the home page of its Web site, you can count FOUR photos of Mr. Martin shaking hands with his close, personal, long-time friend George W. Bush. Non-partisan, you say? Um, okay.
