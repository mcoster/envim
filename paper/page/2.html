Opting out of unwanted phone books

EnviroMom readers Erin and Christy have both sent me this info about opting out of unwanted phone books from the Oregon Department of Environmental Quality. Let me share a few fun facts (well, you decide if they're fun or not):

  • The Oregon DEQ estimates in 2003 there were 6.45 million sets of phone books published and distributed despite there being only 1.33 million households in the state
  • Nationally only 20 percent of phone books are recycled (the rest are landfilled or burned)
  • Recycling a 3 pound phone book reduces greenhouse gases by 5.9 pounds of carbon dioxide equivalent
  • Preventing the publishing of that same 3 pound phone book reduces greenhouse gas emissions by more than three times that amount (18.1 pounds)

The DEQ offers suggestions to opt of out phone books. I'm telling ya. Someone has got to get on this. All efforts I've made so far have done nothing to stop the flow of phone books to my front stoop. But it is time, dad-gummit! My father cracked our one set of phone books while visiting the other day -- quite possibly for the only time in 2008. We use them so rarely it took me 10 minutes to remember where the phone books might be stored.

For extra credit, read about the national effort to reduce the overpublishing of phone books by the Product Stewardship Institute's Phone Book Project.

Disposable hat. Useless products. Buyer beware.

I did not mean to buy a disposable hat, but that is in fact what I did. A little background. On our family vacation to Florida last summer, I knew there was no way we'd leave this tourist magnet without spending a little bit of money on souvenirs. Gift shops abounded. We were shocked and horrified at how much some of the Disneyfied trinkets go for. So the siren call of the mermaid store, which was between Disney World and our vacation rental, soon became irresistible. She beckoned not only with her giant mermaid sculpted body in bas relief along the expanse of the storefront -- but with shopping teases such as "beach towels as low as $2.99!" and "Disney t-shirts for $1.99." Sure, we knew all this stuff would be poorly made, overseas, probably in sweatshops. But the alternative was spending about ten times as much for the same stuff in Disney shops. So we stopped to shop.

We spent a long time shopping. I told my two daughters they could each pick one thing. My younger daughter had a hard time deciding. After picking up and putting down about 200 items, she settled on a toy Tinkerbell made-in-China cell-phone that started falling apart before we were even 10 minutes from the store. Price: $9.99. My older daughter, ever practical, opted for a sun hat. I was overjoyed. What a wonderful, useful souvenir! What a sensible child I have raised! I have not been good at enforcing sunscreen and hats when we work in the garden, but felt this would make that job easier if she could wear a hat of her own choosing. Price: $12.99. Much self-congratulation for not spending a lot of cash on souvenirs.

Now, I have done a good job in recent years of religiously reading the labels of all foods we buy. We routinely say "no" to the overly processed, high fructose corn syrup, GMO, individually packaged, trans-fat containing food-like substances (thank you Michael Pollan). Woe to me for not examining the label on my daughter's hat. It reads: "100% paper."

Sign the petition for a Do Not Mail Registry -- NOW!

End junk mail now! A movement is afoot to enact a Do Not Mail Registry similar to the the successful Do Not Call Registry. To get Congress moving on it, Forest Ethics, a non-profit environmental organization, has created a petition and is collecting online signatures to show support. Telemarketers stopped bugging us when we registered on the Do Not Call registry, and now I want a Do Not Mail registry! Don't you?

Weigh in: Should cities ban or tax plastic and paper grocery bags?

Here in Portland we're engaged in a huge debate: incoming Mayor Sam Adams has proposed a ban or tax on disposable grocery bags. Plastic bags are made from petroleum, and paper bags are made from trees. Both the production, transport and recycling of these bags are energy-intensive and produce greenhouse gases in the process. Other cities have already gone down this path: San Francisco banned plastic grocery bags last year, and Los Angeles and Seattle have plans to follow. Many other countries have long banned plastic bags. Litter is another problem. You see those bags floating around everywhere, and they are a hazard to wildlife.

Sam Adams wants to encourage Portland consumers to carry reusable grocery bags (as no doubt many EnviroMoms already do). But many folks here are outraged. Outraged! How dare the government stick its nose into our grocery bags! I suspect that most of these folks don't believe global warming is real and are resentful that they may be forced to change their behavior.

There was a quote from one man on OregonLive: "My wife and I have three children (two are teenage boys) and we go to the grocery store every week. Those trips we can have a dozen bags. This is a great example of a politician not being in touch with the working class."

I'd like to tell this man that they could easily go from twelve plastic bags down to four reusable grocery bags. You can buy reusable bags with flat bottoms for .99 each. Adams has also proposed using a bag tax to fund a program that provides lower income folks with free reusable bags.

It took me a few months to develop the habit of carrying my reusable bags into the grocery store. I found a post I'd written in March 2007 about San Francisco's ban on plastic bags where I admit my difficulty remembering to bring my bags. But now, I never forget. I bring them everywhere -- to Powell's, the hardware store, clothing stores. It's a habit now.

I'm in favor of a ban on plastic bags (and don't you go substituting those ridiculous 'compostable' plastic bags either, Sam) and at least a 10-cent fee for paper bags. Build up a nice fund so that everyone can have access to reusable bags and then get rid of the paper. The Oregonian is in favor of charging a fee for plastic bags. And if people are worried about what they'll use to pick up their pet waste? Perhaps the Oregonian could start collecting all of those plastic newspaper bags they distribute every morning and make them available to pet owners. You want a bold mayor, Oregonian? Show us your bold.

What do you think about disposable grocery bags: ban 'em? charge for 'em? or neither? Have a brilliant alternative to propose? Let's hear it!

Opting out of catalogs, chapter 2

We've talked about Catalog Choice and Green Dimes before, free online services that will contact mail order companies on your behalf and ask them to cease and desist all the catalogs filling up your mailbox. I signed up with Catalog Choice back in October 2007 and they were able to stop a couple of my catalogs, but not others. Green Dimes requires you to provide a credit card number for verification (they don't charge it), but I think it makes some folks wary. I signed up with them in April 2008, and a few catalogs have been successfully stopped, but I just realized that 'action is required' to stop the bulk of my requested catalogs. The 'action' usually means you have to print out, sign and mail a letter to the company.

You know what's even faster? Calling them yourself! One of our readers had this brilliant suggestion a few months ago, so I took her up on it. I called the Pottery Barn customer service number at 1-800-779-5176 and got them to remove my name from ALL of their company mail order catalogs: West Elm, Williams-Sonoma and all of the Pottery Barn offshoots like Teen, Bed & Bath, Kids, etc. It helps to have a catalog in hand to give them your customer number. I'm going to start doing this with all the catalogs (and I'm talking to you, Oriental Trading Company) because it's much, much faster.

Another idea: check out these cute owls on the Apartment Therapy Re-Nest blog made from a Design Within Reach catalog!

Hey, have a happy July 4th and a great weekend! We'll be back next Monday to kick-off Kitchen Week for the One Can A Month Challenge!

Baby step: cutting down on disposable paper tissues

Given that it just snowed a few days ago, it's probably premature to say winter and cold and flu season are behind us. But, we can hope, anyway. Regardless, I'm really pleased to say this year we've seen a drastic reduction in the amount of disposable paper tissues coming in and out of our house. Not that we didn't have colds and flu. Plenty of those nasty viruses found their way into our noses, etc. But the tips we got from you, our readers, led to some changes...

I believe it was Carol who noted in my post about using handerkerchiefs that Kirkland baby wipes (which are made of cloth, not paper) can go through the washer and dryer. So we've experimented with that, and it's been very successful. I almost never use a paper tissue to blow my nose anymore, since I can usually tap into my store of washed-and-dried Kirkland hankies now. The kids have actually paid attention to my talks about using less paper. So in years past, we would have weathered winter with a giant Costco case of 8-10 tissue boxes. This year I think I only bought a few Seventh Generation boxes (not a case, just the regular boxes). They are using less, or they are wiping their noses on their sleeves more. Either way, we're using less disposable paper, so that's a good thing, right?

Thanks to Julie F.'s great recommendation, we are using a bandanna as our "drying towel on the go" instead of using disposable paper towels in public restrooms. And since we almost exclusively use cloth napkins in the kitchen, we've gotten used to wiping our noses and mouths on cloth.

So yes, it's a baby step. But it feels good to celebrate small sucesses. And I remember thinking how weird it would be to wipe my nose with cloth when I first started thinking about it. Now that we've switched, the idea of using paper sounds equally weird!!

Sweating the small stuff: deodorant and minimizing other stinks

When I first started walking down the primrose path to green living, one piece of advice jumped out at me: don't sweat the small stuff. Yet, that's what I literally find myself doing. The deodorant thing. I know it's not just me. Mom go Green has struggled to find a natural deodorant that works, but doesn't irritate her skin. Is it vanity? I don't know. But I really couldn't stand it if EnviroMom's motto became: "EnviroMom is so green you can smell them coming!" Yet I fear that's the road I've been on with the natural deodorants I've tried so far. Now I realize everyone's body chemistry is different so what works for you may not work for me. Two cases in point:

  • Jason Aloe Vera: This deodorant may work for you. For me, it works for about 7 minutes, then goes into nuclear meltdown, and I start to smell like a sailor who has been out to sea, under a hot blazing sun, and hasn't taken a shower for far too long...
  • Kiss My Face Active Enzyme: This deodorant may work for you. It did not work for me. It's just a good thing they don't call it "Kiss My Armpit."

Both the above sticks are paraben and aluminum free. That's great. Regrettably, they don't help in the de-stink department for my body chemistry. Sadly, I've reverted back to something along the lines of Secret. I don't stink, but I'm creeped out by the active ingredients in the one I'm using and the Speed Stick or whatever Cheap-o-Dad uses: aluminum chlorohydrate, propylene glycol and triclosan (the key antibacterial in those hand sanitizers I despise so much!!). I'm not sure what to do about the stinky armpit business. Maybe I'll just take a hall pass for now, and hope that I'm not giving myself cancer or killing the earth by using crappy deodorant.

As far as other stinks go (specifically the ones that emanate from children's bottoms after going poop), I have managed to wean our family off those moist flushable wipes that I at one time had placed on our sacred cow list. We are managing quite handily with pre-moistening (with tap water) a small wad of recycled-content toilet paper for those times when a wet wipe is needed. No need for an extra chemical-laden disposable product and that annoying plastic box that's hard to recycle.

Is your home becoming paperless?

The New York Times ran an article recently about the fact that more and more families are creating 'paperless homes.' The cost of ink is one factor (they are more hesitant to press 'print' than an office worker) but they can also find what they need electronically rather than looking toward paper sources (phone books, cook books, news, etc.) Sounds environmentally responsible, yes? The down side is that your electrical usage goes up, so are you really making progress?

I know that I seldom press 'print', but I don't think it has any great impact on the amount of paper in our home. We get two newspapers each morning, and I'm a total bookworm. I don't see myself laying in bed, curled up with a Kindle. We continue to get loads of junk mail every day, despite my efforts to cut it out. My daughter brings home tons of paper from school each day and there are always receipts swirling around in my purse. We never touch the phone book, but that doesn't stop those renegade phone companies from tossing them on my porch every other month. Sure, we've stopped buying paper towels and napkins...that was easy! And we pay most of our bills online (though the bank insists it's illegal for them to stop sending those mammoth statements). The paper just keeps coming and coming! Have you had success stopping the flow of paper, and has it made a difference in your life?
