Oatmeal at McDonald's is a nutritional scam

I hadn't heard about McDonald's foray into my favorite breakfast until this morning when I read Mark Bittman's spot-on critique of the fast-food chain's 'bowl full of wholesome.' Apparently McD's has managed to take simple oatmeal and turn it into a bloated sugar-bomb that has almost as many calories as its cheeseburger and is more expensive than its double cheeseburger. Writes Bittman:

A more accurate description than “100% natural whole-grain oats,” “plump raisins,” “sweet cranberries” and “crisp fresh apples” would be “oats, sugar, sweetened dried fruit, cream and 11 weird ingredients you would never keep in your kitchen.”

All of this in the name of convenience, which is debatable considering the amount of time you spend driving to the restaurant and standing in line or waiting in the drive-thru. C'mon people, think. At $2.38 do you really think you are doing your body, your wallet and your busy schedule any favors?

Some of you might remember that a couple of years ago I started assembling instant oatmeal packets for my husband to take to work. He's probably the ideal McDonald's target for convenience oatmeal (though he's smarter than that): a workaholic who never has time to eat breakfast at home. So I made up those instant oat / brown sugar / cinnamon / raisin / walnut packs for him, which he ate most mornings right up until we moved. (And we're still reusing those original plastic baggies!) Just the other day he told me he was ready to start packing again. One minute in the microwave at work, and bam! he's got breakfast at a fraction of McD's cost, and much, much healthier. Yes, it takes a little planning and advanced prep (and in our case, a wife who finds store-bought oatmeal packs off-putting). But if you divided the time it takes to assemble those packets by the number of breakfasts you get, it probably takes less than a minute per breakfast plus a minute to cook. In the end, it's so much better all around.

Good books for a potentially snowy day

Here in Portland, we are on snow-ma-geddon alert. Get this: there is a possibility of up to eight inches!! Today. So, batten down the hatches! Prepare for the worst! (We really like to blow things out of proportion here). In the case there is a snow day tomorrow, you probably should have some good books around. I've got some suggestions.


Feast your eyes on some really delicious looking curry paste recipes from Jamie's Food Revolution. You may remember Jamie Oliver from his Food Revolution TV show a while back. I personally got all excited about it, signed his petition, loved what he was doing, and then put it on my back-burner and totally forgot about him. Anyway, picked this cook book up at my library the other day, and I'm fired up to try out some of the recipes. I've always wanted to make more Indian food, especially vegetarian, but usually get bogged down by how complicated the recipes seem. These one seem do-able. Yay, Jamie Oliver!

Have you ever thought it would be cool to have a garden at your school? Just put it over there in that nice sunny spot? And then the kids can learn about growing food and stuff and wouldn't that be great? Well, I've had those kinds of vague thoughts. But the more I've learned about school gardens, you really need your ducks in a row. Lots of things to consider. How to Grow a School Garden sounds like a great book (I haven't read it yet). If your school is thinking about a school garden, this looks like a great starting point.

This last one is for the kids. From Portland's Maggie Rudy, The House that Mouse Built (on preorder through Amazon) features engaging scenes created from things the artist found, and then had photographed. Think of the 'I Spy' books of Walter Wick, crossed with the intricacy of Jan Brett illustrations. Big thanks to my friend Amy for letting me know about this book.

Care to share any good books you've recently read that would help fill a snow day?

Home solar: six-month status report


If our solar panels could talk, they might be saying: 'I think I can! I think I can!' Remember the book 'The Little Engine That Could'? Recently, I've had a few friends ask how it's going with the solar: 'Hmm. Well, I've been meaning to crunch those numbers. I'll get back to you on that. OK?'

So I looked at our electric bills of the past six months. Our photovoltaic solar system came online right at the beginning of September. I compared those bills to the bills from the same months (September to February of last year). And… survey says: 'Can we talk next summer?'

No, here's what I really found out. Some months are about the same. Some are drastically lower. Others are inexplicably more! During the past six months, we were billed for a total of 2493 kWh. In the same period a year ago, we were billed for 2692 kWh. So it's down overall! But not by much. In price, it's pretty much the same story. Our cumulative bill is $16.76 less than for the same six-month period last year. Not a really sunny picture (sorry, pun intended).

Thus, I return to my previous comment. I'll give you another update on the solar system in summer. We are hoping for a real summer this year. With sun. And warmth, and tomatoes and stuff. Stay tuned!

Dude, That's Not Food: Episode 1

Several months ago Renee half-jokingly floated the idea of starting DudeThatsNotFood.com and posting photos of typical food-type products you see at most grocery stores. She and I batted the idea around and ultimately decided that we'd probably just be setting ourselves up for a lawsuit-a-day. Well. Ahem. I thought I'd live on the edge today (but am not going to mention the product by name). This could very well be the first and last episode of Dude, That's Not Food. But I was at a grocery store this morning and couldn't resist taking a photo. Can you guess what this is?

 

This edible food-like product been around for several years, is geared towards kids and marketed to 'busy' moms (what mom do you know that isn't busy?), and it costs about $3.49 per package. I just checked some of the nutritional info online:

Fat: 13g

Sodium: 780mg (!)

Sugars: 31g

Carbs: 52g

Protein: 14g

It just. I mean. Who does? Really? WILL NO ONE THINK OF THE CHILDREN?

Did you guess this?

 

Look at all of those 'healthy food' catchphrases: whole grain, 100% white meat, 100% juice, excellent source of protein. Hey, sounds good, right? WELL IT'S NOT. And just looking at it has given me an awful case of caps-lock fever and a burning affliction of self-righteous indignation. C'mon people (but not you guys, certainly!). Don't feed your kids this crap for lunch. (And don't even get me started on the packaging.) The only point of this product is to make a bunch of people wealthy.

I make my son a ham (Niman Ranch, uncured) and cheese (organic) sandwich (100% whole wheat) and an apple (organic, Fuji) for lunch, and I know I spend less than $3.49 on that lunch and it's about FIVE MILLION times healthier. Because it's REAL FOOD.

Okay then! I'm not sure my blood pressure can handle another episode of Dude, That's Not Food, but the stores are chock full of similar products just waiting to be lambasted. It might be hard to resist...

Green Valentine's Day

Our children snuck into our bedroom at 5am, armed with paper-chain hearts, hand-written valentines, and a roll of tape. So far, it's been about the sweetest, simplest Valentine's Day you could wish for.

I'm not going to lie to you. This whole relocating-and-starting-over-in-a-strange-new-town business has not been easy. Plastic bag ban inches forward in Oregon

In 1971, Oregon became the first state to pass a bottle bill. Will it be the first state to ban plastic single use check-out bags? Oregon Senate Bill 536 amazingly has the support of environmentalists, the grocery industry, a bi-partisan group of legislators and business lobbyists. So it's looking good. Of course not everyone is for it. Officials from the plastics industry are working hard to see it fail. The Oregonian published a great pro/con story on the proposed bag ban today.

Here's the high level summary: Senate Bill 536 would stop most retailers from using single use plastic check-out bags, and require them to charge 5 cents for paper bags. Pharmacies and restaurants would be excepted.

The pros of banning the bags:

  • Plastic bag litter clogs sewers, recycling machinery and are easily blown in the wind.
  • A plastic bag ban would greatly encourage reuseable cloth bags, and possibly paper bags (which are made in our fair state).
  • A statewide ban would simplify any local bag prohibitions, that can be confusing to consumers.

The cons:

  • People love plastic bags! They are the best for lining your trash can and picking up pet waste. And they only account for a small amount of litter.
  • Plastic bags are made in the US. Those pesky, unsanitary reuseable bags are made in China. (please note sarcasm).
  • The 5 cent charge on paper bags equates a bag tax (this one brought to you by the plastics lobby).

If you feel strongly that banning single-use plastic bags is the way to go for Oregon, write or call your elected officials and tell them just that. This legislation is still in committe, but stay tuned. It's the biggest piece of environmental pie being served up this session.

Reuse ideas? Buried in old cotton socks & t-shirts

Betty, from Springfield, PA, has a challenge. Perhaps you can relate:

I LOVE your website and really admire what the two of you do in making the world a greener place.  I am also trying to follow your advice and live a greener life and run a greener household.  My problem is this - I refuse to throw out old cotton socks and t-shirts (the ones that are not in good enough shape to be donated/handed down/etc.) but have no idea what to do with them.  I have garbage bags filled with them!  Are there organizations out there that can use them for rags or something?!?

If you are like Heather, you might make whimsical crafts from them. It is what she gave me for Christmas with this little note attached:

Don't you hate it when you open the pantry and see a mish-mash of canned food labels with garish colors and competing fonts? Aren't you embarrassed to let guests see your Hunt's canned tomato sauce? Here's your solution! Decorative Canned Food Cozies! Featuring our new Cheerful Pom-Pom and Drunken Party Girl designs (pictured)!

I laughed so hard after opening that gift that I nearly wet myself. But that's only two socks. It sounds like Betty has a bigger challenge on her hands.

I find old t-shirts make great hankys and lunch-box napkins. Just cut them into the square sizes you like, and they don't need hemming. I also will use cotton t-shirt scraps for patching holes in the knees of jeans (by hand-sewing onto the reverse side).

Bet let us brainstorm. Tell us, dear readers. How do you reuse old cotton socks and t-shirts? Do you know of reuse organizations that will take them as rags?
