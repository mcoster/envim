My family went to Charleston and all I bought were three stupid postcards

And I'm feeling pretty darn okay with that! Seriously, don't you always feel tempted, compelled to buy something 'to remind' you of your vacation? And in a place like Charleston, South Carolina where there is plenty to buy (I'm talking to you, seagrass baskets), temptation lurks everywhere. Boutiques, the City Market, national monuments and plantation gift shops -- there are ample opportunities to bring home a souvenir. But this trip, for me, was not about bringing back stuff. It was about seeing, eating and rinsing sand off of my feet. I have a camera full of pastel-colored visions, a belly full of fried green tomatoes (oh dear God), and the freckles on my nose as a reminder of all that we experienced and loved about our spring break excursion. That's really all I need. And I never even got around to mailing the postcards. (Sorry, peeps!)


Don't get me wrong. I do love some items that I've bought during my travels that remind me of where I've been, and I do love supporting local artisans. (Note: we did support a lot of local restaurants.) But it was freeing to resist the call of consumerism. To be able to sail past those market tables and not give in, boo-yah. We really have all we need right now (well, except a house of our own, but that's whole 'nother story). I'm not looking to buy something for the sake of buying something just to create more clutter in my life. You?

As an aside, Charleston was awesome. It had everything: history, beauty, food, sun. Highly recommend.

Happy birthday, to one fabulous garbage can!

As I get older, I spend more and more time muttering to myself, 'just where does the time go?' For example, it was 4 years ago that our family first went on a garbage diet. Going from weekly garbage pickup, to monthly. Four years, reader. Four years!


Now I will confess that just about every month, right before our pickup day I have a minor freakout. I start to wonder about all those unnecessary things hidden behind closet doors, under beds, stashed in linen closets. Don't even get me started about storage areas or the garage. I know you know the things. Various rag bags. The seemingly endless supply of temporary tattoos my kids acquire. Stickers. Ribbons. Old mylar birthday balloons that I mean to use as wrapping paper, but usually forget I have. Little bits of odd assorted stuff. That could have a reuse purpose. Or could perhaps be better organized. Or perhaps really need to be thrown away.

So there's this dual impulse. Keep saving all those assorted odds and ends, to creatively REUSE them! Or, THROW IT AWAY ALREADY, and fill up that danged garbage can, because you only get one chance each month and may as well fill that bad-boy up the brim and get your money's worth! And in truth, what happens most often is I close those doors and walk quickly away. So much easier than actually dealing with it all.

Happily, I've never once missed a single garbage day since going to this once-a-month scheme. Our pickup is on the first Friday of every month, at the crack of dawn (which was last Friday, if you're paying close attention -- it's just that I didn't get around to writing about it until this Friday). I remember each pickup day simply by putting a note on our calendar (I know I'd be kicking myself if I forgot). It's cool that this low-garbage diet works over the long haul. That it fits our needs. We can put the recycling out whenever we need to, which seems to average about twice a month for our family.

Do you limit your family to one garbage can a month? How does the garbage-lite diet work for you?

A winter salad for Mom (and you)



I don't typically yearn for salad when it's 35 degrees out, but that's probably because my idea of salad involves vegetables that simply aren't in season when it's 35 degrees. So when I stumble onto a 'cozy' salad that's light on the out-of-season stuff, then I'm pretty intrigued. This is that salad. And if I were still in Portland I'd be making this salad for my mom, right after she beat the pants off me in Scrabble (or vice versa). We'd lick our wounds/celebrate sweet victory with a microbrew and then I'd turn on the oven. This salad invites some of Mom's favorite foods to the party: sweet potatoes, black beans, cilantro, limes. It's meaty without the meat, has a nice kick of flavor with a bit of crunch tossed in for good measure. I've made it twice now for dinner and my husband and I both agree it's a keeper. (And no, my kids won't touch it.)

Another thing I love about this recipe is that it came from my (new) friend Molly, who posted it over at Edible Columbus, so it's something of a bridge between my western and eastern life. And if you're looking to sink in to a fresh food blog, you might want to visit Molly's -- Remedial Eating. Great stuff there. Visit the Edible Columbus blog for the salad recipe!

PS: Only 12 more days until Spring.

EnviroGripe: Dish towels that don't dry dishes!

I will start with a positive. We are moving forward with our kitchen remodel. My husband and I have been working on a checklist over that past few months. Yes, we move very slowly. But we're now both (literally) on the same page on what a kitchen remodel needs to accomplish. We've listed what we like about our current kitchen (seriously, only two measly things). What we don't like about out kitchen: about 50 things. Hopes, dreams. Crazy ideas. Concerns. Real goals. And we've been meeting with professionals this week who can help us bring this project to fruition. So that's the positive.

On the negative… I have an EnviroGripe. New Category? I think so. One of the major topics of discussion about the remodel is the lack of storage space in a 1942 kitchen, and how we'd like to maximize efficiency this next go-around. Which has totally gotten me thinking about just what lurks in my cabinets and drawers. Truth be told, I have some weeding out to do. We surely have more dishes than we need. More spoons and spatulas. Rummage sale items that seemed too good to pass up, and then just end up taking space.

But back to my EnviroGripe (I really enjoy using that word). I have a stack of dish towels THAT DON'T WORK! Sure, you can dry your hands on them. But dry a dish? Forget about it. These towels don't absorb water. They just move water around, and said water sticks stubbornly to the dish! Who are these towel makers who make defective towels? Shouldn't its ability to dry a dish be tested or deemed somewhat important in the making of a dish towel? I feel like the Lorax! I speak for the dishes!! Or the dish dryers. Or the cotton that sacrificed its fluff to become these lame-o towels.

The only dish towels I have that actually dry dishes are pure white, and consequently, I almost never use them, except when drying (hand wash only) wine glasses. The rest of the time I use my frustratingly defective dish towels and curse their very existence. How do you find a dish towel that actually dries worth a darn? And since we're on this very scintillating and important topic, how do you exist without a dish rack? I see many minimalist kitchens without them, and always mean to ask, but usually forget. If people are hand drying instead of using a dish rack, surely they have found some dish towels that work!

We're taking it easy, but welcome your questions and ideas

I just realized that it was about four years ago that Renee and I launched EnviroMom. We'd both decided that we needed to make changes to our lifestyles (and drag our respective families along with us) and figured that there were other folks who needed encouragement as well. We started out by going through Portland's excellent Master Recycler program, had some amazing media coverage along the way (thank you, Nightline) and built a community that has been overwhelmingly supportive and positive. We are so darn thankful.

My cross-country move has been a bit more, um, challenging than I expected, and I'm finding it difficult to come up with anything original to write about these days. Maybe the winter doldrums have something to do with it since Renee is feeling it, too. So we've decided that we're not going to stick to any kind of posting schedule (believe it or not we've always had one, though it's not been as noticeable lately). We'll only post when we feel we have something of value to share. But! We encourage you to email us -- enviromompdx [at] gmail [dot] com -- whenever you have questions to ask or ideas to share. We do love spreading our Master Recycler reduce / reuse / recycle knowledge and other green living tidbits we've picked up over the years and are always happy to try and help problem-solve when we can. We're not going away, just taking it easy, and we hope you'll let us know if you need any green living support. Thanks, all!
