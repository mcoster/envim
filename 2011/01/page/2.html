When did kids forget how to play?


Photo by my (then) 5-year old

I miss our old playroom. Back in Portland we had a big space in our house dedicated to play, and that's where you would mostly find my kids: building, creating, zooming, imagining and making mayhem. It was perfect. Here, in this small rental home that we'll be living in for another few months, there is no playspace beyond their bedrooms, and they are feeling frustrated. (It's hard to race matchbox cars over wall-to-wall carpet.) It's not that our kids have oodles of toys that we need the space, it's just that they need the space to play the way they like to play: setting up a restaurant or a school room or a vet clinic; building a huge city out of blocks and train tracks and Littlest Pet Shops (and leaving it up for days); running, jumping, dancing, putting on a variety act. We're trying to adjust, and we grown-ups are trying to be more tolerant of play in our small living room. But. I find it's sometimes easier to give in to screen time when the kids come to me, frustrated with the limitations of this house. 

(Though now that I think about it, they are actually spending more time outside in this big, flat yard. It's cold here, but it's dry, and the snow is fun. Ah, the silver lining.)

I recently read an excellent, albeit disturbing article in the NY Times about a 'movement to restore play' at home and school. (I suspect these 'movements' are often manufactured by the media, but this article is still worth a read.) The article gives examples of parents who want perfect mess-free homes, or don't want the noise that comes with play, so they encourage screen-based activities to maintain order and sanity. So now you've got all of these kids who don't know how to entertain themselves. Over the years we've had playdates with kids who don't know how to play, and they are challenging and seldom repeated. Everything is 'boring' and no one is happy and then I end up having to provide the entertainment. It's sad.

Some schools aren't helping matters, either. I'm always shocked when I read about elementary schools that have abolished recess. The article talks about one mom who tried to bring it back to her school, but was denied by both officials and other parents. They didn't want to deal with unruliness or potential liability or take time away from maintaining high test scores. Our new school (like our old one) has three recesses each day (wahoo!) while also managing to be exceptional academically. What gives?

Creative play that is not structured or led by grown-ups helps kids learn how to work together, solve problems, resolve conflicts and stretch their imaginations -- all vital skills that will serve them well as adults. I wish more parents and schools would just let kids be kids.

What do your kids like to play? How have you set up your home for play? Does your school have unstructured recess?

Alternative gift bags!

I will never be mistaken for a person who wraps presents beautifully. Oh, there was a time when it was all about wrapping paper. So I learned how to make nice straight corner folds and somehow managed to contain my childish desire to overuse tape. But with the advent of near waste-free living, and the adoption of reusable cloth gift bags, more often than not, my wrapped presents usually look pretty terrible. In fact, they look like they were wrapped by a child. So, I've handed over present wrapping responsibilities to the children!

My oldest daughter's birthday is this week, so we are wrapping gifts. They are not especially pretty. But! We have a nice little discovery to share. If your gift bags are not big enough for the wrapping job at hand, your linen closet may come to your rescue, as it did for us. Voila! The pillow case gift bag. Featuring seasonal forest green in flannel, for that cozy as a pillow present! As Heather has said before, all you need is ribbon. And the help of a 6-year-old.

Help me give up my kitchen sponge

For all of my adult life I've used a kitchen sponge. I like the size of a sponge, the convenience of the coarse scrubber on one side and the soft sponge on the other, and the way you can make it all soapy. But I am an irresponsible sponge-user. I don't disinfect it or microwave it or run it through the dishwasher. I just use it until it's gross and then (gasp) throw it away. It's a habit I'm ready to shake. I've known for years that sponges are laden with bacteria, but for whatever reason I've simply never been concerned about it. Frankly, I'm still not terribly concerned (why is that?), but giving up the sponge seems like the responsible thing to do. Ahem.

So what's the best solution? Dishrag? Scrub brush? Spaghetti scrubber? What tools, preferably of the non-disposable variety, do you use to wash your pots and pans, your counter tops and appliances?

Finally! Opted Out of Receiving New Phone Books


I was walking home after volunteering at my kids' school yesterday, and was greeted with a familiar sight: a stack of new phone books in a plastic bag, at the base of every mailbox. Urgh.

Lo and behold, my mailbox was bare. Now, I've gotten overly excited having come upon something similar in the past. I've thought that I was exempted from delivery, only to find out later that my new phone books were placed by our garage door or front door. But this time, it appears that a true miracle has happened. I've finally been listened to. My phone book opt-out worked.

Problem is, I took a fairly shotgun approach to opting out of unwanted phone books. I know I filled out forms on the Web and called 800 numbers several times over the past few years. So I cannot pinpoint one specific act that will get you out of receiving new phone books. But I will emphasize that something finally worked. So, keep trying. Have faith. Maybe someday, you too will cease to receive phone books. And then, sort of like Steve Martin in 'The Jerk' you can exclaim: 'The new phone books aren't here!'

(And just in case your're wondering, yes, I did feel like a dork roaming my neighborhood looking for an unattended phone book bag to photograph.)

How to soak up bacon grease with a clear conscience

Every once in awhile we peek into our inbox and actually find a good question from a genuine reader (versus the endless PR product pitches). Here's one from Erin:

Do you have any good solutions to the problem of bacon grease? I haven't bought paper towels to drain the greasy stuff in years. We don't use paper bags (although if we slip and have one from the store we use that and it works just fine). We don't use newspapers either (yet again, the occasional Willamette Week from the grocery store is more than adequate!) Older kitchen towels were the staple for awhile, but they get nasty fast and we cant seem to wash the grease off them. So what do we non- paper product people use to absorb the grease? Any ideas??

Once you've given up disposable paper products in the kitchen, it's really hard to cut yourself a break and use them (sparingly) on occasion. But this is exactly what I did a few months ago after having a conversation with Renee about bacon grease. Up to then I'd been blotting our bacon with a towel, like Erin, and then tossing it in the wash (and yes, they always remained a little sticky). Renee gently suggested I give up that practice since there really isn't any difference between washing grease down the drain (no-no) and putting it through the laundry. Ah.

Now I keep a stealth roll of recycled-content paper towels in the dark recesses under our sink, out of sight for everyday use, but there when we really, really need it. But it's recently occurred to me that there might be an even better solution: digging into the rag bag. I don't know about you, but we go through A LOT of t-shirts around here. Whether they be pitted out or stained or ripped beyond repair (or donation), I am constantly shaking my head and tossing more into the rag bag. There are only so many rags you can use, so why not put them through the wash, cut them up into smaller pieces, keep them on hand for greasy clean-ups and then throw them away? It really is okay to throw some stuff away, particularly after it's exhausted its useful life.

I'm also really keen to try baking bacon in the oven. I've read you can bake it by placing the bacon on a broiler pan so that the grease drips through the slats. Once the grease has cooled and solidified, you could just scrape it out into the garbage (or save it for future cooking, if you're into that).

Any other suggestions? The comments in this post from a few months ago also have some great insight into cleaning up gross messes.

Time for some green new year's resolutions!

I'm a pretty goal oriented person, so I've got nothing against new year's resolutions. Some years I focus on one big resolution. Other years, smaller or private ones. This year I'm noodling over a few medium sized goals:

1. Actually grow fruit on my fruit trees instead of just keeping them on life support. Here's what has happened so far... We bought some fruit trees in the past few years. We planted them following the helpful suggestions from the tree grower. After that. Hmm. Well. Um. Did you ever notice that trees don't come with owner's manuals? And I'm pretty much clueless. I did have a chat with a master gardener at our farmers' market back in summer, and they said fruit trees should be sprayed twice a year in November and February, which I confess, I have already blown as far as November was concerned because it was just too darned cold and rainy. However, I did go to the library this weekend and now have a book about growing citrus. I will try to get my little potted lemon and lime trees off life support this year, and into fruit production mode. And I will do that February spraying if it kills me!

2. I need to get out of my winter produce rut. Maybe you're afflicted with something similar? You go to the store and buy the same darn things, regardless of where they come from or if they are in season or what they cost, simply because you know how to make them and are mostly certain that your family with eat them. Right now, for better or worse, I buy broccoli, apples, pears and carrots. Boring. Lame-o. Now, I know I could really push myself to a higher level by subscribing to a produce delivery box like those offered by Organics to You or Spud. But I'm scared. I keep stalling. I keep sticking to my rut. Help me.

3. Get rid of stuff. Being a Master Recycler and minimal producer of garbage, there's this part of me that hates to throw anything away. I'm not a hoarder. It's not that sick. But there comes a point when enough is enough. I need to be a little more selective. I've got to accept the fact that we can no longer recycle mylar here in Portland and either find a really fantastic reuse for it (or reuse org), or get rid of it already. Same with a lot of stuff. Clearing out feels so good, especially at this time of year.

4. Remodel our kitchen greenly and affordably. We're in the very early planning stages. Super, SUPER exciting. But, let me remind myself that it's in the EARLY planning stages. So I must contain said excitement and remember to work on the other previously stated goals.

I could probably go on and on. Lots of things to work on this year. And you? Did you have a happy, happy holiday season? Are you super ready to greenify your new year's resolutions? Please share what you are working on. It's great to be back and get the EnviroMom conversation started again.
