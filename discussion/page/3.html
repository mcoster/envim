Brown thumb seeks green thumb for possible love connection

So how does one become a gardener? I have big plans this year. Last year's flower garden will become this year's produce garden. I have visions of lettuces, herbs, carrots, melons, berries and bananas and coconuts.

Wait, what was that? Bananas and coconuts only grow in the tropics? OK, well scratch the bananas and coconuts then.

That's the thing. I don't know nuthin' bout plantin' no gardens. And I find the endeavor intimidating. Last year I think I got fairly lucky as a first-timer growing my peas, beans and blueberries. But I do think I need to move my blueberry to a better sun spot.

Heather suggested today that one becomes a gardener by trial and error. And that's the problem. I don't want to mess with the "error" part. I just want to dig in, plant some perfect plants and see my garden grow by the bushel-full. I don't want to read a book about it. Or watch a video. I think I need a gardening mentor.

Does such a thing exist? Should I place a personal ad in the paper: "brown thumb seeks green thumb for possible love connection and lots of produce?" I'm aware of the Growing Gardens program helping out lower income people in the Portland area. But what about for us middle-class folks? Is there something similar?

Beg, borrow or barter

While bartering is often considered a thing of the past, it's starting to become my favorite way to strike a deal. Saturday night, while talking about sewing, a friend offered to babysit both of my kids for several hours if I'd hem up some pants for her. Are you kidding? Deal! My husband is also no stranger to bartering. He's one of those Mac gurus, and often gets roped into helping friends and family with technical difficulties. To him their technical difficulties are easy. So a few hours of desktop support equals a few hours of babysitting our darling little moppets. Can you tell what our currency would be called if it ever came to that? MasterCard, Visa or will you be using your Babysit card? I also recently repaired a hook on a friend's bra that got all messed up in the washing machine. She's sworn to use a lingerie bag next time, and the fix was easy-peasy for me. Plus, given how expensive bras are, you can't just throw the baby out with the bathwater if you know what I mean. This was so super easy, I actually just volunteering to do it, no strings attached, but she did offer to loan me her Walt Disney World guide when they get back from a family vacation this month (as we are going there in July). Gosh I love bartering. Don't you?

Greening up a playgroup: taking it a step further

Heather posted a bit ago about how to green up a playgroup. Mostly we discussed ways to limit waste in the food and drink aspects of playgroups. We were thinking a little further about the topic, and came up with additional suggestions if you wanted to make "green goals" a part of your playgroup's focus. Here are some of the activities that could be integrated:

  • Organizing swaps on a regular basis, that could include clothing, diapers, gear, books, toys, games, puzzles, etc. It'll teach kids about the value of reuse, and that swapping can be just as much fun as shopping for new, since they'll come home with stuff that's new to them.
  • Farm and food field trips: u-pick farms for seasonal fruits. Getting back in touch with our food supply! Follow up with a canning party. Heather, our friend April and I canned apple butter last fall. It was a blast, and we've been enjoying delicious apple butter all winter.
  • Pool resources: join a CSA with a bunch of friends and share recipes.
  • Have home field trips/teach-ins: share what you know about composting, permaculture, canning, raising urban chickens, etc. with your playgroup in the hope that it will inspire others to try these things in their homes. You can also brainstorm on solutions for green living challenges.
  • Pool and share magazine subscriptions. If one of you gets Tessy & Tab, another Highlights, and another Nick Jr., you can all read and pass along magazines after you've finished, so that everyone shares one copy instead of each subscribing individually.
  • Have a sewing bee, or a knitting bee: Teach what you know about knitting to your groups of friends, or teaching how to darn socks, mend clothing or patch the knees of jeans. Or get together with friends to sew cloth gift bags before the holiday season to reduce paper waste.
  • Everyone pitch in with some ingredients and mix up home-made household "greener cleaners" with castile soap, glycerine, vinegar, water, etc. Kind of like a science experiment, and you get to take home your share of what is made.

Do you have any green activity ideas that you can add to this list?

EnviroMom green resolutions for 2008!

Thanks to everyone who shared their green resolutions for the PH12.6 drawing! I'm so inspired! Sometimes you might think, "what difference does it really make?" But then your actions influence others, who influence others and suddenly you've got a ton of people making a difference. Check out a few of these resolutions:

  • We're finally moving to our house in St. John's this summer, and will be able to set up our rainbarrels, our compost bin, and get a decent swath of yard dedicated to garden.  As well, we're building a chicken tractor and hope to have some hens to settle into it.  We're also finally finishing the upstairs floors (ripping out the carpeting, ick) with aglia (sp?  have never seen it in print, but it's a non-toxic wood finish from what used to be EBS that we have on the floor downstairs).  Oh!  And I'm cloth diapering our babe, so I finally get to set up a stash of my own prefolds (probably new, but I'll be looking for unbleached at least, organic if I can find/afford it) and can wash them and line dry them in the back yard.
  • One of my resolutions is to cut down on waste products. I got a waffle maker for Christmas so now I make waffles and freeze them for the kids and have made protein bars for my son's lunch (he likes them too!). I just bought the Diva cup, haven't tried it yet:). I am so much more aware of packaging since reading your website. For example I used to buy single serving apple sauce cups, now I buy a jar and put single servings into tupperware for lunch.
  • In 2008, I want to cut down on our family's use of paper products - napkins and paper towels.  I also want to rededicate myself to composting our kitchen scraps!
  • I have made many strides in 2007, thanks in a large part to you at Enviromom, to change the way we live.  But we have a long way to go.  I have done a lot in 2007 to reduce/eliminate our use of paper plates, plastic utensils, paper napkins, and other disposables.  So my concerns are about cleaning the reusable items that we use more of now.  My goals for 2008 are to switch entirely to "green" cleaning products including my dish detergent, laundry detergent, and household cleaners. My other goal for 2008 is to commute by bike (with daughter in tow) to work and back home starting in the spring.  Much easier said than done, but I think I'm up for the challenge.
  • Walk (don't drive to the mailbox); Be more diligent about composting; Plant a more efficient garden; Use our cloth bags EVERYWHERE, not just the grocery store.
  • My green resolution for 2008 is to recycle more.  Collect those hard to recycle items and get them to a master recycling pickup.  Get recycling bins in our bathrooms.  Use the cloth diapers I have for my daughter more often.  Use ALL the produce in my weekly bin before it goes bad.  Drive less.  Walk more.  Plant a vegetable garden.  :)  I guess that's a little more than just recycling...but there are just so many things!!

Learning to do without

I have a problem digesting lactose. It seemed to get much worse after my pregnancies. The simple answer would be to stop drinking cow milk. I didn't. I started drinking lactose free-milk. Recently they started charging $4.39 per half gallon of lactose free milk at my regular store. I refused to pay that when the milk my kids drink is $4.99 for an organic whole gallon. So I bought some tablets in bulk at Costco to help me digest lactose. And started drinking the regular cow milk. Only sometimes I forget to take the tablets, and then there's hell to pay in my gastro-intestinal system.

This situation, which I struggle with, reminds me of a story in the Oregonian the other day about exporting Oregon Christmas trees to Hawaii. They've had to quarantine the tree containers due to wasp infestations, and the odd snake here and there. Of course, the simple answer would be: don't export Christmas trees to Hawaii. Instead they are creating new shaking procedures so that the wasps don't try to hitch a ride to Hawaii, and impact the ecosystem there.

It seems as a nation we need to learn to do without. Of course, that means I need to kick my morning cafe au lait habit. And the jury's still out on that one. Maybe $4.39 isn't so expensive after all... I'm reading Blessed Unrest right now. I'm hoping to be inspired by the movement. Do you think there's hope that our civilization can learn to do without on a broad scale?

Fun with pinecones, sidewalk chalk and letters

Having the kids at home this week has really put a cramp into my blogging schedule. I usually brainstorm my topics, write 'em and post 'em in the fleeting hour between when my 3-year-old takes her nap and my 5-year-old comes home from kindergarten on the school bus. But we did do something kind of fun while the little one was napping today. We wrote out some letters on the driveway, then outlined them using some pine cones we'd gathered on a nature walk earlier this month.

We thought about writing "gobble, gobble" or "happy turkey day" but it was a little too chilly and windy to spend much time outside today!! At the very least, it'll be a nice greeting for Cheap-o as he rides home on his newly tuned up used bike, recently purchased off Craigslist (he was able to justify the cost of the bike from money saved on gasoline). Sadly, he did have to drive his car to work during the severe rainy days of earlier this week, and filled his gas tank a few days shy of Thanskgiving. But he's been a real trooper, having only filled his gas tank twice in the past two seasons. He's ridden his bike on a lot of days I would have easily made excuses and opted for the car. So as we celebrate Thanksgiving this year, I'm thankful for:

  • a husband who bike rides for health and gas-savings
  • two lovely little girls who ask me so many questions, I think my brain is going to explode, but always make me laugh and smile
  • beloved family both near and far
  • good friends who come here, share ideas and challenges, and teach me a whole lot about being green...

Happy Thanksgiving!! What are you thankful for?

Attempts to capture the fleeting lovely fall color of leaves...

While helping at my daughter's kindergarten class today, the teaching assistant and I were talking about the beautiful fall weather and the striking color of the leaves the kids were picking up and making into bouquets. I was wondering out loud about the accidental color impressions leaves sometimes make on sidewalks. I imagine it has something to do with water and sun leaching the color out. So I figured we'd be able to wet down some leaves, throw a hot iron over it, and extract some pretty colors onto white fabric. No dice. We tried dousing with the fabric and leaves with water and vinegar, but didn't try anything stronger than that.

Ultimately, we took a permanent marker and outlined the leaf patterns onto the fabric (which my 5-year-old embellished with smiley faces in the center). But my interest is piqued... I wonder if I could fix up some kind of experiment with a wet leaf under glass, and keep it in the sun for a while, and see if that would transfer any color to the fabric. I did a google search and didn't find much on the subject. Does anyone have any ideas? Or is this the reason people flock to those New England forests, because seeing it during that one time of year is the only possible way to view those colors...

Can any crafty EnviroMoms shed some light or throw some ideas toward my experiment of trying to transfer the color of fall leaves onto fabric?

Ripening tomatoes on the vine in the garage

OK. So I took the advice of several readers and hung my tomato vines in the garage to ripen them. (It's gotten a bit too chilly here at night for them to keep growing in the garden and not split/rot). So I hung them in the garage. Yes, it's a miracle. They do ripen. My girls thought ol' mom and dad had really lost it when they saw us hanging plant vines from the broom rack. But here's the surprise of the deal: the vines are decomposing slowly but surely leaving my garage smelling like... wet dog. Is that not one of the worst smells in the world? So we picked as many little tomatoes as we could, and chucked the vines into the compost heap over the weekend. Sadly, the stench of eau de wet dog still lingers in the garage. I know you green moms would not recommend I spritz my garage with Oust. So how do I get rid of that nasty, stinky stench?
