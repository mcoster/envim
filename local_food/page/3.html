Greening up the holidays

The Give Green! Holiday Food & Gift Festival is this weekend, November 17 & 18 at the Doubletree Hotel Lloyd Center. The festival will feature local, sustainable, handmade and organic gifts just in time for the holidays. Sounds like fun. 

Are you giving green this year? Care to share some gift ideas? I was thinking that Sigg or Klean Kanteen water bottles would make perfect gifts -- very cool, healthy and priced just high enough that some people wouldn't buy one for themselves. Over the summer I bought some mango pepper jelly at the Hillsdale Farmers Market from the Crabapple & Company stand, which was OUTSTANDING. They also have pear pepper jelly. Just unusual enough (and local!) for a good gift. (And they'll be at the market through Christmas.) A cute organic kid's cotton t-shirt presented in a fabric gift bag would be awfully sweet. Any gift ideas you'd like to share?

Found it! A local sandwich bread my kids will eat

Several years ago I started buying Oroweat 100% Whole Wheat bread. I love it. It's big and soft and has a hint of sweetness to it. It's the only sandwich bread I fed my kids, and I've always given myself a little pat on the back for getting them to love whole wheat bread. Then one day I read the ingredients. Can you guess what the third ingredient is? High fructose corn syrup! Are you kidding me? Why, oh why? Apparently that's why it tastes so good.

So we've been on the hunt for alternatives. Tried the New Seasons whole wheat sandwich bread -- son and I loved it, daughter hated it. Tried Dave's Killer Bread -- husband and I love it, kids hate it (but it's pretty expensive, so that's fine with me). Then one day I noticed a loaf of Franz Big Horn Valley 100% Whole Wheat Bread. And they proudly proclaim on the wrapper: No High Fructose Corn Syrup! And all the ingredients are natural. Bonus: it's made here in Portland. The kids like it, I like it (though it's not as soft as the Oroweat) and so our search is over. What are you slathering PB & J on these days?

Making apple butter

Renee and I and our friend, April, came together Sunday afternoon for a good old-fashioned apple butter-making party. There is nothing, I mean nothing, like spreading homemade apple butter on a slice of warm toast in the morning or swirling it into a steaming bowl of oatmeal. Mmm. With the holidays approaching (egad) it never hurts to have a few jars of homemade preserves on hand for gifts of gratitude. So, armed with peelers, jars, canning equipment and 30 pounds of Mt. Hood Fuji apples, we got to work.

I had never canned before, but Renee and April had, so it was a great learning experience for me. I don't think I would have had the guts to do it on my own (I have a vague memory of a jar of freshly canned tomato sauce exploding in the kitchen of my childhood). The classic recipe came from the red gingham-covered Better Homes & Gardens cookbook that called for basic ingredients: sugar, cinnamon, cloves, allspice, apple cider and apples. We sat around my old red dinette table, peeling, coring, slicing, drinking wine, noshing and gabbing (nothin' like it).

Seven hours later...we had 48 half-pint jars of creamy apple goodness. Yep, it took awhile (good thing we like one another). Most of the time we just sat around waiting for either the apples to boil down and soften or the mixture to simmer and thicken. The actual canning part went pretty quickly. In assembly line fashion, one of us filled the jars, one wipe them clean and pulled freshly-sterilized jars from the dish washer, and one put on the lids. Five minutes in the hot-water bath, et voila, homemade apple butter.

I think altogether we spent $40 on ingredients and extra jars, which comes out to about 83 cents a jar. Money savings aside, it was a wonderful way to preserve some of the bounty of Oregon, stock our cabinets and gift bags with a tasty spread, and enjoy the company of good friends.

Storing local apples

My 3-year old, who has never been fond of fruit, has begun a love affair with apples. It's like someone turned on a switch, and it's all he wants. Sometimes just plain apples sliced up, sometimes slathered in peanut butter. I could not be happier. We're smack into apple season right now, and I just bought loads of Hood River Fuji apples to make apple butter. (Oregon's apple season ends in early November.) Now I'm thinking about buying loads more to store for the winter. Until a few months ago I didn't pay that much attention to food origins, so I can't remember whether the grocery stores carry Oregon apples through the winter. It seems like they might carry Washington apples.

I bought my Hood River apples out at The Pumpkin Patch on Sauvie Island for 59 cents a pound, which seems like a fantastic price (though they weren't organic). There were some organic varieties at the farmers market for $1.50/pound last week. I've been doing some research on apple storage and have come up with a couple of different storage suggestions:

Backwoods Home Magazine suggests wrapping each individual apple in newspaper and storing them in a cardboard box in a cool spot, like an unheated garage or enclosed porch. The apples need to be in perfect condition -- no bruises or holes -- and should keep for 3-4 months. A number of other sites suggested this storage technique.

eHow says to store perfect apples in a plastic bag in the crisper compartment of the refrigerator, but they will only keep for 6 weeks.

Have you ever successfully stored apples for 3-4 months? Any tips and techniques to share? Gotta keep my boy in apples, and I'd love it if they were local!

Reducing waste in the fast-food nation

We try not to eat out often. Part of my concern is the cost. Part of my concern is the question of where the food comes from, and how nutritious it really is. Part of it is that my kids seem to then want to eat out all the time. But mostly it's the waste that is generated in the restaurant business.

Having taken the Master Recycler course, I have a better understanding of how challenging it is for a Portland Metro restaurant to implement composting and recycling plans: Unlike with residential waste/recycling haulers who use the hauler that is assigned to their geographical area, businesses bid for haulers from all over the area, and typically hire one that gives them the best price. So myriad haulers criss-cross all over town, serving only the businesses they have contracted with, and sometimes a hauler's offerings for recycling/composting are slim to none.

So it's beyond exciting to hear about Burgerville's plan to expand a composting and recycling pilot to all 39 of their restaurants.  Burgerville is an established leader in local food purchasing, recycling cooking oil for biodiesel production, and a big customer of renewable power. This move really takes them one step further down the road to sustainability and makes them even more of a role model. They won my heart with their killer turkey burger, and they keep me coming back with each additional green-thinking business decision they implement. It's inspiring to see that restaurants can find a way to be green despite the challenges they face.

Farm to School Web portal launched

Check this out! Farm to School is a Web portal that details every school lunch program in the country working to bring more local food to students. Click on your state and read about what schools and districts are doing, who you should contact for more information, upcoming events and other information. It's heartening that so many states have active programs. This description of Portland Public School's program jumped out at me:

The district has engaged in a visioning process to work towards making healthy, nutritious foods readily available to all students, with significant goals such as 100% of menu items prepared regionally; 50% of menu items sourced locally; hormone, antibiotic and nitrate free meats to the extent possible; dairy products that are free of recombinant bovine growth hormone; fish products from sustainable sources; and fruits and vegetables that are sustainably or organically grown.

Just by chance I checked out Vermont's programs and discovered VT FEED (Vermont Food Education Every Day). They use a three pronged approach to local food education:

1. The Classroom, with standard-based food, farm, and nutrition-based curriculum that involves in-depth graduate level teacher training, including summer workshops and in-class mentoring;

2. The Cafeteria, through the integration of local food and nutrition education through food service institutes, in-cafeteria mentoring, and peer support;

3. The Community, through reconnecting people to their food sources- the farms and farmers.

All very exciting stuff.

The true cost of eggs

Can you tell which egg I bought from the grocery store (Wild Oats) and which egg came from the Hillsdale Farmers Market via Kookoolan Farms? The one from the store was a brown, cage-free, hormone-free, vegetarian-feed fed chicken egg, from whereabouts unknown, that probably cost about $3/dozen. The one from Kookoolan Farms, 35 minutes away in Yamhill, Oregon, was brown, laid by a pasture-raised chicken, and was probably plucked from the nest within 48 hours before I bought it. A dozen eggs from Kookoolan Farms cost $5. For added perspective, I drove by a Walgreens today that advertised eggs for 79 cents.

That's a pretty wide range! Why would someone spend $5 when they could spend less than $1 on eggs? Well, for one thing, I know where this egg came from, and I know how the chicken was raised. I know the farmers, Chrissie and Koorosh Zaerpoor, that raised the chicken, and thanks to last week's article in the Oregonian's FoodDay, I know what they went through to bring those eggs and chickens to my table. This is without a doubt the closest relationship I've ever had with protein. $5 seems like a bargain.

Many would argue that most people can't afford $5 for a dozen eggs. And frankly, it's pretty hard to find fresh eggs anyways, so there's simply not enough to go around (I have to get to the market within 30 minutes of opening to get those Kookoolan eggs before they sell out). But, what if that is just what eggs cost? Would people really stop buying them? (I really don't know -- just thinking out loud here.) I have to believe that lifestyle adjustments would be made, since eggs are a staple.

I hope I don't sound preachy, because I have no room to preach. But when you put food side by side and put a name and place to its origins, it's really hard to get excited about the overabundance of cheap food in the stores. I've also found that I can spend $5 on a dozen eggs by cutting out things like Goldfish crackers, dry cereal and other convenience foods. Buying from the farmer tends to give you a better appreciation of nutritional value and the true cost of food. I hope we see more chicken farmers like the Zaerpoors cropping up (so to speak).

By the way, the egg on the right is from Kookoolan -- bright orange, thick, meaty and delicious.

Hang on little tomato

(With apologies to Pink Martini.)

Ahh. Now I understand why you aren't supposed to plant tomatoes in July. Because by the time they are ready for pickin', it's freakin' 60 degrees out and they look so bedraggled and hopeless. Those little green balls, hanging on for dear life, wishing I'd knit them a sweater. I wonder why we keep getting luscious ripe ones in our weekly CSA bin? So many that we accumulated quite a bowl full. And those sweet bell peppers keep on coming, too. Renee and I ran into a mutual acquaintance as we were divvying up our produce at the farmers market, and she advised us to dice and freeze our tomatoes for soups and sauces. Really. Just wash, dice, put in freezer bag. So that's what I spent the afternoon doing: freezing tomatoes and bell peppers.

I love using red, yellow and orange bell peppers in stir-fries or just eating them raw on a salad. But in the winter time they all come from waaay down south. Now we've got enough local peppers for several winter meals. It's weird how just taking the little bit of effort to freeze those peaches has suddenly made me want to preserve everything I can during these waning harvest days. Wish I'd thought to put some green beans on ice.

Preserving peaches

I had fully intended to freeze and dehydrate local berries this summer so that my family could enjoy them all winter long. (Kind of like the book Blueberries for Sal when Sal's mom tells her that they have to pick blueberries and can them so that they will have food for the winter. As if they will starve if they don't pick blueberries. Love that book!) Well, that didn't happen. Determined to freeze something sweet and fresh, I bought two boxes of local peaches at the Hillsdale Farmers Market last Sunday. Visions of peach cobbler and morning oatmeal flecked with peaches spurred me on.

I pulled out my book Keeping the Harvest, which I've had for 10 years and is in like-new condition (if that gives you any idea about my harvest-keeping history). I remember my mom freezing peaches back home in Indiana. She'd place them in a pie pan already mixed up with all of the spices needed for peach pie, and pop it into the freezer. That way when it was time to make pie, she could just pop out the peach mold and stick it in the crust for baking. I decided just to go with freezer bags. The book recommends using an antioxidant, like ascorbic acid (vitamin C) to keep the peaches from browning. You can buy this at the store in crystalline form, but I went the thrifty route and made my own, crushing up three 500 mg vitamin C tablets in 8 cups of water (a recipe provided in the book).

Basically I just peeled the peaches (they were at the perfect point of ripeness), sliced them, poured a little vitamin C juice over them, put them in quart-size freezer bags and popped them in the freezer. It seemed so easy that I'm afraid I did something wrong. I could have added sugar or honey, but I was going for purity. I love opening the freezer door now and seeing these bags of golden goodness waiting for oatmeal season. It makes so much sense to preserve local foods if you have the space. I think I'm ready to tackle canning next. Next year, that is.

One family tackling the Eat Local Challenge

One of the great things to come out of the Green Sprouts Festival was meeting Portland mom and blogger Katherine Gray, who stopped by our clothing swap. Katherine writes about her family's (including two young children) quest to eat as locally as possible on her blog Dirt to Dish. Her posts are full of details about what she's buying, how much she's spending (trying to stick to the USDA family budget!) and her struggles to source certain local foods. I'm so inspired by her dedication and awed by the time it must take her to research and plan menus, while both working and raising two children. I love her writing style and down-to-earth honesty. I'd like to hear more about her menus and recipes (hint, hint) and what types of things she's getting her kids to eat.

While I haven't limited my shopping to only local foods, I do *try* to buy as local as possible (fruit is tricky -- but have you tried those local peaches lately -- oh yum). As we head into colder months, and the availability of fresh local produce dwindles, I can see challenges ahead. My CSA subscription ends at the end of October, and I'm already in mourning. I'm not sure how well kale and root veggies will go over with my crew. But now that I've become aware of the 'true cost of food' I'm not sure I can bring myself to buy red bell peppers from the grocery store in February. I'll be eagerly following Katherine's adventures this winter!
