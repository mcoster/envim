Cold wash, no sort laundry: what do you think?

Betty from our GreenGroup has an interesting approach to laundry. She doesn't sort it by color, and she washes everything in cold water. I think she said she got the idea from flylady. The pros of this scenario are several:

  • Save energy by not heating the water
  • Save energy by always washing full loads
  • Save some time by not having to sort

Betty claims she's seen no difference in the wash quality. Yet, I can't bring myself to try this; the idea kind of scares me. I've been washing clothing the way my mom taught me for years (i.e., sorting into white, light and dark piles. Hot for whites, warm for lights, cold for darks).

What do you think? How do you wash? Stuck in the old way like me? Or ready to save some energy and try something new?

One Hour, No Power: can you do it?

My friend Betty from our GreenGroup told me about an event called One Hour No Power, scheduled for Sunday, July 1, 2007, at midday.

If you're concerned about climate change, the organizers are asking you to not drive, not run your A/C, not blow-dry your hair, not vacuum your rugs, not blog on your computer, not watch TV... Basically unplug from anything non-vital from noon to 1pm (in your local time zone).

Then do something nice like write a pen and paper letter to someone you love, work on a photo album, walk to a farmers' market, read a book, have a conversation. The possibilities are limitless!!

Can you do it? I'm in.

Baby step: vacuum underneath your fridge

Gift of Green's got a great list of 10 easy green things to do this week. One I'd like to add: vacuum underneath your fridge. Typically, the fridge is the most energy hungry appliance in your house as it's always on. I recently dropped a magnet off of our fridge, bent to retrieve it, and lo, and behold it looked like a cat had taken up residence under there (yes, the hair was that fluffy and gross, and no, we don't have a cat). Blanket your hot water heater, and other ways to be hot water energy efficient

A comment by Becky the other day about conserving electricity got me thinking that my hot water heater was in need of a blanky. I took a trip to the hardware store, and bought a blanket. They're cheap -- less than 20 bucks. And it's one-size-fits-all. I had checked to see how big my tank was before I went, but it turns out I should have done a little internet research, and read the water heater manual first.

The rules of thumb for blanketing your water heaters:

  • If your water heater is more than 10 years old, you will likely save energy by blanketing it
  • If your water heater feels warm to the touch, you will likely save energy by blanketing it

Ours is less than 10 years old. We'd even bought a very energy-efficient model when the old one died a few years back. But what I did find is that our hot water heater has a few energy savings settings that I was not utilizing:

  • I dropped the temp a bit from 120 to 115 degrees. With the current summery weather, I didn't think we'll notice that difference.
  • I switched from Smart, to Energy Smart in the setting. Hopefully that will help a bit. There are also a few other modes for if we go on vacation, that I've never used but I vow to in the future.

The other point Becky made was to insulate exposed hot water pipes coming from the heater. So I am going to return the blanket, and exchange it for some pipe insulation. TTFN! Off to the hardware store!!

KidTips: Teaching your kids to turn off the lights

There have been days in our house where it seems every light is burning. "Why are all of these lights on?" we'd ask the kids. "Because we needed to see." "Why didn't you turn them off." "Because we didn't." Alright then. Well this was really driving my husband crazy, so one night he and I sat down to figure out how to teach them (well, really just the 5.5 year old) to turn off the lights. He came up with a brilliant idea: our daughter, the oldest, loves to be in charge, so why not make her in charge of turning off the lights? Give it a catchy name, like Light Bulb Leader, and make her understand how everyone has important jobs around the house, and one of her jobs, as Light Bulb Leader, is to make sure that the lights are off whenever someone leaves a room. Well, let me tell you, it worked! She was thrilled with the title (Light Bulb Princess, Light Bulb Chief, Light Bulb Warrior would also work, depending on your child's interests) and took the job very seriously. Now, whenever she leaves the playroom or her bedroom, the lights go off without me having to tell her. She understands that it's wasting electricity to leave on the lights and that it costs a lot of money. As she gets older, she will learn more about electricity and how it works, but for now we're just trying to teach her some good habits. And it's actually made my husband and I more aware of unnecessary light bulb usage around the house. If someone does leave the lights on, we'll ask, "Where is our Light Bulb Leader?" and she'll come racing to switch off the lights. I'm hoping our son is learning by watching (since he's still too short to turn off most of the lights) and will pick up good habits as he gets older.

Have you had success getting your kids to turn off the lights? Any good tips and techniques to share?

Does switching Surge Protectors "off" to save electricity still protect from power surges?

In our GreenGroup meeting today we were talking about how if you turn the little switches on your power strips off you'll save a lot of electricity. Phantom load, as it's called, accounts for a significant amount of electricity usage in the typical American home -- up to 75%. Think about what you've got plugged in (but not necessarily on) at any given time: toaster, coffee maker, computers, printers, telephones, lamps, TVs, battery chargers, clock radios, stereos, cell phone chargers, etc... So the idea of unplugging or turning off power strips to these items when not in use to save electricity is a great idea. We we wondering, however, if that negates the surge protection ability of the power strip? Does anyone know? I'll be glad to turn mine off, but not at the risk of frying my hard drive in an electrical storm. Can anyone shed some light?

Erecting a retractable clothesline is something I've had on my mental to-do list, and this article reminded me to get a move on. Timer and new, improved CFL lightbulbs = energy savings

According to Portland General Electric's energy savings tips - switching out your most frequently used lightbulbs to CFLs is one of the best things you can do to save electricity. During the rolling blackouts of a few summers ago, I bought a bunch of CFLs, and put them in as many light fixtures and lamps as I could. The bad news was at that time -- they did not recommend them for dimmers, can lights, fixed mount lights, and some just wouldn't fit into various lamps, etc.

So I was pleased to see there are now flood-light style CFLs that work in can lights. We have can lights in the kitchen, which are easily the most highly used lights in our house. The ones I took out were 120v each. Five of them. Ouch! That's a huge energy drain. The new CFLs take some getting used to -- they are 15v each, and they take a while to warm up. But I'm getting used to it.

The other thing I just got is a timer lightswitch . I learned about this switch on ReadySetMom in a post about home security. I am going to use it on our exterior lamp post -- which I am constantly forgetting to tun off each day.
