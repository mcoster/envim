Washing laundry in cold water

Betty from our SW Portland GreenGroup was the first person to suggest washing laundry in cold water. I admit I was skeptical. So often we get stuck in habits and are just unwilling to try something new. Well, at least that's what I was feeling about washing all my laundry in cold water. Then I heard a little more about it via New American Dream's Beat the Heat, Wash in Cold campaign. I was shocked to learn that:

  • 90% of the energy used in washing clothing is from heating the water
  • If you wash 80% or 4 out of 5 loads on cold/cold, you'll cut 72 pounds of CO2 emission in one month
  • If you wash 80% of your laundry on cold/cold for a year, you should save from $60-100 on your energy bills

So I tried it. And I can't complain at all. The clothing smells and looks just as clean as when I was washing on warm/cold. I can't believe I didn't try this sooner. I also worry a lot less about the laundry, because usually I forget to read the tags and don't know when to wash delicate things on cold. Now everything is on cold. And it's good. I'm curious now to compare some of our electricity bills with past years to see if we are really saving money too.

Do you wash on cold?

In with storm windows, and out with the screens

Fall is here. We spent much of the morning "battening down the hatches" as Cheap-o-Dad calls it. Translation: putting in the storm windows for insulation. In past years, we've been a bit lazy and have just left the screens in during winter (we have mostly old single pane double-hung wooden windows, but with newer storm windows built over them). However, I read recently that you get better passive solar heating if you take out the screens for fall/winter. So we did that too. Man, what a difference. I know I really want that extra light and heat as we head into this cold and gray time of year. We also raised all of our blinds on the eastern and western sides of the house, again, so as much light can shine in as possible (yes, we are very lazy, and we only put them all the way up this time of year, and only fully lower them on really, really, really hot days).

A simple switch to CFLs

Compact Fluorescent Lightbulbs (CFLs) are controversial. They require much less energy to work than incandescent bulbs, thus pump far less carbon dioxide into the atmosphere. However, they contain mercury, which is a toxic substance. But if handled and disposed of properly, they should pose no safety concerns. I did a little research and found four sites that have dramatically reassured me on the safety and effectiveness of CFLs:

    • This article at TreeHugger explains the recycling process of mercury.
    • Snopes.com, the myth debunker, tells you how to clean up a CFL if you break it in your home. (read this!)
    • Greenpeace explains how using CFLs actually reduces the amount of mercury in the environment.
    • Finally, this very cool site -- A Simple Switch -- created by Phillips, provides a gorgeous visual tour of the environmental impact of switching to CFLs. (Make sure to turn on your volume.) Yes, they manufacture CFLs and have an economic incentive to get you to switch. But all of the arguments are valid. Work through the entire tour (on the far right, click the "commit to switch" button and follow it all the way through). Your kids will also love this!

Cold wash, no sort laundry: what do you think?

Betty from our GreenGroup has an interesting approach to laundry. She doesn't sort it by color, and she washes everything in cold water. I think she said she got the idea from flylady. The pros of this scenario are several:

  • Save energy by not heating the water
  • Save energy by always washing full loads
  • Save some time by not having to sort

Betty claims she's seen no difference in the wash quality. Yet, I can't bring myself to try this; the idea kind of scares me. I've been washing clothing the way my mom taught me for years (i.e., sorting into white, light and dark piles. Hot for whites, warm for lights, cold for darks).

What do you think? How do you wash? Stuck in the old way like me? Or ready to save some energy and try something new?

One Hour, No Power: can you do it?

My friend Betty from our GreenGroup told me about an event called One Hour No Power, scheduled for Sunday, July 1, 2007, at midday.

If you're concerned about climate change, the organizers are asking you to not drive, not run your A/C, not blow-dry your hair, not vacuum your rugs, not blog on your computer, not watch TV... Basically unplug from anything non-vital from noon to 1pm (in your local time zone).

Then do something nice like write a pen and paper letter to someone you love, work on a photo album, walk to a farmers' market, read a book, have a conversation. The possibilities are limitless!!

Blanket your hot water heater, and other ways to be hot water energy efficient

A comment by Becky the other day about conserving electricity got me thinking that my hot water heater was in need of a blanky. I took a trip to the hardware store, and bought a blanket. They're cheap -- less than 20 bucks. And it's one-size-fits-all. I had checked to see how big my tank was before I went, but it turns out I should have done a little internet research, and read the water heater manual first.

The rules of thumb for blanketing your water heaters:

  • If your water heater is more than 10 years old, you will likely save energy by blanketing it
  • If your water heater feels warm to the touch, you will likely save energy by blanketing it

Ours is less than 10 years old. We'd even bought a very energy-efficient model when the old one died a few years back. But what I did find is that our hot water heater has a few energy savings settings that I was not utilizing:

  • I dropped the temp a bit from 120 to 115 degrees. With the current summery weather, I didn't think we'll notice that difference.
  • I switched from Smart, to Energy Smart in the setting. Hopefully that will help a bit. There are also a few other modes for if we go on vacation, that I've never used but I vow to in the future.

The other point Becky made was to insulate exposed hot water pipes coming from the heater. So I am going to return the blanket, and exchange it for some pipe insulation. TTFN! Off to the hardware store!!

KidTips: Teaching your kids to turn off the lights

There have been days in our house where it seems every light is burning. "Why are all of these lights on?" we'd ask the kids. "Because we needed to see." "Why didn't you turn them off." "Because we didn't." Alright then. Well this was really driving my husband crazy, so one night he and I sat down to figure out how to teach them (well, really just the 5.5 year old) to turn off the lights. He came up with a brilliant idea: our daughter, the oldest, loves to be in charge, so why not make her in charge of turning off the lights? Give it a catchy name, like Light Bulb Leader, and make her understand how everyone has important jobs around the house, and one of her jobs, as Light Bulb Leader, is to make sure that the lights are off whenever someone leaves a room. Well, let me tell you, it worked! She was thrilled with the title (Light Bulb Princess, Light Bulb Chief, Light Bulb Warrior would also work, depending on your child's interests) and took the job very seriously. Now, whenever she leaves the playroom or her bedroom, the lights go off without me having to tell her. She understands that it's wasting electricity to leave on the lights and that it costs a lot of money. As she gets older, she will learn more about electricity and how it works, but for now we're just trying to teach her some good habits. And it's actually made my husband and I more aware of unnecessary light bulb usage around the house. If someone does leave the lights on, we'll ask, "Where is our Light Bulb Leader?" and she'll come racing to switch off the lights. I'm hoping our son is learning by watching (since he's still too short to turn off most of the lights) and will pick up good habits as he gets older.

Have you had success getting your kids to turn off the lights? Any good tips and techniques to share?

Does switching Surge Protectors "off" to save electricity still protect from power surges?

In our GreenGroup meeting today we were talking about how if you turn the little switches on your power strips off you'll save a lot of electricity. Phantom load, as it's called, accounts for a significant amount of electricity usage in the typical American home -- up to 75%. Think about what you've got plugged in (but not necessarily on) at any given time: toaster, coffee maker, computers, printers, telephones, lamps, TVs, battery chargers, clock radios, stereos, cell phone chargers, etc... So the idea of unplugging or turning off power strips to these items when not in use to save electricity is a great idea. We we wondering, however, if that negates the surge protection ability of the power strip? Does anyone know? I'll be glad to turn mine off, but not at the risk of frying my hard drive in an electrical storm. Can anyone shed some light?

Timer and new, improved CFL lightbulbs = energy savings

According to Portland General Electric's energy savings tips - switching out your most frequently used lightbulbs to CFLs is one of the best things you can do to save electricity. During the rolling blackouts of a few summers ago, I bought a bunch of CFLs, and put them in as many light fixtures and lamps as I could. The bad news was at that time -- they did not recommend them for dimmers, can lights, fixed mount lights, and some just wouldn't fit into various lamps, etc.

So I was pleased to see there are now flood-light style CFLs that work in can lights. We have can lights in the kitchen, which are easily the most highly used lights in our house. The ones I took out were 120v each. Five of them. Ouch! That's a huge energy drain. The new CFLs take some getting used to -- they are 15v each, and they take a while to warm up. But I'm getting used to it.

The other thing I just got is a timer lightswitch . I learned about this switch on ReadySetMom in a post about home security. I am going to use it on our exterior lamp post -- which I am constantly forgetting to tun off each day.

No more excuses -- get yourself added to the electric bill

I cringed when I saw the woman at the door, carrying a clipboard and a messenger bag figuring it was someone canvassing the neighborhood for a donation. When she said she was out signing up people for PGE's Renewable Future program I almost hugged her. I've been wanting to sign up for one of the PGE renewable energy programs for months, but my husband is listed on the account so only he could sign us up. I assumed I would have to get him to call PGE and add me or sign up for it himself -- and the only time I'd think to talk to him about it was on the weekends or evenings when PGE was closed. I know, lame-o excuse, but that's my story and I'm stickin' to it.

Wellll, the PGE canvasser told me that all you have to do is call PGE at 503-228-6322 from your home phone and ask to be added to the bill. Then either of you can sign up for renewable energy. So don't use my old excuse! PGE's Rewable Future program means 100% of your energy comes from renewable sources. Your rate is locked in for 5 years, and based on our energy usage our bill will increase about $14. This actually gives us an even greater incentive to look for ways to reduce our energy consumption.

Dreamin' of a green Christmas

This week both the Oregonian and Portland Tribune had some good ideas for making the holidays a little greener.

Recycle everything! A no-brainer, right? I've always been stymied about blocks of styrofoam and rigid plastic packaging. Turns out that Far West Fibers can recycle both, though the styrofoam will cost a small fee. If you've never been over to their site on SE 28th, it's worth a trip. Save up all that nasty stuff that you hate to chuck in the garbage, and head on over. They can recycle just about anything -- but some of it, like styrofoam blocks, costs a small fee. Some branches of Postal Annex will accept foam blocks and bubble wrap, but call ahead to check. Clean packing peanuts can be dropped off any UPS store for free. Also, Metro has a comprehensive site on recycling (though I find it a little cumbersome to get straight answers).

Re-using gift wrap! I don't know anyone who doesn't hoard and re-use gift bags, ribbon and bows -- so I won't preach to the choir on that one. I've never been one to save and re-use wrapping paper, but a lot of it can't be tossed in the recycle bin. Consider saving foil gift wrap for re-use -- plus it's a great craft project material for the kids. Some gift wrap is so darn cute -- I bet if we start saving it, we'll end up finding ways to re-use it.

Don't chuck the greeting cards! Art from the Heart (503-528-0744) and SnowCap Community Charities accept used greeting cards. And SCRAP is offering a workshop tomorrow on creative uses for old cards. Cards with embellishments like foil, glitter and plastic can't be recycled.

Using LED Christmas lights! LED lights supposedly last forever and they are far more energy-efficient than the incandescents. I really, really wanted to do this, so I went over to A-Boy to check out the selection. Maybe they didn't have a great selection. I found solid green or solid blue lights for $10 for a 35-light strand. They also had multi-colored lights, but the colors looked a little garish. The Tribune says you can get a strand of 100 white LEDs for around $30. I usually use about 8-10 strands of lights on our tree. Gulp. If they really do last for years, I know it's worth it in the end.
