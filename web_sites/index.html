Donors Resource makes it easy to donate items

It's spring cleaning time, isn't it? Can you believe all the stuff you've accumulated? Where does it come from? Sometimes it can be really overwhelming to try and figure out where to take items to be donated. We aren't fans of the big 'non-profit' thrift stores here at EnviroMom corporate headquarters, so we like to identify organizations that work to help people in our communities. Now there's a Web site that has made this task even easier: Donors Resource. We've posted about it before, but the site has been recently revamped and bulked up. There are a couple of different ways to use the site:

1) You can search for non-profits based on items you have to donate: furniture, CDs, toys, musical instruments, etc.

2) You can post the descriptions and photos of items you have to donate, and non-profit organization will contact you if they are in need of those items and will arrange pick-up or drop-off.

The site is free to both non-profits and donors and makes it really easy to get your goods into the hands of people who really need them. Right now the site only serves Oregon and Washington, but they plan to expand it across the nation. Woo hoo!

Swap sites: have you tried 'em?

It seems everytime I turn around I hear about another 'swap site' -- a Web site where you list items you want to swap for other items at no cost. Here are just a few:

Books: Both FrugalReader and BookMooch are free swap sites; list the books you want, list the books you have and just pay for postage to ship 'em out. In addition to books, SwapTree has listings for music, DVDs and video games.

Children's Clothing & Gear: At Swap Baby Goods and Zwaggle you can swap clothes, toys, gear, furniture, pretty much anything related to kids.

Women's Clothing & Make-up: SwapStyle and MakeupAlley are a couple of options (and I would hope that the make-up is not used).

Ever used a swap site? Were you happy with the way it worked?

I'm dreaming of a YouTube Christmas (card)

When Behan asked about reducing the impact of holiday greeting cards, it really got our family thinking. This was really a stumper. I love getting holiday cards. And despite my complaints about the process, some years I actually like the picture we get of our entire family. For those family and friends that live far away, I also like the aspect of showing them how the children are growing. So we decided to focus our creative efforts and create a YouTube holiday video card this year.

There are some prerequisites:

  • digital video or still images (most of us use only digital now, right?)
  • digital music (most of us listen to digital music now, right?)
  • digital editing software (if you have a Mac, iMovie software is included)
  • a little bit of creativity

It just so happens, we have all of these in spades. Cheap-o-Dad really missed his calling as a movie maker. Heather can vouch for him. His iMovies are gems.

So, as is the case in all of the movies our family makes, Cheap-o is the director. The kids are the talent. Cheap-o and I occasionally have walk-on parts. And I get to be the executive producer. This mostly entails boring questions and comments, such as: "Well, the concept is great. Very creative. I love it. My concern is will we finish on time and on budget?" Budget is not really a factor, since it doesn't cost us anything but time and creativity. But hey, if I'm getting credit as the Executive Producer I've got to act like money is a concern, right? First and foremost is the timeline. Seeing as this is a Christmas video, we do truly hope to finish filming, editing and production by Christmas. So that gives us about 10 more days to get this baby in the can...

The talent and director have been busy today on their B-roll. We shot the A-roll the other night. Editing will probably commence late tonight, after the kids have gone to bed. Quite possibly, it will be an all-nighter for Cheap-o. The good news is he enjoys the creative process, and says once he has his storyboards the movie will pretty much make itself.

There was a part of me that has always resisted email holiday greetings. But this year, I've changed my mind. I'm looking at lessening the impact of sending cards and photos. And I also love the fact that there's no limit to the amount of friends we can send it to, and they can comment or send us a note back right away. Since that is the one pleasure lacking in mailed paper/photo cards: the friendly dialog. I can't tell you how many lovely cards we get each year with fabulously cute photos and newsy notes, and I always want to talk to those senders right then and there. But I never do pick up the phone. I'm not really a phone person. But email, video and blogs, those are my thing. That's why our family is dreaming of and creating a YouTube Christmas card this year.

Reclaimed crafty-love: Littleput Books pendants

When I opened up my Portland Picks newsletter this morning, I saw this pendant and immediately fell in love. How could I not? The artist, based in Portland, makes these pendants from old Scrabble tiles, which she covers in Japanese or Italian paper, sealed with a non-toxic acrylic coating. The pendants hang from a thin, organza ribbon and come in a matching tin box. At $15 -- it's a no-brainer! The hard part will be deciding which one to buy! Check out the Littleput Books Etsy shop for the whole kit-and-kaboodle. I love that so many talented artisans are making such beautiful works from reclaimed materials these days. Hooray for them (and us)!

Oh, and what is Portland Picks, you ask? Really? This gem of a newsletter arrives in my inbox every Friday morning filled with news about locally-owned Portland stores and Web sites. It's heavy on fashion, home decor, and restaurants, and it really makes you appreciate the staggering number of fantastic local boutiques and talented artistic/foodie types we have in Portland. Why on earth would ya go to the mall when you can shop local? Portland Picks is free, did I mention? Go subscribe now! (And they have one for men, too!)

Globio: online paradise for nature-loving kids

Fostering a love of nature is one of the goals of our Outdoor Kids feature. Yet, as the weather changes it's not so easy to go outside and explore the wonders of nature. That's where Globio comes in. Farm to School Web portal launched

Check this out! Farm to School is a Web portal that details every school lunch program in the country working to bring more local food to students. Click on your state and read about what schools and districts are doing, who you should contact for more information, upcoming events and other information. It's heartening that so many states have active programs. This description of Portland Public School's program jumped out at me:

The district has engaged in a visioning process to work towards making healthy, nutritious foods readily available to all students, with significant goals such as 100% of menu items prepared regionally; 50% of menu items sourced locally; hormone, antibiotic and nitrate free meats to the extent possible; dairy products that are free of recombinant bovine growth hormone; fish products from sustainable sources; and fruits and vegetables that are sustainably or organically grown.

Just by chance I checked out Vermont's programs and discovered VT FEED (Vermont Food Education Every Day). They use a three pronged approach to local food education:

1. The Classroom, with standard-based food, farm, and nutrition-based curriculum that involves in-depth graduate level teacher training, including summer workshops and in-class mentoring;

2. The Cafeteria, through the integration of local food and nutrition education through food service institutes, in-cafeteria mentoring, and peer support;

3. The Community, through reconnecting people to their food sources- the farms and farmers.

All very exciting stuff.

Are you game for a Green Halloween?

So here's another fast-approaching holiday that could benefit from losing some of the excess: Halloween. Heather and I were just talking about Halloween the other day. I have such mixed feelings about Halloween. I loved it as a kid. As I recollect, we trick-or-treated with pillow cases, filling them until they were laden with pounds of candy. Our parents would pick through, make sure it was all safe, then let us partake until our teeth rotted and our bellies ached.

One friend recently said that the candy fairy comes and takes away the excess candy after a day or two, then replaces it with a present (I was eavesdropping on this conversation, so I hope I got that right). Even that gives me mixed feelings. I love that it keeps kids from eating all the junk, but I hate to throw away anything, even if it is junk food. Someone built a manufacturing and distribution channel, and made the candy. Someone then bought and paid for it. And if we throw it out, then our hand-working garbage haulers have to truck it, transfer it, then landfill it. Senseless waste! In past years, I've foisted the excess candy on my husband or his work, but again, it's not like adults really need a lot of candy either.

So Heather was thinking about temporary tattoos as a giveaway. I was toying with the idea of stickers. Or pennies. What I don't want to do is foist other forms of crap (i.e., the made in China "goody bag" type of crap) on kids either. Then today I came across the Green Halloween site (via Red Tricycle, out of Seattle), who urge us to "think outside the candy box." They suggest healthy foods and "treasures." What do you think? How do you green your Halloween?

What I love about this store (aside from their new, larger location) is that their prices are low, their selection is huge and the two women who run it, Michelle and Tracy, are so darn welcoming. Carbon offsets are a joke

I've never really liked the idea of carbon offsets. It seems to me that people and companies should be looking at the root cause of what they are doing to release carbon and other greenhouse gases, and fix those things, rather than pay a penalty, plant some trees, all the while wailing "mea culpa! mea culpa!"

Love this post from eco-chick with Cheat Neutral's take on offsets. They mockingly liken offsets to buying flowers and candy to make it up to your significant other after cheating on them. To become "cheat neutral," cheaters get paired with registered faithfuls, and we all balance out. Gotta love Cheat Neutral's tagline too: "Helping you because you can't help yourself." Although this site is a joke, they've got some good references on carbon offsetting and why they think carbon offsetting is also a joke.

Paperless billing: some local links

In July, I posted about the paper savings of paying bills online. The flipside, of course, is receiving the bills in a paperless way. Most of mine are arriving via email, others still on paper. So I thought I'd find and share the online links of the companies I routinely pay, so that you can be spared that work, and just go paperless all the way. So here's the links:

Qwest: Click on residential. You will need to log in or create an account, with your phone number and a special 3-digit number on your bill. I was prompted to go paperless, once I was logged into my account. Otherwise, log in, go to "Paperless Billing" under Billing & Payments.
PGE: Click on yellow Go Paperless button (you will need an online registration) or call PGE customer service at 503-228-6322 or 800-542-8818. You will need to know your account number.
NW Natural: Create and/or log into "Your Account", then select "Paperless Billing" under Your Bill, or call customer service at (800) 422-4012.
Comcast: You will need to create or log into your account, then go to "statement delivery" under View My Bill, to select electronic delivery, or call 1-800-266-2278.

All told, it took me about 10 minutes to sign up for all of these. The hardest part was trying to remember which email address I had listed with Qwest from 7 years ago. The really good news: the less paper bills I get, the less I have to file... (did I ever mention how much I hate filing?)

Rate your neighborhood on its walkscore

Lunch gear for the EnviroKid

I've been putting a bit of thought into my kids' lunches and how I might send them off to school with something healthy, tasty and non-toxic. Luckily, there are lots of options out there:

There's a whole bunch of stainless food containers out there in a range of prices. At Target you can pick up a 10-oz Thermos Food Jar ($17.99) or a 10-oz Nissan Food Jar ($19.99) for warm soup lunches.

Small stainless food containers are nice for chopped veggies (yeah, right), pasta and other finger foods. Pearl River has several options, including these round and rectangular containers. The round ones range in size from 4.75 to 6.25 inches and price from $3 to $4. The rectangular ones are 5.5 and 6.25 inches and $3.50 to 4.50. The prices are great, but I don't know if they are leak-proof (which isn't always an issue). Life Without Plastic also offers similar round containers in various sizes, though each one comes with a little interior container to separate foods. Pricier at $9.45 to $46.25.

I like this Gaiam To-Go Ware Food Tin because it stacks and has a little handle, but it's a bit bigger and might not be practical for a small kid.

For wrapping up sandwiches, Natural Waxed Paper Bags are nice and cheap, but not really reusable. There's the Wrap-N-Mat fabric sandwich wrap for $6.50, though I've read that one mom didn't like the plastic smell coming from the liner (ew).

Finally there's the lunch box. Reusable Bags has a gazillion lead-free lunch boxes and bags in a range of sizes and prices. This Mimi the Sardine bag is pretty darn cute ($19.95). I think it's hard to buy a lunch bag online -- you don't want it too small or too large. Might have to scout around town for something.

And don't forget the cloth napkin and packing your own silverware! Yikes. This is starting to sound like a lot of work. Well, there's lots of options and bunches more that I simply don't have the time or energy to research. Any others you like? Any good local Portland stores to scout out for reusable lunch box items?

Eco-friendly back-to-school shopping

Pencils, crayons, scissors, markers, glue, folders, recycled paper, the list goes on and on. I got it all from Schoolhouse Supplies just sitting at my computer. Renee and I have written about this fantastic reuse organization many times, and they just keep on giving and giving back to the community. Six elementary schools (Rieke, Ainsworth, Stephenson, Alameda, Bridlemile and Laurelhurst) are participating in Schoolhouse Supplies' online shopping this year, so take advantage of it if your kids attend one. The schools give Schoolhouse Supplies the shopping list for each grade; you simply go to the site, find your school and grade, and click! the supplies will be delivered to your child's classroom. No gas wasted. No standing in line. Plus your purchase benefits this fantastic organization! Win, win, win.

I got everything on the Rieke kindergarten list (except the backpack) for $30. (Note: all of the supplies are new.) Prices will vary because each school requires different supplies, but the price is pretty competitive with what you'd pay at a big box store. Schoolhouse Supplies hopes to have more schools on board for next year -- talk to your principal if your school isn't on the list!

Facilitating reuse: Swaptree

Thank you Mark, for sending us this great email telling us all about Swaptree:

Dear Enviro Moms: Nice job with your site.  Its beautiful - inside and out!

I thought that you and your readers might be interested in a site I co-founded called www.swaptree.com.

Blackle: does it really save energy?

JJ sent us a link to Blackle and had the same question. Blackle is a Google search engine with a black background and white text, which is supposed to conserve energy. According to the site, a monitor requires more energy to display white/light screens than black/dark screens. Hmmm. Inquiring minds want to know.

The Numbers Guy, a Wall Street Journal blogger, found that older, bulkier CRT monitors might see a 5-20% energy savings using Blackle. However, newer LCD monitors won't see much benefit. In any case, Blackle's founder says, "Even if the energy savings are small, they are representative of the need for each of us to start taking small steps to save energy." Hey, we're all for baby steps around here, but I'll take mine where it's not so hard to read.
