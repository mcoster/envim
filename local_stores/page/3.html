Reuse: glass flower vases

There's nothing I like more than a fresh floral arrangement. I have no skill in arranging flowers myself, so the lovely ones always come from professional florists. Occasionally, Cheap-o will pop for some flowers, for a big wedding anniversary (especially if I drop a lot of hints). And my dad is quite thoughtful about getting flower arrangements for holidays and special celebrations. The challenge is with the glass flower vases.

If you are like me, you've probably collected flower vases in all shapes, sizes and colors. You reuse them as best you can. But perhaps, you too, are finding that your cabinets are near to bursting with glass flower vases. Due to different types of glass, these vases cannot be recycled curbside in the "glass on the side" bucket. And if you've ever been to a thrift store or estate sale, you'll usually find an abundance of glass flower vases there as well. So this challenge is clearly widespread. Once, I suggested to our local florist that she take back and reuse some of the vases she'd delivered to our house, and she looked at me as if I were suggesting she take back and reuse the dead flowers.

That was a while ago. Perhaps times have changed. Does anyone use a local florist who will take back and reuse glass flower vases? Or, do you know of a local organization that wants glass flower vases?

Reclaimed crafty-love: Littleput Books pendants

When I opened up my Portland Picks newsletter this morning, I saw this pendant and immediately fell in love. How could I not? The artist, based in Portland, makes these pendants from old Scrabble tiles, which she covers in Japanese or Italian paper, sealed with a non-toxic acrylic coating. The pendants hang from a thin, organza ribbon and come in a matching tin box. At $15 -- it's a no-brainer! The hard part will be deciding which one to buy! Check out the Littleput Books Etsy shop for the whole kit-and-kaboodle. I love that so many talented artisans are making such beautiful works from reclaimed materials these days. Hooray for them (and us)!

Oh, and what is Portland Picks, you ask? Really? This gem of a newsletter arrives in my inbox every Friday morning filled with news about locally-owned Portland stores and Web sites. It's heavy on fashion, home decor, and restaurants, and it really makes you appreciate the staggering number of fantastic local boutiques and talented artistic/foodie types we have in Portland. Why on earth would ya go to the mall when you can shop local? Portland Picks is free, did I mention? Go subscribe now! (And they have one for men, too!)

Fred Meyer giving away reusable bags through Nov. 17

Fred Meyer has sold its reusable, polypropylene grocery bags in stores for awhile now, but for the next two weeks they'll be giving bags away to encourage shoppers to reduce usage of the paper and plastic variety. If your grocery bill exceeds $75 at checkout, you'll get three free reusable bags and be eligible to purchase additional bags for 2 for $1. The give-away starts today and will run through November 17.

It's good to see mainstream grocery chains jumping on this bandwagon. It wasn't that long ago, when you saw someone carrying in their own bag, that you *might* have discretely rolled your eyes. Are we getting to the point where we'll be raising eyebrows when we see someone NOT carrying a reusable bag?

Reducing waste in the fast-food nation

We try not to eat out often. Part of my concern is the cost. Part of my concern is the question of where the food comes from, and how nutritious it really is. Part of it is that my kids seem to then want to eat out all the time. But mostly it's the waste that is generated in the restaurant business.

Having taken the Master Recycler course, I have a better understanding of how challenging it is for a Portland Metro restaurant to implement composting and recycling plans: Unlike with residential waste/recycling haulers who use the hauler that is assigned to their geographical area, businesses bid for haulers from all over the area, and typically hire one that gives them the best price. So myriad haulers criss-cross all over town, serving only the businesses they have contracted with, and sometimes a hauler's offerings for recycling/composting are slim to none.

So it's beyond exciting to hear about Burgerville's plan to expand a composting and recycling pilot to all 39 of their restaurants.  Burgerville is an established leader in local food purchasing, recycling cooking oil for biodiesel production, and a big customer of renewable power. This move really takes them one step further down the road to sustainability and makes them even more of a role model. They won my heart with their killer turkey burger, and they keep me coming back with each additional green-thinking business decision they implement. It's inspiring to see that restaurants can find a way to be green despite the challenges they face.

Portland Fashion gets even greener

Did you know that green is the new black, at least in the fashion world? Portland Fashion Week kicks off this Friday, and as reported in today's Oregonian green is in this year. The article details a desire from consumers to know where and how clothing is made:

"The growing awareness of the high costs of cheap clothes -- cost to the environment and cost to the people working in sweatshops -- has created conscientious consumers. It's a political movement without organized rallies or protests: They simply put their money where their hearts are."

And it's not just about sourcing sustainable fibers, such as hemp, bamboo and organic cotton. Get this: the Art Institute of Portland recently added classes in sustainable design in response to students' requests. That rocks!

The fashion industry can seem inherently un-green, when you consider they're changing what's hot and trendy every year in an attempt to get us to buy new and more, more, more. So this growing awareness and attentiveness to conscientious consumerism and sustainability is exciting.

Now let's get down to the nitty gritty. I've seen cool things for grown-ups made from sustainable fibers with sustainable practices. And lots of cute soft baby things. But who's making and selling the things all kids go through like crazy: socks, undies and tights? That's what our family needs...

Three cheers for Biokleen!

I never thought I would be the kind of person who got excited about a new dish washing soap. But I am. I really am. I recently bought a container of Biokleen Lemon Thyme Dishwash Liquid. And I love it! It smells divine (really!) and it gets the dishes clean. Now, I guess if I had read the label I would have realized it was a local company. I did not realize that until I read in the paper the other day that Biokleen, Burgerville and one other (that I forget now) just were awarded Washington state's top environmental honor -- the 2007 Governor's Award for Pollution Prevention and Sustainable Practices. How cool is that?

And since I'm reading the label now, it says you can make your own handsoap, bubble bath or pet shampoo using this one great product. So if my soft soap recipe looks too hard to make, you can dilute some of this super concentrated Biokleen liquid soap, and voila, you are in business!
