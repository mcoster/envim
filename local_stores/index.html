Free lead testing kits at Hoot-n-Annie

The city of Portland is distributing 3,000 free lead testing kits and one of our ReadySetMom sponsor's -- the fabulous Hoot-n-Annie is a pick-up site for them (the kits are for those living in homes built before 1978).
 
Did you know lead is still the number one environmental health threat to children? Lead-based paint was used in many homes built prior to 1978. When lead-based paint is disturbed through remodeling, daily wear and tear on doors, windows and other painted surfaces, or deteriorated by age, it creates dust. It is this dust that can poison children. Even a small amount of dust can be enough to harm a child. Lead can cause permanent learning disabilities, behavioral problems, and, at very high levels, seizures, coma, and even death. Because lead poisoning often occurs with no obvious symptoms, it frequently goes unrecognized. All children should be have their blood tested at ages 1 and 2. If the test kit shows there is lead in the home, families may be eligible for a grant of up to $10,000 for lead clean-up.

A trip to Habitat for Humanity's ReStore

I'm finally getting around to 'decorating' my 3-year old son's bedroom. Up until last week his chamber was looking pretty bleak: a mattress and box spring on the floor, an old rocker and a beat-up Dania bookcase. Poor guy. But after all, he is number two, so he'd better get used to it. :-) It would be really easy to go to IKEA and buy all new stuff, but I'm trying to temper my instant gratification urges by shopping some local second-hand stores. (Can't wait to tell you about the bed I found! Later...) Not only can I save a lot of money, but I feel good about extending the lifetime of a quality used product rather than buying something new. Buying new stuff isn't a bad thing (gawd knows I've done some serious damage at IKEA) but I want to see what I can reuse.

I've always heard great things about the Habitat for Humanity ReStore located under the Morrison Bridge, so I finally made it over last week. It is all stuff for the home that's been donated by builders, businesses, individuals, etc. Windows, doors, lighting fixtures, scrap wood, sinks, appliances, table legs, you name it. Yep, very similar to the ReBuilding Center, but not as overwhelming (for me, anyway). They also have furniture! The furniture they get is random, but I have friends who go there frequently and have told me about amazing finds (guess it's more about luck and timing). When I was there, they had this really, really cool suite of office furniture -- modern, brightly-colored, on casters, loved it. I didn't buy anything, but now I know where to go if I need a piece of scrap wood, or just to scout around for treasure. Plus, it all goes to benefit a great organization.

A tip: if you go in winter, dress warm. It's a huge, unheated warehouse, and you'll want to spend more time digging through stuff if you aren't shivering. There are Habitat ReStores throughout the US and Canada, so check out the Habitat Web site for locations. Happy thrifting! (PDX ReStore is open Tues-Sat, 9-5.)

Local food businesses that make our lives easier & healthier

Do we Portlanders live in a bubble, or is this sustainable food movement happening nationwide? I keep hearing about all of these small Oregon food companies that prepare local, organic foods for children and families, and some that will deliver meals right to your door! Happy day! I vaguely remember those early days, fresh from the hospital, with a tiny bundle that seemed to keep me up all hours of the day. Who had the energy to cook? Check out some of these options that cater to babes and parents-of-babes:

Building Blocks Foods: Jonathan Grumbles is a professional chef who's worked in NYC, Minneapolis, and now Portland. He cooks up fresh, local, sustainable fare and delivers it to your doorstep! He's a dad to two little kiddos and knows how hard it can be to get nutritious food on the table when your life revolves around a newborn. Heck, it's a struggle for most of us with older kids to get this kind of food on the table. His menus are mouth-watering!

Gaia Baby Food: Another local parent (and she just gave birth to #2 -- congrats!), Alisa Gaylord, cooks up local, seasonal baby food the same day it is delivered to your door. She works with local farmers and only cooks up what is fresh today. Three categories of foods: Smooth (for babes up to 8 months), Chunky (9-plus months) and Toddler (11 months-plus) serve all phases of development. Check out her online store and delivery schedule (she's on a limited maternity-leave schedule until early Feb.)

Organic Fresh Fingers: Imagine your child at his full-time daycare center. It's lunchtime. What would you like him to be eating? (Certainly not the ho-hum, questionable fare our public schools are serving up, right?) Enter Organic Fresh Fingers, a Salem-based business that prepares fresh, nutritious, certified-organic meals that contain no GMOs, artificial flavors or coloring for schools and daycare centers around the region. It almost sounds too good to be true, but it's not. In fact, schools with a large percent of low-income students get extra funding for meals and have recognized that going organic is an affordable and smart option for them. Hooray!   

Nice Cubes: Mom-of-two-boys, Kate McNamara, used to cook up batches of baby food and freeze them in ice trays, which her husband referred to as 'nice cubes.' Nice for us, she expanded her talent into a business and now offers a variety of Nice Cubes organic frozen baby food in stores all over the Portland Metro area.

Reuse: glass flower vases

There's nothing I like more than a fresh floral arrangement. I have no skill in arranging flowers myself, so the lovely ones always come from professional florists. Occasionally, Cheap-o will pop for some flowers, for a big wedding anniversary (especially if I drop a lot of hints). And my dad is quite thoughtful about getting flower arrangements for holidays and special celebrations. The challenge is with the glass flower vases.

If you are like me, you've probably collected flower vases in all shapes, sizes and colors. You reuse them as best you can. But perhaps, you too, are finding that your cabinets are near to bursting with glass flower vases. Due to different types of glass, these vases cannot be recycled curbside in the "glass on the side" bucket. And if you've ever been to a thrift store or estate sale, you'll usually find an abundance of glass flower vases there as well. So this challenge is clearly widespread. Once, I suggested to our local florist that she take back and reuse some of the vases she'd delivered to our house, and she looked at me as if I were suggesting she take back and reuse the dead flowers.

That was a while ago. Perhaps times have changed. Does anyone use a local florist who will take back and reuse glass flower vases? Or, do you know of a local organization that wants glass flower vases?

Reducing waste in the fast-food nation

We try not to eat out often. Part of my concern is the cost. Part of my concern is the question of where the food comes from, and how nutritious it really is. Part of it is that my kids seem to then want to eat out all the time. But mostly it's the waste that is generated in the restaurant business.

Having taken the Master Recycler course, I have a better understanding of how challenging it is for a Portland Metro restaurant to implement composting and recycling plans: Unlike with residential waste/recycling haulers who use the hauler that is assigned to their geographical area, businesses bid for haulers from all over the area, and typically hire one that gives them the best price. So myriad haulers criss-cross all over town, serving only the businesses they have contracted with, and sometimes a hauler's offerings for recycling/composting are slim to none.

So it's beyond exciting to hear about Burgerville's plan to expand a composting and recycling pilot to all 39 of their restaurants.  Burgerville is an established leader in local food purchasing, recycling cooking oil for biodiesel production, and a big customer of renewable power. This move really takes them one step further down the road to sustainability and makes them even more of a role model. They won my heart with their killer turkey burger, and they keep me coming back with each additional green-thinking business decision they implement. It's inspiring to see that restaurants can find a way to be green despite the challenges they face.

Portland Fashion gets even greener

Did you know that green is the new black, at least in the fashion world? Portland Fashion Week kicks off this Friday, and as reported in today's Oregonian green is in this year. The article details a desire from consumers to know where and how clothing is made:

"The growing awareness of the high costs of cheap clothes -- cost to the environment and cost to the people working in sweatshops -- has created conscientious consumers. It's a political movement without organized rallies or protests: They simply put their money where their hearts are."

And it's not just about sourcing sustainable fibers, such as hemp, bamboo and organic cotton. Get this: the Art Institute of Portland recently added classes in sustainable design in response to students' requests. That rocks!

The fashion industry can seem inherently un-green, when you consider they're changing what's hot and trendy every year in an attempt to get us to buy new and more, more, more. So this growing awareness and attentiveness to conscientious consumerism and sustainability is exciting.

Now let's get down to the nitty gritty. I've seen cool things for grown-ups made from sustainable fibers with sustainable practices. And lots of cute soft baby things. But who's making and selling the things all kids go through like crazy: socks, undies and tights? That's what our family needs...

Cleaning out the cabinets, part II

I've been doing some major cabinet purging lately and amassed piles of stuff from the kitchen, bathrooms and laundry room that I really wanted to avoid tossing out, but knew my family would never use them again. Beth's recent email to us also got me motivated to find reuse organizations for as much as I could. Happily, I managed to find homes for almost everything:

  • Kitchen: An old mixer and Dustbuster, both working great; sturdy Avent sippy cups; plastic toddler forks and spoons; a wok and some miscellaneous kitchen utensils went to the Oregon Community Warehouse. I love this organization because they work directly with Portlanders in desperate need of household items.
  • Laundry Room: Lots and lots of mainstream cleaning products, most partially-used, like Drano, Comet Cleanser, Soft Scrub and industrial-strength floor cleaner (why did we buy this?) all went to the Good Neighbor Center in Tigard. I'd never heard of this organization before, but they are a shelter that serves many families with young children. In addition to the cleaning products, the Good Neighbor Center also gladly accepted items from my...
  • Bathroom: Half-used shampoos, skin lotions, bug spray, shaving cream -- products we'd tried or bought on impulse and didn't like. Also, baby lotions and diaper cream that we no longer need. The only products I was unable to get rid of were expired prescription medications. I took them to the Burlingame Fred Meyer pharmacy and was told that they are unable to dispose of them. The technician suggested I burn them in the fireplace! Obviously, not an option. Anyone have luck getting rid of medications? (Renee, I swear you took yours to Fred's.)

All of the lotions and potions made me realize that I really need to stop and think before buying anything like this -- this is where the REDUCE part of the Reduce-Reuse-Recycle equation comes in. I'm glad that someone can use it, but what a waste of money (and resources)!

Umpqua Bank offers cash back for buying local

We bank with Umpqua Bank (my husband's employer) and I was amazed and delighted to receive notice of a new program they've started that gives customers cash back for supporting local businesses. Anytime you use your debit card at a participating merchant, you receive up to 20% back on your first visit and between 1-14% back on subsequent visits. You get cash, you support a local business, plus the Boys and Girls Club receives a donation with every eligible purchase. The greening of computers and electronics

I kid you not. On the first day of the Master Recycler course I asked what eWaste is. I honestly did not know. If you're in that same boat, it's short for electronic waste, or unwanted, obsolete, or unusable electronic products such as computers. eWaste is a particularly tricky waste stream because electronics are made up of a diverse mix of materials (some precious and some toxic) put together in complex ways.

So have you got eWaste sitting in a closet at home somewhere? Understandable. It's hard to part with computers or electronics that you paid big bills for and were considered "top of the line" not so long ago. It's best if you can find someone to reuse it. We've found new homes for some of our old computers via Craigslist. Free Geek may be your solution if you don't want to sell it. They rebuild and adopt out good computers, and they break down truly obsolete machines into recyclable parts using volunteer labor. Volunteers can work toward earning their own computer once they've put in a certain amount of hours.

Now Oregon is one of several states on the cusp of eWaste legislation. The measure would require electronics manufacturers to open recycling sites or to help pay for collective sites where electronic devices could be returned free of charge. Amen to that!

There's also a few other organizations we should be aware of as electronics consumers. There's a voluntary program for electronics manufacturers called EPEAT that helps consumers pick products based on their environmental attributes. There's also MyGreenElectronics to help us make greener choices when we are buying electronics. We are a pretty hard-core Mac family, so I was pleased to see Steve Jobs' letter about a "greener apple".

I could go on and on, but let me finish by tying in a discussion of the power of consumer opinions in our final Master Recycler class this week (so, yes, indeed, Heather and I are newly ordained "Master Recylers" now). Use the 800 numbers. Write letters. Tell manufacturers what you think about excessive packaging and environmental impact. The green choir is growing and getting louder. Hallelujah! Vote with your dollar! OK, someone make me step away from the keyboard now!

Smell that? Me neither.

Volatile Organic Compounds. They are evil. Up until a few years ago, no one really talked about VOCs, but they've snuck into our vernacular thanks to the recent introduction of products that no longer contain them. Basically, VOCs, "are emitted as gases from certain solids or liquids. VOCs include a variety of chemicals, some of which may have short- and long-term adverse health effects. Concentrations of many VOCs are consistently higher indoors (up to ten times higher) than outdoors," according to the flowery prose on the EPA Web site.

So when it came time to paint my office, I turned to Miller Paint's Acro, a zero-VOC paint that contains anti-microbial protection. There are virtually no fumes and the paint goes on beautifully. In fact, I'm sitting in my office now with wet paint on the walls, and lo, no odor. You can't get zero-VOC paint in really dark colors (color adds VOCs) but you can get low-VOC paint. Other paint stores, like Rodda, Benjamin Moore and Environmental Building Supplies, carry low or zero-VOC paints, but I think Miller is the least expensive at $26 per gallon (plus there is a buy one get one free coupon in the Chinook Book). We'll breath easier tonight!

And the co-payment goes to... the Fanno Creek Clinic

I'm feeling like a bit of a traitor. Let me start by saying I love my OB/GYN like a sister. She's the only doctor I've seen (not counting my dentist) with any regularity over the past 6 years. However, I know with certainty that I'm done with birthin' babies, and therefore no longer in need of an OB. So the thought of waiting 4-5 months to get a regular checkup with my OB/GYN at Women's Healthcare Associates, and then on the day of the appointment, waiting in the lobby for hours while she tries to catch up from deliveries and her overbooked schedule - I just can't do it.

So today I had a physical at the Fanno Creek Clinic in SW Portland. I was expecting the blood pressure check, cholesterol screening - you know, the works. And I was not disappointed. They even zapped a sun-damaged spot off my cheek - without my having to come back in for another appointment and another co-payment.

If you don't know about this clinic, they are one of the most socially and environmentally responsible small businesses I've ever heard of. They pay their employees incentives for making environmentally friendly purchases. And they installed solar panels on their roof in 2005, which earned them a Portland BEST Energy Award for Small Businesses. They are also a veritable United Nations - with more than 10 different languages spoken at the clinic. It's a funky looking little place, but the care is top-notch. My husband, dad and I go there for primary care, see three different providers and rave about them all.

So if you're shopping for a doctor, this is one place to look into. They are accepting new patients.

And now that I'm approaching 40 and no longer exempted due to pregnancy or breastfeeding, I need to go get my first mammogram. Oh joy. I'm sure I'll be telling you all about that one of these days.

gDiapers - are you using them?

I was so excited when I heard about the first biodegradable, flushable diapers that I almost wet myself. (I wonder if they come in adult sizes?) GDiapers mean no more diaper pail, no more smell, no more landfill guilt. You can buy them at stores like New Seasons, Wild Oats, Market of Choice and probably Whole Foods. It's a 2-part system: there is a cloth panty with a plastic liner and the flushable insert, which is about the size of a maxi-pad. The insert fits into the panty, and when it's soiled you simply take it out and flush it. Unless you have a toilet like mine.

The first time I tried it, I ended up sticking my hand into the flush hole to retrieve the stuck insert. Lovely. The second time I tried it, I let the insert soak in the toilet for about two hours hoping it would break down. It did, and it flushed. Neither of these scenarios where particularly appealing to me, so I gave up on it. But then my neighbor, Shelly, told me that you can pull apart the insert and it will flush easily. Excellent. So I'm going to give it another try. I'm wondering how often you have to change the insert...after every little pee? Because gDiapers aren't cheap. It costs about $15 for 32 medium flushable inserts. I'd love to hear whether anyone else has gone the gDiaper route.
