Arlington: the end of the line for most Portland Metro trash

Have you ever wondered where your trash goes once your local hauler takes it "away"? I did. It was one of the first things we talked about in the Master Recycler course: solid waste overview.

We were greeted with that often-cited stat: Americans who comprise 5% of the world's population consume 24% of the world's energy and generate 30% of the world's waste.

Portlanders, for all our greenieness, are a little better than the average American. In the Metro area, we generate 8.3 pounds of waste per person per day. Haulers take the trash to a transfer station. There, recyclables are recovered, and the trashiest trash is compacted to fill large transport trucks. Our trash fills 80 semi trucks a day that travel to Arlington, OR (which is about 135 miles away). That's a lot of trucks and a lot of mileage.

Thanks to fellow Master Recycler Gretchen, here's a link to an article from the Seattle-Post Intelligencer about how the landfill got placed in Arlington and what the locals think about it. Despite those common "not in my backyard" arguments cropping up when something like a landfill (or a windfarm) is proposed in a new area, amazingly Arlington seems to be happy with the income and benefits this large landfill (serving Portland Metro and Seattle) has provided.

OSU Food Preservation hotline available through October 12

Oregon State University Extension offers a hotline from 9am to 4pm Monday through Thursday through October 12 to answer questions you may have regarding food preservation and safety. The number is 800-354-7319.

The golden rule of preservation: Use up-to-date recipes from reliable sources.

Our bountiful CSA box has delivered us oodles of fresh produce this summer. I often find myself thinking about preserving some of it via canning, freezing or drying. If you're like me and know little to nil about said processes, give the hotline a call if you are in doubt when trying some new-to-you food preservation techniques.

Help pass Oregon's Farm to School bill

We need your help today to get Oregon's Farm to School bills passed. Key decisions will be made this week. Now is the time to call your legislators in Salem.

Oregon is one of a handful of states that doesn't contribute additional funds to support implementation of the National School Lunch and Breakfast program. Now it's catch up time. Let's improve the quality of food we're serving our kids, while simultaneously supporting Oregon farmers. While we're at it, let's fund school gardens and ensure that children across the state learn that carrots grow in the ground.   

In April, the House Education Committee unanimously sent all three Farm to School bills on to the Joint Ways and Means Committee.
Why wouldn't they? With no opposition, these are "apple pie" bills. But that doesn't mean it's time to break out the ice cream. These bills, great as they are, won't get passed unless we keep the pressure on to find the money to pay for them!

Please contact members of the Joint Ways and Means Committee and show your support.
The final frontier for these bills is a funding decision that can only be made by the Joint Ways and Means Committee. The Oregon State Legislature will likely wrap up business at the end of June. Let's make sure our elected officials fund the Farm to School bills before breaking for the summer.

Solar Now! Workshop on June 6

Portland gets such a bad rap on the rain thing. So much so that many Portlanders don't think solar is truly an option. Well, that's definitely not the case. But, there's much to consider when going solar:

  • Does your roof have southern exposure?
  • How much shading is on your roof?
  • Does your roof have 15 years of life remaining?

If you answered yes, to all of the above, solar is probably an option for you. Want to know more? Sign up for a "Solar Now! Basics of going solar" workshop on Wednesday, June 6, 6:30-8pm at the Ecotrust Building.

Mythbusting the lights on/lights off controversy: just turn the lights off!

Best way to reduce waste? Buy less stuff.

I can't tell you how much I love the Master Recycler program. We had two amazing speakers on Tuesday night, and one of them, David Allaway from the Department of Environmental Quality, just rocked our world. I wish I could repeat everything he said (and he spoke at about 80 mph, so it's impossible) but here are some things that stuck:

Upstream versus Downstream Waste: Upstream waste is all the harvesting of raw materials, manufacturing processes and transportation of stuff that happens before it gets to you. Downstream waste is what happens to the stuff once you get rid of it. The upstream impacts are significantly worse for the environment than downstream. So, if you buy less stuff, less stuff will be manufactured.

It is much better for the environment to reuse a product and extend its lifetime than recycle it. Example: reusing a corrugated cardboard box saves 3-4 times more energy than recycling it. Reusing a PC uses 5-20 times less energy than recycling it.

Landfills are not evil (at least the new ones aren't). We will never run out of landfill space. Keeping stuff out of the landfill is NOT an important reason for recycling. Recycling helps the environment because it takes significantly less energy and emits fewer greenhouse gases to manufacture a product from existing (recycled) materials than from virgin (new) materials.

The markets for recyclables are not too far away to justify long-haul transportation. I've worried about shipping plastics to Asian markets and whether that uses more energy than you save by recycling in the first place. Allaway says not: you'd have to send the plastics on a freighter 184,000 miles before you hit the "break-even" point. For aluminum it's 538,000 miles. (Aluminum is the most energy-intensive recyclable; yet it takes about 95% less energy to produce a product from recycled aluminum than from new.)

The take-away? He got me to really think about purchases and realize that investing in really good quality stuff that will last makes a greater impact than the impulsive buys I might make at Target. (Basically I will just have to stop going there. The temptation is too great.) It got me to thinking about furniture, and that our next purchase will be "vintage". It got me thinking that the next time I need a pair of jeans, I can head over to Katelyn's Closet consignment store rather than the Gap. And do my kids really need birthday party presents from their friends when they get plenty from their family? Do you have any good tips for reducing or reusing stuff that you care to share?
